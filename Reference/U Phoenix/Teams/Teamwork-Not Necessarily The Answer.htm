Teamwork--not necessarily the answer
Allan B Drexler, Russ Forrester. HRMagazine. Alexandria: Jan 1998. Vol. 43, Iss. 1; pg. 55, 4 pgs
Abstract (Summary)

Many managers institute a team-based production method without considering whether the work calls for a team effort. The nature of the work should dictate whether teamwork or individual effort is needed to boost productivity. Teamwork takes more time than individual work. Teams have to develop some common sense of purpose and shared goals. There are 3 tests to apply to see if a team fits a situation: 1. Can the work be done better by more than one person? 2. Does the work create a common purpose or set of goals for the people in a group? 3. Are the members of the group interdependent?

ld McDonald was in high dudgeon. "Output is down 15 percent. I've asked you to work together as a team, yet you just sit in your own cubicles and do your own thing.

"Teamwork is where it's at. Starting today, I'm upping your production standards 25 percent. It's your team, so work it out."

With that, the farmer stomped out of the hen house. The hens sat bewildered. There had been fewer eggs than last month, but how would teamwork help? Nonetheless, fearing they would lose their cushy jobs as egg layers they set out to work more as a team.

They met twice a week to set goals and evaluate results. They agreed that no one would lay an egg without informing the others. One group drafted a team mission, vision and values statement. Two stopped producing eggs altogether and spent their time writing a policy on how to deal with low producers. Four went off to learn about consensus decision-making. A subgroup was in charge of designing a teambased incentive system. Despite their best efforts, production continued to fall.

It is clearly ridiculous to ask egg layers to work as a team, because the work they do can only be done alone. Although they all do the same kind of work, none of them could have any effect on the work the others were doing. The absurdity of the proposition "You're now gonna work as a team!" isn't as clear when the required team members are bank tellers, first-line sales people, loan specialists or teachers. Yet there are a lot of McDonalds out there telling people whose work doesn't require or benefit from the involvement of others that they will function better as part of a team.

DISILLUSIONMENT WITH TEAMS

Have you noticed a backlash against the increasing use of teams as an organizational unit? To some extent that reaction stems from disappointment that teams aren't a panacea. But it may, in part, be a response to the fact that potentially useful teams aren't managed or supported well. Often, however, it's a reaction against the process of forcing people to form and act like a team when a team isn't needed at all.

Teamwork takes more time than individual work. Up front, teams have to develop some common sense of purpose and shared goals. Team members then have to plan and schedule their work together. Throughout, they have to communicate to keep their activities in synch. And at least some decisions may have to be made as a team. Often, training is required to build a distinct set of skills, including communication, conflict resolution, group decisionmaking and the ability to run a meeting. The time required for all of this can only take away from task performance. Unnecessary teamwork wastes resources. If there is no payoff for teamwork, it is simply an expense.

DOES THE WORK CALL FOR A TEAM?

Better than starting with the idea of forming a team to get the work done is starting with the work and seeing if it leads to the idea of a team. There are three tests to apply to see if a team fits the situation:

1. Can the work be done better by more than one person? Tying shoe laces, obviously no. A kiss, certainly yes. In between, a lot of gray areas. A good indicator is the complexity of the work and the demand for different perspectives, technologies, knowledge bases, skill sets, interests or client groups. Rapidly changing conditions and a diversified product set can also dictate the need for numerous people on a task.

2. Does the work create a common purpose or set of goals for the people in a group? A common purpose means more than simply doing the same work. The purpose and goals must be more than the aggregate of individual goals. Although each member of a team is responsible for a piece of the work, the team is collectively responsible for the whole process. A chef, cook, waiter and runner who have the goal of providing a pleasant dining experience for a customer would be an example of a true team.

A key issue is whether a team member individually produces something of final value to the customer or whether only the joint product "works." In the example above, a diner will see little value in a wellcooked meal left cooling in the kitchen or in a promptly served meal that tastes terrible. By comparison, I may be fully satisfied with the work of the person who makes my airline reservation, whether or not other members of the reservation "team" botch the travel arrangements of other customers. The team must collectively produce something of value to a customer.

3. Are the members of the group interdependent? The members of a true team have to work hand in glove. Their activities are interlocking, like the pieces of a jigsaw puzzle. The success of the whole depends on the success of each one, and the success of each one depends on the success of others individually. Presenting a play, designing a better hair dryer, climbing Mount Everest, and performing open heart surgery are all types of work that demand interdependence.

Teams that are established because the work calls for a team have a much higher probability of being successful and paying off for the organization.

How MUTUALLY DEPENDENT ARE GROUP MEMBERS?

The dictionary defines interdependence as "mutual dependence," which implies a two-way exchange requiring mutual support. Interdependence may have different looks on different teams, depending on the task. Here are four common variations.

Interdependence concerning results. Group members may be evaluated or compensated based on the overall production of the group. For example, all salespeople in a department may get a bonus based on the overall level of sales. In the performance of the task, however, they have no interdependence at all. At most, they may support one another through coaching, cheering or peer pressure, but they act alone. There is no task interdependence at all. Partial task interdependence. Team members work independently for a significant part of the time, but also have to work hand-in-hand sometime. Examples range from a team of lawyers who share office space and administrative support but otherwise work alone to the Supreme Court justices, who individually develop opinions and draft documents, but have to issue collective decisions.

Partial interdependence may vary in other ways. There may be a great deal of interdependence among some team members and less or none among others. Interdependence may also vary over time within the life of a team-at some moments frequent interactions are necessary and at other times mostly individual work is needed. Partial interdependence may be the most difficult type of team interaction.

Full interdependence. On some teams, the demand for collaboration and mutual support is practically constant. The crew of a space station or the negotiating team at the table in an international crisis are examples. The work of these teams may be relatively brief and intense. Or it may be continuing. The production team in a fruit dI-ink manufacturing plant is an example of the latter. The team members are multi-skilled and have to rely on one another fully to keep hitting production goals.

Interchangeability. On some teams, members not only depend fully on one another, they actually stand in or take each other's place. That may happen on self-directed work teams with multi-skilled members or in situations that are fluid and call for quick response to changing demands. Members of a legal team may spontaneously decide who is to lead a cross-examination. A team of rodeo clowns may have to move themselves into the best positions to protect a rider. Often, on teams like those, the give-and-take is spontaneous and intuitive.

While task interdependence determines primarily how a group of people can best work together, interdependence isn't necessarily constant. For example, when members of the top management team of a chemical company we consulted altered their vision of the company and its future by deciding to expand the company's market on the Pacific Rim, their work as a team changed significantly. The members of that team had been able to function independently most of the time in the past, but they now had a shared stake in the future.

A team's task can also change through the assignment of different work, a move to another line of business or a reengineering of processes. Work teams need to monitor their work and periodically revisit the issue of interdependence to maintain a good match between what the task demands and the way the team works.

CLARITY ABOUT INTERDEPENDENCE NECESSARY

The level of task interdependence within a group can range from the negligible interaction of 10 people washing clothes at the same time in a Laundromat to the total interdependence of a team of high-wire acrobats supporting one another in a pyramid-a situation in which a misstep by anyone would cause them all to fall. The crux of the matter is not how to label the group, as a team or a nonteam, but to have a clear idea about what the group's interdependencies are, and then to manage them well. The match between the game the team is playing and the way the team works is a fundamental factor in team success.
