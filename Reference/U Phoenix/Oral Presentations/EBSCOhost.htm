EVALUATING AND ASSESSING STUDENT ORAL PRESENTATIONS: A LIMITED BUT EFFECTIVE ROLE FOR EMPLOYERS IN THE GEOGRAPHY CURRICULUM

ABSTRACT Employers were involved in assessing students' presentations, giving feedback and in the development of staff skills for providing feedback. The curriculum context to the oral presentations is described and the problems and benefits of employer involvement are considered. It is argued that it is possible to develop an effective small-scale approach to employer involvement as an alternative to major schemes.

KEYWORDS Oral presentations, feedback, assessment, employer involvement

Employer Involvement--more problems than benefits?

This paper argues that it is possible to develop a limited but very effective small-scale approach to employer involvement, which occupies the middle ground between large-scale initiatives and non-involvement. Our approach has been to identify a particular area of our geography curriculum, skills teaching and evaluation, where one employer could make a significant impact in the short term in a manner that would neither distort our academic aims for the curriculum nor make major demands on the resources of academic staff or employers.

Many individuals working in higher education may be very wary of involving employers in curriculum development. Clearly, as other papers in this collection show, there are immense potential benefits to students in terms of skill and career prospect enhancement. However, these have to be offset against significant potential management and academic problems. A large-scale initiative such as a sandwich course will undoubtedly put pressure on resources, both in the development and operational phases, as time and effort are needed to ensure employer involvement is maintained. Academically, problems may occur in terms of balancing the impact of employers' requirements on the curriculum and the more important need, for these authors at least, to adjust course content to reflect developments within the discipline. Employers' needs and disciplinary advances are not of course incompatible, but equally there may well be some significant differences in terms of how they relate to the curriculum. Our approach to employer involvement has allowed us to develop an initiative in which employers have played an effective role but in which our other strategic academic aims have not been compromised.

Employer involvement in geography at Birkbeck College is focused on assessing and providing feedback on student oral presentations. As part of a second-year geography project students present their findings in a 15-minute oral presentation. The training and guidance students require prior to the oral presentation is provided by the departmental staff, with no employer involvement. Employers only play a role at the final stage by assisting with evaluation and the crucial process of providing useful feedback. The input of employers produces tangible benefits for students and staff, but it also raises a number of important dilemmas for teachers.

The Context: the geography curriculum, skills teaching and the Enterprise Initiative at Birkbeck College

Birkbeck College is a long-established London University institution that specialists in the teaching of mature, part-time undergraduate and postgraduate students in the evening; 87% of the college's students are mature part-timers, most of whom pay their own fees. Typically, undergraduate students complete a degree in four years of evening study. Despite this we would argue that the initiative outlined below is transferable to other institutions with more traditional student profiles.

In the geography department curriculum development is a continual process but, in 1987-88, a major review was instigated as a response to changes in the discipline, and in order to improve learning amongst mature, part-time undergraduate students. Employer involvement had not been an initial aim of the curriculum review but was stimulated by the involvement of the geography department two years later in the Enterprise Initiative. At the same time the curriculum review meant that the department was in the process of establishing an assessed summer project undertaken between the first and second year of undergraduate studies, which in part aimed to develop certain student skills that were classed as 'enterprise' skills. The funding and employer contacts from the Enterprise Initiative allowed the geography department to extend the summer project to include a key role for employers that had not initially been planned, whilst also achieving one of the main aims of the Enterprise Initiative, which is the embedding of 'enterprise' skills teaching in assessed courses. The role employers played in the summer project was quite limited but very clearly focused on the evaluation and feedback process.

The compulsory summer project started in 1989-90 and was designed to relate to first-year human geography lecture courses. The academic objectives of the summer project are to enable students to examine the processes that determine economic and social change in a particular locality and to study local political geography in the form of interest group and local alliance construction. The skill-based objective is to allow students to develop the following skills:

  • information collection;
  • advocacy;
  • working in pairs;
  • role playing;
  • oral presentation;
  • use of visual aids;
  • responding to questions;
  • receiving and acting upon feedback.

Some 20-30 students per year work in pairs and are given a detailed pack of information on a major redevelopment site in London which they have chosen from a list of 20 (students may also select a site of their own choosing). At the end of the summer term, training is given in preparing and delivering oral presentations. The summer is spent obtaining further information on the sites and collecting material for use as visual aids. At the start of the second year students give an assessed, 15-minute oral presentation in which they have to argue a case for or against the development of the site with the other student in the pair arguing the opposite case. Assessed oral presentations are becoming more commonplace in geography curricula (Jenkins & Pepper, 1988; Sharp, 1989; Daniel, 1991; Healey, 1991; Hay, 1994) and there are a number of different methods for involving students in the marking process. In the case of the summer project the additional involvement of employers in assessing student presentations provided some useful insights into the role employers can play, not just in curriculum development but also in improving the feedback and evaluation process.

Employer Involvement in Assessment and Feedback for Oral Presentations

There has been considerable discussion of the value of oral presentations within the geography curriculum and approaches for teaching oral skills (Jenkins & Pepper, 1988; Keene, 1988; Sharp, 1989; Walker, 1993; Hay, 1994). Particular skills and techniques are needed to teach and assess oral presentation skills and the approach described below, except for the feedback process, has many similarities to the methods outlined in earlier papers in this journal (Sharp, 1989; Daniel, 1991; Healey, 1991; Hay, 1994). Many of the previous discussions of oral presentations in geography and other disciplines deal with feedback mainly as part of the assessment process; feedback commonly involves discussion with markers and fellow students after a presentation, written comments and a mark (Coiling, 1988; Sharp, 1989; Daniel, 1991; Healey, 1991; Hay, 1994). Indeed, students' concern over the assessment used for oral presentations means that much effort is put into developing negotiated assessment methods that include a key role for students themselves (Jenkins & Pepper, 1988; Daniel, 1991; Healey, 1991; Hay, 1994). The approach we adopted to oral presentations has also involved negotiating the criteria for assessment with students, but this negotiation process indicated a strong desire from amongst mature students for a high level of interactive feedback rather than just assessment. Our students were not only interested in their general performance (i.e. a mark) but, for a substantial majority, specific advice on technique was as important. This feedback would allow them to make relatively simple changes to their oral presentation style and would lead to improvements in the future.

After three years of employer involvement an approach developed that makes clear to students that feedback and assessment are two separate but interrelated parts of the approach used for evaluating oral skills. Students are then able to separate out feedback and guidance on technique improvements from a broader overall assessment of their presentation based on agreed criteria. There is some justification for this separation of assessment and feedback in the literature on public speaking, which indicates that many of the difficulties speakers encounter and the mistakes they make are easily corrected by basic guidance of a technical nature from experienced oral presenters (Leeds, 1988).

The problem facing academic staff in 1989 was that the staff development undertaken for teaching oral skills had dealt with feedback in a limited manner as part of assessment and staff were unsure of their skills for providing good quality student-centred feedback. Simultaneously, the Birkbeck College Enterprise Initiative Unit had established links with employers and were looking for projects where employers could play a useful role. A contact was provided with the education officer of a major retail company who arranged meetings between lecturers and the company staff who were responsible for training shop-floor staff in oral presentations. These individuals had considerable experience and training in developing assessment methods for oral presentations and providing supportive feedback. Furthermore, their experience of training staff meant they were used to dealing with groups of very varied maturity and ability. Staff at Birkbeck felt that direct employer involvement in the assessment and feedback process for student presentations had considerable potential benefits and, after discussion with the education officer at the retail company, five objectives were identified for the employer involvement:

  • Employees from the retail company would analyse the assessment process for oral presentations and provide advice to staff.
  • Employees from the retail company would provide students with high-quality feedback.
  • Employees from the retail company would employ a variety of methods for providing feedback.
  • Staff at Birkbeck would aim to improve their skills in providing feedback by observing different methods being used.
  • Staff at Birkbeck would aim to improve their skills in providing feedback by experimenting with the use of different methods and receiving advice and comments from the employees of the retail company.

These five objectives identified a very precise role for employer involvement in the summer project that it was hoped would be beneficial to staff and students. The retail company agreed that three employees would attend each student presentation for a period of three years, to provide feedback to students, and to advise Birkbeck staff on the assessment process and feedback methods. The education officer for the retail company also indicated that she was pleased for her employees to have a clear pro-active role that was designed to assist not only students but academic staff as well. She felt that their involvement with curriculum development in a number of universities had become rather predictable usually involving either very generalised advisory roles or major involvement in sandwich courses and student placements.

As part of the summer project each student has to attend three class sessions and it is only in the last of these three that employers were directly involved. At the first session the project aims and content are outlined and the assessment process is discussed. The second session provides detailed written and spoken guidance on preparing an oral presentation, and at the third session students present their 15-minute talk and each student receives 20-30 minutes of feedback. One of the aims of the first session is to establish a negotiated assessment schedule and whilst the employees from the retail company did not attend these sessions they did provide advice on improving this process. A number of techniques for devising negotiated assessment criteria were experimented with in the first three years of the project, including large seminars and students working in small groups. After discussions with the employees from the retail company and an analysis of student evaluation questionnaires an approach has been developed that is now used regularly. In the first session, students are shown a video of a past presentation, asked to work in small groups to identify evaluation criteria, and then discussion amongst the whole class is used to obtain a consensus on assessment criteria. The employees from the retail company provided very useful advice on the techniques for structuring this session whilst allowing students considerable control of the discussion process. Consequently, the assessment process varies slightly from year to year but has always included criteria to evaluate content, delivery, clarity, use of visual aids and response to questions. A student's performance on each of these criteria is outlined on the written report they receive shortly after their presentation.

In discussing the assessment process in the first session the students are also made aware of the form that feedback will take in the third session after they complete their presentation. The feedback process was also developed over the three-year period that the employers were involved and different methods were tested by staff at Birkbeck. The three employees from the retail company attended all of the student presentations over the three-year period and used a variety of feedback techniques in order to provide good feedback to students, but also to illustrate the range of possible methods to staff. In the first two years the retail employees would each adopt a different approach to each student: one would concentrate on using a positive, individual, student-centred approach that always started by allowing the individual to outline their reactions to the presentation they had just completed; the second would utilise a critical assessor-centred approach where students are given direct advice, some of which may be quite critical; and the third would use a peer-group-based approach which encouraged other students to provide feedback.

After the presentation, students completed an evaluation questionnaire and a self-assessment form. The former provided valuable reactions to the different feedback methods which suggested that over 80% of students strongly favoured the individual student-centred feedback and were least interested in peer-group evaluation. In the third year of the summer projects operation Birkbeck staff provided the majority of the feedback after each presentation and the employees from the retail firm provided advice on how staff might improve their technique. The summer project is now in its fifth year of operation and the approach currently used for feedback endeavours to ensure that 70% of time in the feedback session is based on an individual student-centred approach whilst the rest is used for assessor-led or peer-group feedback.

The self-assessment forms are used by students to identify the ways they might improve future oral presentations based on the feedback they receive. The structure of the self-assessment forms is quite simple and they were devised with advice from the retail employees. Following their advice, these forms are kept by Birkbeck staff and then presented to students before any human geography seminar they undertake in future years, to encourage them to implement improvements.

Overall, the input of employers to the summer project led to the following changes and improvements:

  • useful changes to the negotiation process used for agreeing assessment criteria with students;
  • feedback and evaluation is much more individual, student-centred and supportive;
  • structures exist to encourage limited but useful student feedback to each other;
  • student self-assessment mechanisms have been changed;
  • student self-assessment is now a long-term process that is returned to in future years.

The presence of the staff from the retail company was always well received since over two-thirds of students felt this added authority to the process. This positive reaction to employers was initially surprising for the lecturing staff who were concerned that they might be viewed as intimidating by students. Furthermore, a number of students clearly seemed to value the opinions of employers above those of the academic staff!

Employer Involvement: benefits and problems

The employer involvement in the summer project was quite a small-scale initiative. Only one major retail firm was involved which also reduced the time required to develop the employer link. Twenty to thirty students were involved each year and the summer project is part of a course unit that amounts to about 8% of a student's total degree mark. Nevertheless, by setting limited aims for employer involvement it was at least possible to feel they had largely been achieved. In addition, the role identified for employers would be transferable to other institutions. It may be that the aim of Birkbeck staff to pay special attention to assessment and feedback reflects the particular concerns of mature students, but quality evaluation and feedback will be valuable in any environment where oral presentation skills are taught, and the experience at Birkbeck indicates that employers have a contribution to make to this process. The involvement of employers produced the following general benefits to staff and students:

  • more sensitive and student-centred assessment and feedback;
  • improved student awareness of their skills and limitations in oral presentation and the use of visual aids;
  • improved staff skills for assessing and providing feedback on oral presentations;
  • a greater sense of student ownership over the summer project;
  • improvements in student seminars in years three and four.

The last of these benefits has been particularly valuable as students have to give seminar papers in other course units and they have all indicated that their oral presentation training has provided them with valuable skills for use in seminars. Fellow students also benefit since seminar papers have become better presented and easier to follow.

The benefits to the retail employer were less easy to identify. Generally, it was part of their public commitment to the Enterprise Initiative, and the staff who attended the oral presentations claimed it was useful for them to be assessing people who were not company staff. The education officer of the retail company indicated that the limited benefit to the employer was one reason why their willingness to commit staff did not continue after the three-year period initially agreed. In the case of this initiative this was not a problem since it was designed to be sustainable once the employer was no longer involved. More generally, it seems reasonable to surmise that where employer benefits are small or hard to identify they are more likely to be persuaded to become involved in an initiative where their role is clearly identified and not too demanding in terms of resources. One other problem of fixed-term employer involvement is that, if it is a success, at least one group of students will feel that they missed out on a beneficial process. The students participating in the summer project in the fourth and fifth year of its operation had heard about the benefits of employer involvement from students in the years above them and indicated that they would have liked the same treatment. Other problems and worries relating to employer involvement in this initiative include:

  • the large amount of academic staff time needed to set up and maintain employer links (i.e. the standard moan about the lack of promotion-related credit for this sort of work);
  • the difficulty of identifying a clear benefit to employers that could be justified in terms of their time commitment;
  • staff turnover in the retail company meant that some of the employees involved changed from year to year and time had to be spent briefing the new employees;
  • a concern that students became more interested in feedback on skills rather than the content of their presentation.

At a more general level, the experience of developing links with employers inevitably led academic staff involved to consider broader educational issues concerned with employer involvement. The Enterprise Initiative was encouraging us to develop employer links quickly, leaving little time to consider the educational value of exploring other approaches to improving feedback and assessment that did not involve employers. Just because an initiative involves employers does not necessarily mean it is educationally the best approach. In our case we felt that the employer involvement was justified since the literature we managed to examine in the brief time available provided plenty of guidance on assessment for student oral presentations, but far less on providing feedback. Employer involvement in the geography curriculum may seem initially attractive but there are drawbacks that need to be carefully assessed by any department before jumping on board this particular bandwagon.
