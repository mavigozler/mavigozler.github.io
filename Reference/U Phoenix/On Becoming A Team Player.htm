Abstract
On becoming a team player
James T. Scarnati. Team Performance Management. Bradford: 2001. Vol. 7, Iss. 1/2; pg. 5
Abstract (Summary)

This paper discusses teamwork as a synergetic process in which the efforts of the cooperative group surpass individual efforts. It provides historical and contemporary examples of successful team efforts from business, industry and organizations. The benefits of the team's energy are described as guardianship, status, learning, self-esteem, fellowship and power along with the circumstances in which a team effort is neither needed nor desired. Structure, ineffective communications, lack of resources, lack of trust and corporate culture are listed as factors that may hinder or not support the team effort. Interdependence is defined as a key characteristic of successful teams. Quotes from contemporary leaders as to the value of the team effort in today's workplace are included.

James T. Scarnati: James T. Scarnati is the County Superintendent, Warren County School District, Warren, Pennsylvania, USA. E-mail: jtjsdr@wcsd.k12.pa.us

"Do not worry about holding high position; worry rather about playing your proper role (Confucius)." ""A": "A" is the first word in the dictionary and the first letter of the alphabet. Being first does not make it more significant than other words in the dictionary or more important than other letters of the alphabet. By itself, "A" is rather drab. The significant aspect of "A" is its interaction with other words and other alphabet letters to form meaning. Leaders know that being first is not always significant or relevant. What is important is the manner in which individuals join together to work as a meaningful team (Scarnati and Scarnati, 2000)."

An outdoorsman once said, "We can't move this boat upstream unless everyone paddles together". Teamwork is described as a co-operative process that allows ordinary people to achieve extraordinary results. Teamwork is neither a new concept nor one that is unfamiliar to most people. From elementary school through high school everyone participates in some form of team activity. A functional family environment exists whenever there is a team effort by all members to ensure the happiness and well-being of each member as well as of the entire family. Even after high school countless hours are spent viewing television and admiring the synergetic power of teamwork, such as auto racing, football, soccer, the news team or a group of detectives solving a mystery.

An unco-ordinated group of musicians tuning their instruments prior to a performance makes sounds best described as irritating noise. However, once the initial tuning process is completed and the conductor appears before the orchestra, there is a dramatic change. On cue from the conductor, the woodwinds, the brass, the strings and the percussion begin to work as a team. They produce harmonic sounds, even though each is making a different sound and each is playing a different harmony. As individuals tuning their instruments they make "noise"; as a co-ordinated and focused orchestra they produce beautiful music that is pleasing to the ear.

You are literally surrounded by the concept of teamwork. Take a lesson from Mother Nature who provides the ultimate team model in our insect friends, the ants and the bees. Like ants and bees, teamwork means that everyone works together and we all sink or swim together - the people on the team are interdependent. Which leads to a basic question, "Why, then, does our society have to constantly reinvent the concept of teamwork?"

Before venturing further, a true story. Around the area in Pennsylvania where I grew up there were two brothers who owned a small farm. Although not rich by any person's standards, they saved enough money to buy a workhorse. The brothers, being of disagreeable temperament, often did not work as a team, even though it would have been mutually beneficial. During an ensuing argument over whose turn it was to feed the horse, each accused the other of not taking his fair turn. Therefore, because of their stubborn and unco-operative attitude, no one fed the horse, and the horse died. The moral is to use a little common horse sense and teamwork to feed your dreams, or you'll never be able to put them out to pasture.

Together we can

The team has synergy. By sharing a common goal or vision, the team can accomplish what individuals cannot do alone. Synergy is a process of interaction where 2 + 2 = 10. It is a magnifying effect of each component in which the whole surpasses the sum of the parts. It makes sense and does have practical application.

Would you want to be admitted to a hospital for major surgery where the operating room staff did not operate as a team? Would you want highly skilled individuals operating as unco-ordinated and competitive independent agents? Of course you wouldn't!The public expects every phase of a surgical procedure to be a co-ordinated team effort. That is why they call themselves a "surgical team". Proficiency is achieved as the team works co-operatively and practices procedures on a daily basis. Statistics show that patient survival rates increase and morbidity rates decrease in direct relationship to the number of surgeries performed by the operating room team.

The operating room's counterpart to industry's chief executive officer (CEO) is the chief surgeon. Each team member specializes in providing the chief surgeon with the facts and the data necessary to make decisions. Prior to surgery, collaboration among the team is sought and different views or alternatives are examined. However, once the operation has begun, the chief surgeon is the master, and all supporting efforts focus on the patient's (customer's) needs. The anesthetist co-ordinates closely with the surgeon, while the operating room nurses and technicians ensure that proper procedures are followed. The entire team anticipates the needs of the surgeon and places the welfare of the patient as its focus. The Recovery Room staff's effort is an extension of the surgical team and provides services that support the total effort. Only a well co-ordinated team can fulfill the requirements of such a complex task.

The operating room team model is used, because it clearly demonstrates key factors for team success. Nahavandi (1997) says that, in order to ensure success:

(1) "First, the members of a team have to be selected carefully for the complementary skills and expertise.

(2) Second, the team members need to focus on and be committed to a team goal."

Clearly, a surgical team meets Nahavandi's criteria. The team is composed of carefully selected individuals possessing complementary skills. The inclusion of musicians, truck drivers and other similar individuals would do little to enhance the level of expertise on an operating room team. The key point is that team members are selected for their ability to work together and for the expertise they bring to the group. There are many analogies to be made between a surgical team and industry's management team. Suffice to say, the one thing both groups have in common is the need of the customer.

Benefits of team efforts

Individuals are limited by the narrow scope of their knowledge, their skills, and their experiences. On a winding country road, traffic moves only as fast as the slowest vehicle; teamwork enables a group to collaborate in bringing everyone up to speed and to create new superhighways. Working in concert with others permits us to effectively clear obstacles and lend mutual support. Each team member compensates for the other by filling the gaps and voids we all secretly possess. For example, history portrays Michelangelo as a solitary artist who painted the ceiling of the Sistine Chapel, when in fact it was a collaborative effort between Michelangelo and a team of 13 individuals under his direction. This does not negate Michelangelo's contribution as a visionary artist, it only emphasizes that the greater the stature of the individual, the more they recognize the importance of teamwork. Properly implemented, true teamwork brings out the best in each person and enables the group to develop an action plan far superior to that of any individual. Collaboratively, all of us are more creative than some of us, some of us collaboratively are more creative than one of us, and one of us isn't as creative as all of us collaborating together. "Genius lies within the collaborative efforts of an empowered group" (Scarnati and Scarnati, 2000). The primary benefits derived through teamwork are:

- Guardianship: isolation in the workplace is replaced by a family of people with common interests. The individual no longer stands alone. The team provides reassurance and guardianship to its members.

- Status: groups provide each member with prestige and recognition. Successful groups engender a sense of pride in their accomplishments that is widely recognized.

- Learning: belonging to a team is a learning experience. Individuals experience a wide range of new ideas and skills when interacting with team members.

- Self-esteem: team membership can provide individuals with a sense of worth. Being a team member provides a sense of accomplishment along with fulfilling a need for growth and status.

- Fellowship: people enjoy regular interaction with individuals who have similar interests and goals. Teams provide a social, as well as a professional, opportunity to make friends.

- Power: teams are often empowered to accomplish tasks not available to individuals. Provided with the proper authority, teams exercise power. Often people achieve their personal goals through group efforts.

In his book Organizing Genius (1997), Bennis describes the group effort: "Great groups need tangible projects. They can coalesce only around something to which every member contributes but that exists outside the minds of the members, something that can only be achieved collectively."

The Boeing Company is an excellent example of the proficiency of teamwork. The airplane Boeing hopes will carry the company into the twenty-first century is the Model 777. It was designed as a team effort between the company and over 1,200 customers. This is the first time in aeronautical history that customers have been included as part of a functional design team prior to the production process. Industry realizes that, when the team produces a winner, everyone shares equally in the accomplishment. Boeing's efforts are directed toward maintaining its position as the number one manufacturer of commercial aircraft in the world. The people at Boeing are determined to be winners and stay winners for the foreseeable future.

Teamwork creates commitment because everyone must accept ownership and responsibility for the success or the failure of a project. Prior to 1980, the joke among consumers was that FORD was an acronym for Fix Or Repair Daily. Ford, a company not noted for quality, began its turn-around in 1983 when it embraced the quality teachings of Dr W. Edwards Deming. The company removed the old top-down concept of operation and replaced it with a team concept in which every individual has a vested interest in success. In the design and marketing of the first Ford Taurus/Sable there was wholesale involvement from all stakeholders in the company's future. Ford created "Team Taurus" which included designers, engineers, dealers, suppliers, hourly workers, insurance companies, customers, and management. The group was an unthinkable coalition of people prior to the implementation of the team concept. History and profits record the design of the first Taurus/Sable as an innovative winner. Ford's share of the domestic auto market rose from 14 percent in 1985 to 38 percent in 1991. By 1995, the Taurus was the best-selling full size car in the USA.

The 1994 Ford Mustang was also developed and implemented by a 450-member "Team Mustang". The team accomplished the task in three years instead of the usual four-year cycle. Again, the team effort saved Ford considerable money by speeding the transition from concept to product. Today's technology and computer-aided design are making the interval between concept and production constantly shorter. Working as an integrated unit to solve problems answers everyone's "who, what, when, where?", and, most importantly, "why?" questions. When individuals or groups know the answer to these questions they are more motivated and committed to accomplishing the task. Appropriate decisions are made on the spot instead of being sent through an impersonal chain of command to finally land on the desk of a distant executive who is unfamiliar with the concept or the problem.

The whole picture becomes available. The importance of individuals and each team doing nothing less than their best to complete their part in the process becomes readily apparent. Teamwork decompartmentalizes people and provides the overview necessary to understand and appreciate the importance of the group as an integral part of a complex process.

Factors that do not support team efforts

If teamwork is so great, why don't organizations have more of it? Why do companies continually have to harangue people to co-operate and work as part of a group? The answers are complex; however, there are answers. People won't work as a team unless the structure, conditions, organization, and purpose of the enterprise support a team effort. The following provides a glimpse of six factors that do not support a team effort.

(1) Structure. The first factor that does not support teamwork is structure. Merit pay, performance incentives, management by objectives, normal distribution curves, and employee ranking are all disincentives for teamwork. Each structure creates a competitive environment in which there must be winners and losers. There is no interdependence among individuals. In competitive situations everybody wants to be a winner regardless of who loses. This is in sharp contrast to a win-win situation of a "profit sharing" plan. In such plans, the stakeholders share equally in the risks and the rewards. The structure of profit sharing communicates that, when the company does well, the employees do well, and the only way the company can do well is if everyone works as a team. The focus of most organizational structures, as they are typically employed, is on the performance of individuals and not group effort.

(2) Ineffective communications. A second factor discouraging teamwork is ineffective organizational communications. Unless each member of the group has a clear understanding of the desired outcomes, they drift aimlessly and eventually become discouraged. Without a guiding map, reaching the desired destination becomes a matter of chance. Clearly stated goals or objectives developed by the team or provided by a strategic action plan are essential for a team to function. In many cases, a well written mission statement or statement of beliefs will assist the process of providing direction.

(3) Lack of resources. A third factor, the lack of resources, such as time, money, materials, moral support, or mission, has a negative effect on teamwork. Ross Perot counsels, "If something needs to be done, pull together a small team, and give them a defined task and the resources to get it done" (Chiu, 1992). Picture a team about to play a football game where there are no uniforms, no equipment, no playing field, no footballs, and no coach and the concept of essential resources becomes clear. Without resources and a defined mission, the team can't play the game, no matter how good their intentions. Prior to World War II, such a situation occurred on a massive scale in the USA. The defense budget (a monetary resource) during the 1930s was so meager that soldiers trained by utilizing wooden rifles, trucks painted as tanks, and lengths of stove pipe as artillery (materials resources). With no clear military purpose (goal and mission), it was not an environment that promoted high morale or teamwork. At the start of World War II the poorly trained US Army paid dearly for the nation's foolishness and frugality. How many times have you been asked to be part of a team in which management has not budgeted adequate time or resources during the normal workday? Frequent night meetings are eventually resented and are counterproductive in promoting a team spirit.

(4) Lack of trust. The fourth and perhaps biggest destroyer of team cohesiveness is the lack of honesty and trust among team members. Creating situations in which everyone becomes a winner is completely foreign to some individuals. It takes the co-operation of every team member for a group to function, but unco-operative individuals can cause the team to become dysfunctional. Disruptive conduct is comparable with the behavior of an inept arsonist - once the conflagration is initiated, disruptive individuals are often trapped and consumed by an inferno of their own design. Our entire society is built around competitive individual performance, from grading in school to the superstar status of sports figures. Beyond the hero rhetoric is the fact that superstars cannot exist unless there is a valid team effort on their behalf. Most athletes realize this and place high value on the efforts of their fellow team members.

(5) Corporate culture. The fifth factor that does not support a team effort is corporate culture. For teamwork to be meaningful and effective, there must be a paradigm shift in the ranks of both labor and management. Concomitant with empowerment, teamwork requires responsibility and accountability. People who have been taught to fear the results of failure do not readily accept a team process. Everyone must buy into the team concept, and this won't happen if it is perceived as a forced, top-down decision. To develop a team mentality takes time, support, training, and most of all trust. Trust, the glue that holds teams together, is a precious commodity that is not readily given. Teamwork must be perceived as having a high priority in the corporate hierarchy. Even so, the concept takes time, practice and patience to become ingrained in the organization's culture. Employees must believe that the corporation's values are congruent with its team-building rhetoric.

(6) Inappropriate use of the team approach. A sixth and most obvious consideration is that the team structure is not always desirable or appropriate. Often, time-sensitive decisions must be made unilaterally. Self-managed teams take a great deal of time to grow and develop. For example, place people untrained in group dynamics together and assign them a simple task. If the task is to draft and mail a letter, the letter may never arrive at its destination because the group will argue over where to place the stamp on the envelope. The point is, teams don't just happen, they are built, and the process takes time and focused effort. Therefore, teams best function on long-term issues that are important but not urgent, rather than short-term crisis issues that are important and urgent. In a highly competitive environment, time is a critical factor. When a house is on fire, forming a committee to debate the issue is senseless - the standard procedure is to call the fire department. Timely decisions are often not amenable to a group effort. Modifications or adjustments to standard operating procedures are decisions best made by a single point manager - in the same manner that the chief surgeon makes timely decisions during surgery. Sometimes the leader has to be directive and tell others what to do. Otherwise, the competition will be knocking at the door, while the team gingerly participates in building consensus. Teamwork is particularly effective in the long-range planning process. Planning for the design and development of new products, or in the modification of the production process, is an appropriate domain for team decisions (i.e. Team Taurus, Team Mustang, Team Xerox ... ). Like everything else in life, overkill must be avoided and thoughtful moderation becomes the goal for implementing a team model. Don't create a team process that robs the organization of its ability to react and make rapid and timely decisions.

Interdependence

A roofing shingle by itself can do little good. However, placed together with other shingles it becomes an interdependent unit that repels the severest storm. Teamwork requires all individuals to embrace the concept of interdependence. My fate is in our hands. Although the classic western television series was named "The Lone Ranger", he never worked alone. The Ranger's faithful partner, Tonto, worked with him in a co-ordinated team effort to combat villains. Teamwork and a hearty "Hi-Ho-Silver-away!" was the theme of the masked man's efforts as he fought injustice. On a successful team, everyone, including Tonto, is a hero. Each member is recognized as a valued contributor to the group's success.

To actively participate on a well functioning team is an exhilarating experience. It is essential that administrators understand the power of a dynamic team. Max DePree, chairman of Herman Miller, provides a question that must be answered on an individual basis, "Would you rather work as part of an outstanding group or be part of a group of outstanding individuals?" (DePree, 1992). Teamwork does not diminish or lessen the authority, responsibility, or the status of the leader. Becoming a team member, greatly enhances and expands the effectiveness of the leader. Leaders who work with and through others are essential in today's competitive environment.

John F. Welch, former CEO of General Electric, provided his view of teamwork during an interview in the December 13, 1993 issue of Fortune magazine. Welch states, "When you make a value like teamwork important, you shape behavior. If you can't operate as a team player, no matter how valuable you've been, you really don't belong at GE" (Mcmanus, 1993). The restructuring and realignment of business and industry reflect the reinvented concept of teamwork. Managers must be aware that team building does not happen spontaneously. It requires time, patience, effort, support, practice and trust to be successful. The highly successful 3M Company fosters a corporate attitude that supports innovation and teamwork. The 3M Company's culture is expressed in the following doctrine:

- Listen to anyone with an original idea, no matter how absurd it may sound at first.

- Encourage, don't nit-pick. Let people run with an idea.

- Hire good people and leave them alone.

- If you put fences around people, you get sheep. Give people the room they need (O'Neil, 1997).

Eventually, in a supportive environment, the team concept takes root and the corporate language changes from I to we, or the group, or the team.

Every organization is rich in talent. The value of the gold we mine is in the people we lead. The best team efforts center on a shared vision, idea and mission. Teamwork requires everyone to accept responsibility and everyone to contribute toward the achievement of a goal. The USA must relearn that collaboration and interdependence are to be encouraged to avoid "the dark side of the force". Rugged individualism was excellent on the US frontier, but not in the complex workings of today's modern corporations.
