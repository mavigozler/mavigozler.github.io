How To Use

This calculator determines the volume of a more concentrated stock solution that will be used in preparing a dilute working solution whose desired concentration and volume is known.

This type of calculation is virtually the most commonly done among biological scientists/researchers, and this calculator makes it easy. Calculations of this type apply the law that describes the conservation of mass: the product of solution density (or concentration) and the volume of a solution is equal to the mass of the substance in solution, and since dilution of a more concentrated solution does not change the mass of the solute in solution, then the products of concentration and volume of the solution before and after dilution are equal.

Expressing that as an equation:

C1V1 = C2V2

Example #1: As an example of using this form, suppose one wants to make a 500 ml solution of 0.2 M sodium acetate solution and there is a 3 M sodium acetate stock solution available. Enter "3" in the input box for the stock solution (first box) and select "mol/L" in the dropdown selection list, enter "0.2" in box below for dilute working solution and select its units, and then enter "500" in the box for the volume and select "mL" for the volume dimensions. To get the answer, click the button "How is it done?"

Example #2: Suppose one has a 70% concentrated perchloric acid stock solution and one wants to make 1 liter of a 0.5 M stock solution. (Do not be confused here: "stock" essentially means a solution one will keep stored for regular use. One more dilute stock solution is being prepared from a more concentrated stock, so this calculator is still useful.). Enter "70" top box, "0.5" in the one below, and "1" in the box for the volume. Notice that as the units are selected, that a new entry suddenly appears, asking for a "formula weight" (or molecular weight). Read the formula weight for perchloric acid from the label, and enter it. Note that whenever the concentration units for one solution is ‘a form of grams per unit volume’ and the other is ‘a form of moles per unit volume,’ a formula weight for the substance is required to make the proper conversion.

This calculator also provides a service of letting you calculate the answer yourself and checking it against the computed result. Check the box to activate the part of the form that allows you to enter an answer.
