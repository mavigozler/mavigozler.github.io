Instructions

This module is supposed to take the difficulty of remembering the individual components of SDS-PAGE gels (as done by Laemmli) and the volumes necessary for pipetting them. There are basically two gels in this system. A separating (also called resolving or running) gel, in which the proteins are separated, and a stacking gel, in which proteins in solution in the well at the start of electrophoresis are concentrated into a tight band before entering the separating gel. Each gel has its own column both for entry of input values and for display of calculated volumes.

User-required inputs. Wherever a user input is required or allowed, an input textbox is a form control offered to the user. For each gel the user must enter a final percentage. For example, if the user is going to run a 10% gel, then the value 10 should be entered in the Final percentage textbox under Resolving/Separating/Running Gel. Note that a default value of 4% is indicated for the Stacking gel; although this is typical, it can be changed.

The Final volume of gels can either be given by the user directly or computed indirectly from a number of inputs given which include the count of gels and the height, width, and depth of the gel dimensions. Note that when calculating volumes, a pipetting error can be applied to the calculations. Click the checkbox at the top of the table columns where it indicates In volume calculations, add <error value > for pipetting error. A typical value of 15% is a default entry.

There are also five inputs for solution concentration values. Typical values are entered by default but can be changed. The Tris solutions are usually 4× stocks. Note that the final (gel) concentrations of Tris, SDS, ammonium persulfate, and TEMED can not be controlled, since these values are fixed in the method of Laemmli and important for SDS-PAGE. For acrylamide/crosslinker (crosslinker is usually bis-acrylamide) a list of typical acryalmide concentrations is given. Only the total (T) concentration is actually used to calculate the final percentage.

With the exception of the total polyacrylamide concentration, the final concentration of components in the gel is immutable:
  • Separating gel buffer: 0.375 M TrisHCl, pH 8.8
  • Stacking gel buffer: 0.125 M TrisHCl, pH 6.8
  • SDS concentration in both gels: 0.1% SDS
  • Ammonium persulfate concentration in both gels: 0.05% APS
  • TEMED concentration: 0.05% in the resolving, 0.1% in the stacking

This is because these values were determined by the work of Laemmli (Nature:London 227: 680, 1970). See below for an explanation of the many variations of the SDS-PAGE system.

Note that the electrode (running) buffer of 25 mM Tris, 192 mM glycine and 0.1% SDS is always made up by dissolving the following in distilled water, and bringing it to a final 1.0 liter volume:

  • 3.0 g Tris (base)
  • 14.4 g glycine
  • 10 ml of 10% SDS or 1 g SDS

There is no need to adjust the pH of the electrode buffer (it will be approximately 8.3).

The acrylamide/crosslinker concentration is expressed usually as a %T / %C, where %T is the total weight per volume of the acrylamide and the crosslinker and the %C is the weight per weight of the crosslinker as a proportion of the acrylamide plus the crosslinker:

However, the weight per volume of acrylamide and of the crosslinker (bis) is also usually provided: thus a 30% T solution is shown as 29:1, or 29 grams acryamide and 1 gram bis per 100 ml solution. Note that the %C is 3.3 = 1 / (29 + 1).

The final gel percentage, whether it is an 8, 10, or 12% is always an expression of the %T of the solution during polymerization. Thus a 10% gel will be a one-third dilution of a 30% stock, or a one-fourth dilution of a 40% stock, irrespective of the proportion of crosslinker (%C).

U. K. Laemmli used 30% acrylamide (30 g/100 ml) and 0.8% (0.8 g/100 ml) N, N′-bis-methyleneacrylamide, or 37.5 g acrylamide for each 1 g bis (37.5:1) in his original formulation, which has not changed at all. Note that %C = 0.8 g / (30 g + 0.8 g) = 2.6% C. For a 40% T stock solution, the bis is (40 g/100 ml)(0.026) = 1.04 g/100 ml, and the acrylamide is (40.0 g − 1.04 g)/100 ml = 39.0 g/100 ml.

Notes and Variations on SDS-PAGE

Scientists have used a great many variations on the SDS-PAGE system, and there are some important things to note:

  • If SDS-PAGE is used preparatively, such as for immunoblotting or gel excision to MALDI-MS, it is important to cast the gel a few hours before its use, so that the gel can cure: freshly polymerized gels are thought to have too many free radicals (persulfate) present which can react with proteins. However this has been disputed by some, who say that the free radicals are negatively charged and will move ahead of the proteins.
  • The final percentage of APS and TEMED in the monomer solution varies a great deal. U. K. Laemmli used 0.025% (w/v) APS and 0.025% (v/v) TEMED in his original widely cited paper. However, Bio-Rad recommends users of its systems
  • A great many are leaving SDS out of the stacking and separating gels. This allows cast gels to be storable for longer periods, and the idea is that the SDS present in the sample buffer and electrode buffer keeps the proteins exposed continuously to the proper concentration of SDS, and that SDS has a significant mobility and would distribute in the gels anyway.
  • Commercial pre-cast gels with a stacking and separating gel put pH 8.8 Tris buffer in both gels rather than pH 6.8 Tris in the stacking. The reason is that mixing of the buffers in the different gels occurs rapidly because of diffusion, and stacking occurs anyway if the sample buffer itself is pH 6.8.

  3. To determine the MW of an unknown band or spot, enter the Rm or the distance traveled. The Estimated MW should appear below.

Notes:

  • The Rm, or relative mobility, is the fraction that a protein band or spot travels in SDS-PAGE relative to the dye front. By definition, it must be a value between 0 and 1. For example, suppose in a mini-gel the BSA (66 kDa) marker moves 2.7 cm and the dye front moves 6.2 cm from the start (top) of the resolving gel. The BSA Rm thus is 2.7 cm / 6.2 cm = 0.435. The system is generally smart enough to know the difference between values entered being Rm or distance travelled.
  • Errors or irregularities will be noted when possible. It is the responsibility of the user to make careful entries since error checking is not rigorous.
  • The principle of electrophoresis should work for protein and DNA gels. For MW you can enter the bases (base pairs), and the Rm value should be the same.
