Select the units/dimensions first, then enter the value in a box. If a value is first entered in a box, and then the unit/dimension for that value is selected or changed, then the value will automatically be converted. A value entered into a box after units/dimensions are changed will not change the value, of course.

The radius of the rotor is never calculated. A speed for the centrifuge (in revolutions per minute, revolutions per second, or radian per second) is calculated if values for the radius and relative centrifugal force (RCF) are entered, and either one of those has its value changed. An RCF is calculated if values for the radius and speed are entered, and either one of those has its values changed.

In the course of entering a value in a box, use of the TAB or ENTER/RETURN keys or movement of the focus/cursor with the mouse will cause an update operation (calculation), if appropriate, with the value entered to be used in the calculation.

The average radius is the distance from the center of rotation to the point in the tube/container where the sample is most likely to be found from the rotational center.

For cylindrical container (tube), the sample at the top of the tube may be closer to the sample at the bottom of the tube. Since the force experienced by the sample depends up the radial distance from the center (radius), if the top of the tube is positioned a shorter distance than the bottom of the tube, sample at the top of the tube (at the minimum radius) experiences less of a force than sample at the bottom of the tube (at the maximum radius). This is especially the case for a swinging-bucket or -carrier type of centrifuge, as the tube swings into its position during the run. For fixed-angle rotors, there is less of a distance between the minimum and maximum radii, and so the force throughout the sample is more uniform.

The practical average radius of a swinging bucket type of rotor where the midpoint of the container's length is the pivot point is the radius to the pivot point. For a fixed-angle rotor, the average radius is the midpoint between the minimum and maximum radius. A little trigonometry solves this problem. If θ represents the angle of the rotor well (tube) from the horizontal toward the center, rmax is the maximal radius of the rotor well, rmin is the minimal radius of the well, and ravg is the average radius (the value desired), then the following equations are true:

(rotor well length) × cos θ = rmax − rmin

Note that ½ × (rotor well length) represents the midpoint of the length of the rotor well, a point that is ravg:

½ × (rotor well length) × cos θ = ravg

ravg can be solved in terms of rmin and rmax:

(rotor well length) × cos θ = 2 × ravg = rmax − rmin
ravg = ½ × (rmax − rmin)

Hence ravg is just the midpoint of the distance between rmin and rmax.
