Theory

Weak acids such as acetic acid (CH3COOH) or phosphoric acid (H3PO4) can be placed in water and reach an equilibrium in aqueous solution in which protons are transferred in the reaction:

HAn + H2O →← An−1 + H3O+

HA represents the protonated (acid) form of A, which is the conjugate base. A has an ionic charge of n while bound to its proton (n could be zero).

The equilibrium constant for this acid-base reaction, also called the acid dissociation constant, is defined as the ratio of the multiplied concentrations of the product and the multiplied concentration of the reactants, as for all equilibrium constants:

Ka = [An−1] × [H3O+]
[HAn] × [H2O]

By long-standing convention, water is removed from the equilibrium equation because its concentration effectively does not change in dilute acid solutions.

HAn →← An−1 + H+

And the acid dissociation constant is thus defined:

Ka = [An−1] × [H+]
[HAn]

The following equation takes the logarithm of both sides of the equation above and does some algebraic rearrangement:

−log [H+] = − logKa + log [An−1]
[HAn]

Note that a principle of logarithms is that log (ab / c) = log a + log b − log c.

By definition, pH = − log [H+], and pKa = − log Ka, and so the following substitutions are made:

pH = pKa + log [An−1]
[HAn]

This equation is called the Henderson-Hasselbach equation, after the scientists who presented what is really another form of the acid dissociation constant. It essentially states that given the physical property of a weak acid, its acid dissociation constant the Ka, and knowing the ratio of its conjugate base An−1 to its protonated form HAn, one can calculate the pH. Actually one can calculate any parameter of the equation if one knows any of the other parameters.

In many cases the ratio of the conjugate base An−1 and protonated form HAn is not set (and thus not known). For example, if only the acid form or conjugate base (salt of the acid) is placed in solution, then the dissociation must be determined from the total concentration. Assume the total concentration of all forms of the conjugate base A is C (a number representing the solution density, such as 1 M [one molar]). Thus:

C = An−1 + HAn

Now if the acid had been added, the concentration of An−1 and H+ are the same, since acid dissociation means that these ions must be formed equally. And with the dissociation, the concentration of HAn should be the total C less the concentration of An−1 (or H+) that is formed. Assume that x = [An−1] which also equals [H+], and so [HAn] = C − x. The equilibrium equation should now look like this:

Ka = x2
C − x

C and Ka are known, and so the solution of x is obtained (use the quadratic equation to solve for the positive root of x). As x represents [H+], the resulting pH of the solution is easily calculated.

Suppose on the other hand, the salt of the acid (conjugate base) is added to water and allowed to reach equilibrium. What is the final pH?

First look at the reaction occurring at equilibrium:

An + H2O →← HAn+1 + OH−

The equilibrium constant, Kb, is:

Kb = [HAn+1] × [OH−]
[An]

Kb happens to be 10−14 / Ka for the acid form of the conjugate base. As proof of this:

It is well-known that the acid dissociation of water itself

H2O →← H+ + OH−

although the reaction is actually:

2 H2O →← H3O+ + OH−

The acid dissociation constant, Kw, is defined and its value is well known:

Kw = [H+] [OH−] = 10−14

Since [OH−] = 10−14 / [H+], the appropriate substitution is made for the Kb equation:

Kb = [HAn+1] × 10−14
[An] × [H+]

Since 1 / Ka = [HAn+1] / ([An] + [H+]), then:

Kb = 10−14
Ka
Now the formation of [HAn+1] and [OH−] in the reaction is equal, and the total concentration of all forms of the acid is C = [HAn+1] + [An]. Substituting correctly, the equation is
Kb = x2
C − x

In solving for x, one gets [OH−]. To find the pH, one can calculate the pOH (= −log [OH−]) and then subtract its value from 14, or one can divide 10−14 by the value of [OH−] and then take the negative of the logarithm of the result.

These calculations work well for monoprotic acids and for polyprotic acids whose acid dissociations constants are separated by 3 or 4 orders of magnitude, but can become more complex with polyprotic acids whose acid dissociation constants are within 1-2 orders of magnitude within one another.

For examples, see the How To Use section.

How To Use

The use of this interface is shown by a few examples.

1. A solution of 0.1 M (glacial) acetic acid is prepared. What is the final pH?

One needs to know the acid dissociation constant and the total concentration. This problem is solved using the Ka = x2 / (C − x) form, and using the quadratic equation to solve for x.

Either enter the pKa or the Ka for acetic acid into the box (or alternatively, select from the list of well known acids or bases, and it will be entered automatically).

Enter the Total Concentration and make sure to select the proper units.

Select the pH to be calculated, and the pH and the concentrations of all the components will be reported.

If the pKa = 4.76, and C = 0.1, then the quadratic equation is used:

x = −Ka ± √Ka2 − 4(−Ka × C) = −(10−4.76) ± √(10−4.76)2 − 4(−10−4.76 × 0.1)

The solutions for x are −0.001327 and 0.0013096, but only the positive solution is relevant. Thus [H+] = 0.00131 M, and the pH = −log[0.00131] = 2.88. The concentration of acetate ion [CH3COO−] should be equal to the [H+] concentration, and the concentration of undissociated acid [CH3COOH] will be 0.1 − 0.00131 or 0.09869. Note that the calculation of the pH is independent of knowing the volume of the solution.

2. 10 grams of monobasic sodium phosphate dihydrate (NaH2PO4 • 2 H2O) is mixed with 25 grams of dibasic potassium phosphate (K2HPO4) in a final 500 ml solution. What is the final pH?

The acid is a form of phosphoric acid. Phosphoric acid is a polyprotic acid with three acidic protons with pKa values of 2.3, 7.2, and 12.2. When NaH2PO4 • 2 H2O is dissolved in solution, it produces the ion H2PO4−. When K2HPO4 is dissolved in solution, it produces the ion HPO42−. These two ions are in equilibrium:

H2PO4 →← H+ + HPO42−

Because this is the second proton released from phosphate, the second pKa is relevant to this reaction (pKa = 7.2). The formula weight of NaH2PO4 • 2 H2O is 156. 01 g/mol. The concentration is thus calculated to be (10 g) / (156.01 g/mol × 0.5 L) = 0.1282 M. Note that this is the concentration of all the ions in solution: [Na+] and [H2PO4− ]. The formula weight of K2HPO4 is 174.18 g/mol. The concentration of this molecule in solution is (25 g) / (174.18 g/mol × 0. 5 L) = 0.2871 M. Because there are 2 moles of potassium per mole molecule, its concentration is 0.574 M. However neither the [Na+] or [K+] contribute to the pH calculations, although it does contribute to the ionic strength.

The Henderson-Hasselbach form of the equation can now be used to calculate the pH. Note that [An−1] = 0.2871 M (where n = −1 although that is not significant) and [HAn] = 0.1282 M. Thus pH = 7.2 + log (0.2871)/(0.1282) = 7.55, the final value.

The interface tries to give the user as many options as possible without doing pre-calculations, but some pre-calculations may be necessary.
