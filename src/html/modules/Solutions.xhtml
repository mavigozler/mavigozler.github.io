Please read the following steps to prepare your solution:
  1. Weigh out on a balance accurate to the nearest exactly of
  2. Pour (or weigh) the into either:
    • a graduated cylinder if you have a PTFE (Teflon)-coated magnetic stirring bar that comfortably fits into and spins in the graduate cylinder and which can mix the entire solution at full volume in the with a reasonably viewable vortex or,
    • a beaker containing the proper Teflon-coated stir bar.
    Before adding the , fill your container with approximately (about 80% of the final volume) with water of the proper quality (see elsewhere for a discussion of proper water quality).
  3. Begin stirring on a magnetic stirrer to dissolve the . For certain salts, dissolution is aided by slight (40-50°C) or even moderate (as much as 80-90°C) heating. Don't be so quick to use heating however since it will be necessary to cool the solution prior to adding the remaining amount of water to the final volume; note the temperature etched on the glass of the vessel container is the temperature at which the solution and solvent must be for the final addition of water to produce the accurate concentration. Another reason to avoid heating is if the chemical is heat-sensitive and destroyed or altered above a certain temperature.
  4. When the substance has dissolved (there may be insoluble impurities present however) to the best of your experience, then q.s. (add water) to the mark representing the final volume. Note that the bottom of of the meniscus at the air-water interface must align with the mark The final concentration of the solution will be accurate if the temperature of the solution is at the temperature marked on the glass of the container, plus or minus 5°C.
  5. If the entire volume of the solution is not used immediately and it is intended that the solution is to be a working stock solution to be stored and used from from time to time, obtain an appropriate container for storage. If the substance is light-sensitive, it is especially important to use a container that reduces or even eliminates the transmission of room light through the container wall into the solution. A dark brown glass container is adequate for most purposes where light sensitivity is a consideration; alternatively, any glass container wrapped well in something like aluminum foil is sufficient. The bottle cap and its seal should be nonreactive with the solution. Be suspicious of using solutions where inspection of the material of the bottle cap seal shows it has disintegrate or been discolored possibly because of contact or the presence of the solution. Use appropriately non-reactive storage vessels with particularly corrosive solutions.

The bottles of all solutions should absolutely be labeled properly. Proper labeling prevents losses and waste that add to the supply costs of the laboratory when improper labeling requires discarding of substances whose contents or concentrations are unknown. There is also a safety factor, since certain chemicals are hazardous and must be discarded in hazardous waste containers (as opposed to dumping into the sink); hazardous waste disposal is very expensive, all the more so when one is using the service to discard a chemical that is harmless and disposed of by less expensive (or cost-free) means. Proper labeling requires printing in block letters large enough to be seen several feet away first the concentration then the name of the substance: A date (in short form) should be written as well. The initials the person preparing the solution may be useful or necessary as well, perhaps in accordance with policy of a multi-member lab or to identify ownership, etc.

Each solution has a certain shelf or storage life when stored in ideal conditions and when volumes are sampled properly. Where information regarding the life and usefulness of solutions is insufficient, experience will fill the void.
