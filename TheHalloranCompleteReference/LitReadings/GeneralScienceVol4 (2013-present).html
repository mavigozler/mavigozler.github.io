microRNA, miRNA, functional genomics, high-throughput screen, qPCR, tumor suppression

Cheng W-C, Kingsbury TJ, Sarah J. Wheelan SJ, Civin CI (2013) BioTechniques 54, 77-86

A simple high-throughput technology enables gain-of-function screening of human microRNAs

MicroRNA molecules serve the biological purpose of regulating gen exression at multiple levels, serving in cell survival, proliferation, and differentiation. Disturbances in expression can lead to disease. For example the miR-34 family of miRNAs is down-regulated in multiple cancers; these are transactivated by p53-dependent tumor suppressor mechanisms, includding apoptosis, cell cycle arrest, and senescence. In mouse models having low miR-34a expression, mimics were found to inhibit lung cancer growth. Preclinical and clinical studies show that miR-based therapeutics show no toxicity to normal cells, which shows a high therapeutic index. Transcriptome work shows differences in miRNA expression between diseased and normal cells, as well as among different cell types in stages of development (e.g. hematopoietic cells). However difference in expression cannot be indicative of a physiological or pathophysiological state, nor can they be concluded to be a cause of a disease state. There might be miRNAs that drive the cell state, and those which are merely passengers of it.

Large- (genomic-wide) scale approaches to doing miRNA functional screens have been reviewed (Serva et al, J Nucleic Acids 2011: 870903). One approach is transfection of miRNA mimic/inhibitor libraries where synthetic miRNAs are arrayed in 96- or 384-well plates. Wang et al (Nat. Genet. 39: 380, 2007) used this to identify miRNAs involved in G1 arrest of DGCR8 knockout embryonic stem cells. But for this to work, (i) cells must be transfected efficiently and (ii) screening is limited by transient arrays in which the mimic/or inhbitor is diluted with every cell division. These two limitations can be overcome using an miRNA expression lentivirus (lenti-miR) libraries, using limiting dilution to get one virus particle per plate. The lenti-miR approach was used to identify miRNAs that suppress growth or metasstais of cancer cell lines. The disadvantage of all approaches (mimic, inhibitor, virus) per well is that one miR can increase or decrease a function. Screens done in an array format require assays adaptable to microplates and require robotics.

An alternative is to use pool of multple species of lenti-miR or retroviral miR libraries where each virus encodes a unique miRNA or an miRNA cluster. The assay must detect a phenotype that results from over- or under-representation of cells that over-express the miRNAs. The identification of the miRNA causing the effect is done by noting that the miRNA integrates into the host genome, and computing the number of cells that have this property. Properties such as miRNAs that suppress cancer or that promote its invasiveness have been used to identify the miRNAs in question. This study reports the use of a barcode within the pooled-miRNAs approach and which is found by qPCR methods. This system is called miR-HTS. In the test case a pooled lenti-human miR library and custom qPCR program is used to screen IRM90 human lung fibroblasts for miRNAs regulating cell growth.

IMR90 cells were obtained from ATCC and cultured in MEM + 10% FBS, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, 1× GlutaMAX (LifeTech), 1× pen-strep (Mediatech). The genomic DNA representation qPCR (GRE-qPCR) assay design is as follows: System Biosciences (Mountain View, CA) provided the miRNA (plus flanking) insert sequences for each lenti-miR precursor. The GRE-qPCR is designed using Primer Express. Internal miR-specific forward primers for each assay are custom-plated by ABI at 18 pmol/well in 384-well plates. Internal common reverse primer, GFP primers, and external command forward and reverse primers are synthesized by IDT. GFP and internal common probes are synthesized by ABI. qPCR guidelines

Ware LC (2013) BioTechniques

MIQE qPCR Guidelines, Three Years Later

Reverse transcription qPCR is a gold standard for quantifying gene expression in a cell, but even using identical techniques two researchers can end up with different results. S. Ruskin led a group of researchers in 2009 to specify guidelines in Clinical Chemistry which are called the Minimum Information for Publication of Quantitative Real-Time PCR Experiments (MIQE). These address several variables in qPCR: sample quality control, assay design, PCR efficiency and normalization. One problem is the selection of reference genes and the reporting of the efficiency of amplification.
