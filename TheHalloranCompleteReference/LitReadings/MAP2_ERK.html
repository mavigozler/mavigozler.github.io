Purification and characterization of a protein kinase from Xenopus eggs highly specific for ribosomal protein S6

S6 from oocytes is almost all in unphosphorylated form; from unfertilized eggs, it is maximally phosphorylated. Hence the substrate was obtained by purification of 40S subunits from oocytes, whereas S6 kinase was obtained from unfertilized eggs. The 20-40% ammonium sulfate fraction of extracts contained much of the S6 kinase activity, with specific activity 2- to 2.5-fold higher than in the 0-20% and 40-50% fractions. There was no sensitivity to the PK-A peptide inhibitor. Gradient elution off DEAE-cellulose indicated two peaks eluting at 90 and 160 mM NaCl, with equal amounts of activity in both. After dialysis in a buffer for loading on to Mono S, peak II from DEAE was loaded on to the FPLC column and eluted in a gradient using KCl at 200 mM. Subsequent Sephacryl S-200 purification was done with a single peak eluting. (Re-chromatography using internal stds shows elution at 70-80 kDa.) Mono Q purification was done resulting at least a 20-fold purification, eluting at 235 mM NaCl. Afterward a heparin-Sepharose column was used in a 0 to 1 M NaCl gradient, with activity eluting at 0.5 M NaCl. A final purification of 500-fold of DEAE peak II is achieved with a 10% recovery of original activity. Only fractions of the highest specific activity from Mono S, Mono Q, and heparin-Sepharose were pooled. The assay system involved use of a 0.4 μM 40S subunit concentration, about 1/10 of the Km of the enzyme. On SDS gels, a single silver-stained band in the final step (heparin column) showed a protein at Mr 92,000.

It has been previously shown that the S6 kinase autophosphorylates when labeled ATP is added alone, with a 0.4 mol P/mol enzyme final stoichiometry. The rate of this autophosphorylation is rapid. Another very good substrate in Kemptide, and results of phosphorylation of the pure S6 show good activity. In the presence of heat-stable PK-A inhibitor, activity to Kemptide was actually 2-fold greater and not inhibited. By comparison, a 70 nmol/min/mg S6 kinase activity is found with 100 μM Kemptide, which shows a 10,500 nmol/min/mg PK-A catalytic subunit activity.

The Mg2+ dependence was observed to be optimal between 10 and 30 mM (the range of 1.2 to 50 mM was tested). Mn2+ dependence indicated an optimal range of 1.5 to 3 mM (1 to 5 mM tested), although 0.6 mM Mg2+ was present in all tests because they were present in substrates and enzymes added. Addition of Mn2+ to reactions already containing 10 mM Mg2+ did not enhance activity, but instead resulted in inhibition: at 2.5 mM Mn2+, there was 20% inhibition; at 5 mM, 40%. Km determinations for the S6 kinase show 28 μM for ATP and 5 μM for the 40S subunit, although the 40S Km is estimated since it was not possible to achieve saturable concentrations of 40S subunit for the kinase. Specific activities of 300 nmol/min/mg after Mono Q (where S6 kinase ~33% of the total protein) are achievable with 330 μM ATP (saturable) and 5.6 μM 40S subunit (at Km).

Inhibitor studies indicated inhibition by β-glycerophosphate, NaF, and KPi, which were very potent with Ki about 15-30 mM. Heparin also inhibited (IC50=30 μg/ml). In addition, ADP, quercetin, and spermine inhibited 50% of activity at 200, 10, and 500 μM, resp. No effect was observed with CaCl2, ZnCl2, sodium vanadate, or trifluoperazine (all at 0.1 mM), nor with cAMP, cGMP, EGTA, or calmodulin.

Rapid stimulation by insulin of a serine/threonine kinase in 3T3-L1 adipocytes that phosphorylates microtubule associated protein-2 in vitro

3T3-L1 cells are treated for 10 min with 80 nM insulin resulting in a 1.5- to 3-fold stimulation of a kinase which phosphorylates in vitro a bovine brain MAP-2. The stimulation was concentration-dependent (half-maximal at 5 nM). EGTA inclusion at all concentrations indicated no calcium dependence. Extracts of control and treated cells centrifuged at 150,000 × g for 60 min separated the activity into the supernatant, revealing the kinase's soluble nature. The same activity results were true, however, for lower speed spins (30,000 × g for 5 min) using MAP-2 as substrate. Extract supntnts gel-filtered on G-25 generally demonstrated no loss or major increase in activity, indicating that low MW factors are not necessary for activity other than MgATP.

Phosphoamino acid analysis of the substrate MAP-2 showed serine to be phosphorylated primarily, with threonine to a lesser extent. Since phosphotyrosine is degraded to some extent by acid hydrolysis, MAP-2 phosphorylated by membrane fractions of EGF-induced A431 cells showed a phosphotyrosine content on the same figure.

An insulin-induced kinase phosphorylating ribosomal protein S6 has been described and a time course of MAP-2 and S6 kinase activity is compared. Increased MAP-2 kinase activity is found in 3T3-L1 extracts within 5 min, is maximal at the 10 min point, and declines toward basal levels in the 20-40 min range since insulin treatment. The S6 kinase activity developed more slowly, lagging the MAP-2 kinase activity, was maximal at the 20 min time point, and never declined to only 80% of the maximal after 40 min.

The stability of the kinases were tested in extracts treated at 30° for a 10-30 min period. Homogenates were done in β-glycerol phosphate, previously shown to protect an S6 kinase from Swiss 3T3 cells. While the S6 kinase never lost more than half of its activity after 30 min., the MAP-2 kinase activity virtually disappeared.

Biochemical differences in MAP-2 and S6 kinase activity were found in phosphocellulose chromatography, in which the S6 kinase was bound with the buffer used and which was eluted with 0.5 M NaCl. MAP-2 kinase did not adsorb. Chromatography of extracts which had been treated with insulin or diluent for 5 or 40 min were run on phosphocellulose as well. The MAP-2 kinase activity elutes in a very broad peak in the unbound phases (effluent and wash) for the insulin-treated 5 min activity, and in a narrower peak from the wash with the other treatments and times. S6 kinase activity always comes off at 0.5 M salt, and the activity was much higher in the 40 min extract than the 5 min extract.

Phosphatase inhibitors were tried to determine which phosphatases might be at work de-activating the MAP-2 kinase activity. 40 mM p-nitrophenyl phosphate addition allowed 81% and 50% of the activity to remain after 10 and 30 min at 30°, whereas controls showed 12% remaining after 10 min. Phosphotyrosine at 40 mM was as effective as β-glycerol phosphate at 80 mM, preserving 44% and 17% after 10 and 30 min. Phosphoserine was without effect.

Insulin-stimulated MAP-2 kinase phosphorylates and activates ribosomal protein S6 kinase II

INTRO. In every report investigating mitogen-stimulated cells, S6 kinase activity is shown to be elevated. Direct tyrosine phosphorylation of S6 kinase has not been shown in vitro however, since purified S6 kinase II in not an in vitro substrate for the insulin receptor kinase, pp60v-src, or the Abelson murine leukemia virus tyrosine kinase. Immunoprecipitates of extracts of 32P-labeled insulin-treated oocytes in which an antiserum to the kinase fail to reveal the presence of phosphotyrosine. Moreover, serine/threonine-specific protein phosphatases de-activate the S6 kinase II. Extracts of all mitogen-stimulated cells studied so far contain elevated S6 kinase activity, especially insulin-stimulated oocytes and other cells. Direct activation of S6 kinase is possible, but the S6 K II is not a substrate in vitro for the insulin receptor tyrosine kinase nor pp60y-src nor the Abelson MLV-associated tyrosine kinase. Immunoprecipitates of S6 K II show no presence of phosphoTyr after insulin stimulation. S6 K II is deactivated by treatment with protein phosphatase 1 and 2A in vitro, however, showing an indirect mechanism. PK-C and PK-A are not able to reactivate S6 K II after phosphatase treatment; that the MAP-2 kinase is activated by insulin and cannot phosphorylate histones, casein, S6, or of a number of substrates with insulin-stimulated increased phosphate content indicates an indirect effect.

Partially purified MAP kinase is shown to reactivate the S6 K II which had been deactivated by prior incubation with phosphatase 1 and 2A. The phosphatase 2A treatment reduced S6 K II activity 98%, according to the amount of S6 protein able to incorporate 32P-ATP, according to a quantitation method by Erikson & Maller (JBC 261: 350, 1986). Phosphatase 1 treatment has been previously shown to reduce activity by 60%. MAP kinase used after phosphatase treatment (NaF was brought to 10 mM or 240 U of inhibitor-2 was added to inhibit the phosphatases at least 90% by use of a phosphorylase a standard) restored activity 30 to 60% of the control value. S6 K II which had not been phosphatase-treated was activated 2-fold by MAP kinase. Activation was fully dependent upon addition of MAP kinase, and was not observed when the MAP kinase had been heated to 80° for 2 min.

The amount of activation was directly correlated with the amount of phosphorylation of S6 kinase. With maximal reactivation, there was ~0.5 mol P/mol S6 kinase. The activation was much greater than that activation occurring with autophosphorylation. Phosphopeptides mapped by TLE/C cellulose indicated that MAP kinase created two new phosphopeptides in the S6 kinase. The same pattern was seen regardless of whether the S6 kinase was phosphatase 1-treated. Similar results were seen with phosphatase 2A-deactivated S6 kinase treated with the MAP kinase, indicating that at least one phosphorylation site for MAP kinase is distinct from autophosphorylation sites and that MAP kinase merely does not stimulate autophosphorylation. Phosphoamino acid analysis of these peptides from MAP-treated S6 kinase with or without prior phosphatase treatment indicated that only threonine was present. Since the autophosphorylation sites are serine, then the MAP kinase phosphorylates threonine as one of its residues.

MAP kinase prepared from insulin-treated 3T3-L1 cell extracts, in which insulin is known to induce a 20-fold activation of MAP kinase in these cells, was shown to reactivate the S6 kinase which had been deactivated by phosphatase 1 or 2A. This proves that the activity capable of S6 kinase reactivation is stimulated by insulin and is associated with elevated MAP kinase activity. The greater degree of reactivation of ph’tase 2A-treated S6 kinase compared to ph’tase 1-treated kinase merely reflects the ability of ph’tase 2A to inhibit S6 kinase to a greater degree than ph’tase 1, and the ability of MAP kinase to restore the activation. A subsequent ph’tase treatment after MAP kinase activation was able to deactivate the S6 kinase once again.

The partially purified MAP kinase was subjected to Superose 12 chromatography (in 50 mM HEPES, pH 7.5, 50 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, 1 mM DTT) to achieve additional purification of the MAP kinase; it was the major protein in its fraction. MAP kinase activity, S6 phosphorylating activity, and S6 re-activating activity all co-eluted from the column.

The MAP kinase is known to become active in insulin-treated 3T3-L1 cells just prior to S6 kinase activation. Previous work shows that phosphotyrosine and phosphothreonine are the phosphorylated amino acids. We now show that ph’tase 2A treatment of the MAP kinase results in its time-dependent inactivation. Ph’tase 1 had no effect. It is known that ph’tase 1 is absolutely specific for P-Ser and P-Thr, but ph’tase 2 can dephosphorylate P-Tyr. We have been unable to reactivate the MAP kinase with any tyrosine kinases in vitro so far, including the insulin receptor kinase.

Characterization of insulin-stimulated microtubule-associated protein kinase: rapid isolation and stabilization of a novel serine/threonine kinase from 3T3-L1 cells

3T3-L1 cells are treated with insulin (80 nM, 10 min) and found to have 1.5- to 3-fold MAP kinase which persists after DEAE-cellulose chromatography. Elution of a single-peak at 0.25 M NaCl is shown (the extraction buffer contains p-NO2 phenyl phosphate and PMSF as the only inhibitors). Some MAP kinase elutes during washing of the column.

Hydrophobic interaction chromatography was successful with either phenyl-Sepharose or -Superose. Low salt buffers allowed adsorption while high concentrations of ethylene glycol were necessary for elution. A simultaneous gradient of NaCl (250 to 25 mM) and ethylene glycol (0-60%) was employed over 12 column volumes of phenyl-Superose. A large amount of the activity did not adsorb to the column and was eluted during washing. However, the insulin-treated extracts did yield an adsorbed MAP kinase peak eluting at 30% ethylene glycol

Gel filtration on Superose 12 of cellular extracts had shown the MAP kinase to elute just ahead of carbonic anhydrase (apparent MW of 35,000). Gel filtration of phenyl-Superose eluted activity also demonstrated the 35,000 apparent MW. Peak fractions from phenyl-Superose were concentrated and then centrifuged in glycerol gradients with a kinase activity at Mr 37,000. The activity was found to be unstable as reported previously, but phenyl-Superose peak fractions stored at -70° in 1 mg/ml BSA maintained activity suitably. In further preparations of the kinase, extracts were applied to DEAE, step-eluted with 0.35 M NaCl, purified as above on phenyl-Superose, then activity concentrated in presence of BSA.

Protein purification of extracts which had been pre-incubated with 32P i allowed following additional purification from phenyl-Superose on to gel filtration. BSA was added as a carrier during these steps. Silver-stained SDS gel analysis of fractions from Superose 12 showed a 40 kDa which correlated with the MAP kinase activity in the fractions, which is the only phosphoprotein detectable. It was previously reported that phosphotyrosine and -threonine are present.

Ultra high-speed spins have shown the kinase to be cytosolic. Particulates were resuspended and catalyze MAP-2 phosphorylation only 11% of the supernatant. Mixing of the supernatant and pellet roughly catalyzed the same rate as the supernatant alone, demonstrating that no ATPases were responsible for inability of pellet to cause MAP-2 phosphorylation.

Kinetic studies on the enzyme showed that ATP was the preferred phosphate donor, with GTP phosphate transfer being negligible. MAP-2 as a substrate was analyzed from 60 to 1000 μg/ml. An apparent K m range of 4-12 μM is determined by linear regression analysis. Substrate specificity found that MAP-2 was preferential. Casein phosphorylation, while detected, was <10% of MAP-2 levels. Phosvitin, protamine, histones, ribosomal protein S6, acetyl-CoA carboxylase, and ATP-citrate lyase were phosphorylated poorly, if at all. The pH optimum range was determined using extract supernatants, with optimal activity found in a broad range about a peak of pH 8. Potassium fluoride was found to inhibit the kinase, reduced 50% with 60 mM KF present. Half-maximal inhibition was observed with 45 mM β-glycerophosphate, and heparin in the range of 0.5-5.0 μg/ml had no effect, but higher concentrations slightly stimulated incorporation of 32P into MAP-2.

Discussion. The MAP-2 kinase has now been identified in 3T3-L1 cells, BC3-H1 and 23A2 myocytes, H4 hepatocytes, and freshly isolated rat adipocytes and hepatocytes. The MAP-2 kinase isolated from TIG-3 human lung fibroblastic cells by Hoshi et al. (1988), which is activated by insulin and other growth factors, and which does not adsorb to phosphocellulose (like MAP kinase), is probably not the same MAP-2 kinase here since that kinase is inhibited by low Ca2+ concentrations. This MAP-2 kinase has been shown to phosphorylate the S6 kinase II recently isolated by Maller’s group. Other kinases (various tyrosine kinases, PK-C, PK-A catalytic subunit, casein kinases I and II) fail to activate this kinase. The soluble Mn2+-dependent serine kinase isolated from insulin-stimulated rat adipocytes is not the MAP-2 kinase, since it activity fell with high Mg2+ levels (the levels used for MAP-2 kinase), gel filtration analysis estimated a Mr of 50,000 to 60,000, 60 mM fluoride was tested and did not inhibit, and the kinase preferred histones and ATP-citrate lyase, which are hardly touched by MAP-2 kinase. A recently reported membrane-bound serine/threonine kinase from rat adipocytes preferring histone and stimulated by insulin may be distinct from the MAP-2 kinase since the MAP-2 kinase is soluble and does poorly on histones. Casein kinase I, known to phosphorylate MAP-2, and casein kinase II, known to be stimulated by insulin, and both of which have in whole or in subunit similar sizes to MAP-2 kinase, may nonetheless be distinct; MAP-2 kinase does poorly with casein, cannot utilize GTP (casein kinase II does equally well with GTP or ATP), is not inhibited by heparin (a strong inhibitor of casein kinase II), and does not adsorb to phosphocellulose (shown for casein kinase I under the same buffer conditions). Furthermore, MAP-2 kinase is not likely to be protein kinase C, whose proteolytically cleaved form, protein kinase M, is larger than MAP-2 kinase and easily phosphorylates protamine. Also distinguished from the protease-activated kinase II, which is slightly larger, adsorbs to P-cellulose, and readily phosphorylates ribosomal S6 subunit.

Phosphorylation of microtubule-associated protein-2 by MAP kinase primarily involves the projection domain

Intro. MAP-2 is a phosphoprotein shown to stimulate tubulin assembly in vitro and to mediate interaction between microtubules and other subcellular components. Chymotrypsinolysis of MAP-2 results in cleavage into two domains: (1) a small 32-39 kDa fragment able to bind tubulin and (2) a large 240 kDa projection domain. PK-A phosphorylates both domains, and phosphorylation of the tubulin binding domain inhibits MAP-2-assisted assembly, presumably by interfering with ionic interactions of the basic amino acid repeats with the acidic region in the tubulin COOH terminus.)

MAP-2 is phosphorylated in the presence of labeled ATP by the MAP kinase or by PK-A and then digested by chymotrypsin over a time course (from 0.5 to 16 min) and run on SDS gels. Both Coomassie stains and autorads reveal the emergence of two bands at 39 and 31 kDa along with a 240 kDa band which represent the tubulin-binding domain fragments and projection domain, resp. The results show that the projection domain is the only labeled fragment from MAP kinase phosphorylation while both domains are labeled as a result of PK-A phosphorylation. Note that previous work has shown protein kinase C to be distinct from the MAP kinase as it phosphorylates MAP in the tubulin-binding domain.

Requirement for integration of signals from two distinct phosphorylation pathways for activation of MAP kinase

MAP kinase was purified from fibroblasts treated with EGF (30 nM, 10 min) and shown to be active. The protein was incubated over a time course of 0, 15, 45 and 60 min with protein phosphatase 2A and the membrane glycoprotein protein tyrosine phosphatase, CD45. (The reaction for protein phosphatase 2A is stopped with 10 mM NaF + 1 mM EDTA; 0.2 mM sodium vanadate for CD45). MAP kinase activity is assessed with MBP. Both phosphatases inactivate the MAP kinase, although phosphatase 2A is a little faster than CD45. Since the reaction was done in the presence of protease inhibitors, and there was no shift in mobility of labeled phosphoprotein from relative Mr of 42,000, and incubation of the kinase with inclusion of the above phosphatase inhibitors with phosphatase addition for periods longer than 60 min still left sufficient kinase activity, it is apparent that loss of kinase activity does not result from degradation or thermal denaturation.

The CD45 was prepared using immunoaffinity chromatography and lacked Ser/Thr phosphatase activity. 1 μM okadaic acid did not affect the ability of CD45 to inactivate MAP kinase. Since okadaic acid at this concentration inhibits phosphatases 1, 2A, and 2B, phosphorylation of tyrosine on MAP kinase is therefore regulatory.

Phosphoamino acid analysis verifies these results as well. Phosphotyrosine and -threonine are the only phosphorylated residues present in active MAP kinase. When incubated with inactivated CD45 and phosphatase 2A, there is no apparent change in the spot size. Incubation with active phosphatase 2A inactivates the enzyme and eliminates the P-Thr spot while not changing the P-Tyr spot. On the other hand, CD45 incubation for a period serving to partially or completely de-activate MAP kinase also correlated with partial spot reduction or complete spot elimination of the P-Tyr spot without affecting the P-Thr spot. Both phosphatases are necessary to remove phosphate from either residues completely.

From the work, it can be concluded that phosphorylation of both threonine and tyrosine residues is necessary to activate the MAP kinase. This is the first time that phosphorylation of different species of amino acids is required. With MPF, p34cdc2 subunit must be de-phosphorylated on both tyrosines and threonines during interphase in order for activation to occur, which permits transition in the G1-S and G2-M phases. Placental tyrosine phosphatase 1B, which has some homology with CD45 was less effective in de-activating and de-phosphorylating MAP kinase, although it is much more effective than CD45 toward labeled phosphotyrosyl-lysozyme, suggesting some substrate specificity of these phosphatases.

A model in which integration of serine/threonine kinases and tyrosine kinases to activate a common cascade intermediate is suggested by the requirement for double phosphorylation needed for activation of MAP-2 kinase.

Nerve growth factor stimulates a protein kinase in PC-12 cells that phosphorylates microtubule-associated protein-2

NGF has been shown to increase the phosphorylation of a number of proteins in PC12 cells, including some MAPs. These changes also shown with EGF. PC12 extracts either stimulated with NGF or EGF were assayed for MAP-2 kinase activity, incorporation of 32P being assessed by autoradiographs of SDS-PAGE gels. The lysates of the NGF-treated cells consistently showed a 2-4-fold increase compared to controls, which the EGF-treated cell lysates show a 1.5- to 2.5-fold enhancement.

When 100 nM staurosporine is added to the NGF-treated cells, the MAP-2 phosphorylation increases are abolished. Phosphorylation activity in controls remain unaffected however. Staurosporine failed to inhibit EGF-induced MAP kinase activity, indicating that EGF works through another mechanism than does NGF. In contrast, addition of 100 nM K252a or 25 μM H-7 to cells caused a 2-fold increase in MAP kinase activity of both the NGF- and EGF-treated cells, but they had no effect by themselves on the MAP kinase increases. K252a and staurosporine are thought to be potent inhibitors of PK-C, but there is a possibility that they may inhibit other kinases as well.

The kinases which might possibly be involved were analyzed. The PK-A (Walsh) peptide inhibitor added to the assay had no effect on any reaction, showing the PK-A independence of the activity. Direct addition of K252a and staurosporine (100 nM both) also had no effect, showing they work in situ. In addition, there was no Ca2+ requirement for the kinase activity, so it was possible to exclude both PK-C and a CaM-sensitive kinase. Addition of 0.5 μM heparin activated the kinase 58%, which shows that casein kinase is not involved as it is very sensitive to heparin. The NGF-stimulated extract kinase activity could be reduced to basal levels by addition of 50 mM NaF, showing that it is the insulin-sensitive MAP kinase from 3T3-L1 adipocytes.

The NGF dose dependence studies indicated that 0.3 nM NGF was the half-maximal dose, 1 nM the dose at which maximal activity was obtained. Data from the steady state binding analysis at 37° done previously (Schechter & Bothwell, Cell 24: 867, 1981) with a equilibrium dissociation constant of 0.2 nM matches close with the EC50 measure. Time course data seemed to indicate two phases of activation. The first phase is marked by a rapid MAP kinase activation holding a maximum between 5-10 min, then declining (but not reaching basal) to a point at 20-25 min. The second phase is marked by another activation to a maximum at 30 min (about 40% of the first maximum), followed by a slow decline that still had not reached basal at the last time point (120 min); the activity at the 60 min and 120 min time point were the same. If the PK-C activator PMA had been added 10 min prior to NGF addition, the first maximum was unaffected but the second (delayed onset) maximum of MAP kinase activity was potentiated (about double the maximum without PMA). It is interesting to not that these kinetics are identical with the kinetics of EGF-induced activation of S6 kinase activity in Swiss 3T3 cells (Susa et al., Cell 57: 817, 1989), in which the authors attributed the second phase of activation to effects of PK-C.

PC12 cells preincubated instead with staurosporine, K252a, or H7 10 min prior to NGF addition were assessed in a time course. Staurosporine completely abolished the first phase, while K252a had a stimulating effect. Neither staurosporine or K252a affected the late phase. H7 slightly enhanced both the first and second phase of activation. These results taken together provide no evidence for a role for PK-C in mediating these effects.

Biochemical characterization was done using FPLC Mono Q in which both control and NGF-treated cell extracts were partially purified. A major species of kinase activity was eluted at 0.25 M NaCl, in which the NGF-treated was more than 3-fold greater than control cells. A minor MAP kinase (which had the same specific activity in NGF-treated and controls) eluted just ahead at 0.16 M NaCl. Both gel filtration (Superose 12) and sucrose density gradient centrifugation of cell lysates fixed the apparent MW at 36,000 and 41,000, resp. The NGF-treated extracts always had significantly greater specific activity. Elution profile comparison with standards on the Superose 12 column show a Stokes radius of 31 Å. Collision-activated dissociation is a new technology in peptide sequencing. The method is such: (1) the first mass filter of the triple quadrupole instrument, quadrupole 1, is set to pass all ions within a 6-mass unit window centered around the (M+H)+ ions for the amidated and de-amidated forms of the phosphopeptide at m/z 1571.8 and 1572.8, resp. (2) Transmission to the collision chamber of the (M+H)+ ions causes them to suffer 10-100 low energy (15-40 eV) collisions with argon present at 3 mtorr. The kinetic energy change is converted to bond vibrational energy, resulting in fragmentation in the amide bonds along the peptide chain backbone. Water, ammonia, and phosphoric acid are small molecules which are eliminated from side chains, with a collection of neutrals and ions produced. (3) Neutrals are pumped out by the vacuum system, while the ion fragments are transmitted to a third mass filter, quadrupole 3 which separates according to mass. Quadrupole 3 is operated at less than unit resolution to maximize ion transmission.

Spectra are generated by scanning the mass range 100-1600 Da for 16 times, with data acquired in the centroid mode, and the resulting spectra summed. Total instrument time to acquire data is 64 s. The signal peaks are generated in clusters because the data system resolves at more than 1 unit wide, there is sensitivity to the contributions of isotopic forms (13C) of atoms, and the mixture of amidated and de-amidated forms contributes.

Sequence information is obtained by subtracting m/z values for peaks that occupy the same relative positions within two cluster patterns and are separated by >57 and <186 mass units, which are the residue masses of the heaviest and lightest amino acids. Type y ions are the fragments which contain the esterified -COOH terminus plus 1, 2, 3, 4, etc. additional residues. Predicted m/z values are correlated with observed m/z values: 9 of the 13 are observed from m/z 516.3 to 1457.8. Subtraction of the m/z values between type y ions helps determine the sequence of the first nine residues (the differences are the mass of the amino acid). Type b ions (which start from the amino terminus) are also observed in the spectrum, and used to confirm those results of the type y ion data.

In other gels co-polymerized with protamine, mixed histones, Lys-rich histones, or casein, the spectrum was completely different. Although these are all MBP kinases, the presence of MBP in chromaffin cells, or the presence of these kinase in cells where MBP is found, is unknown.

The dependence of these kinases for Mg 2+ or Mn2+ was analyzed: PK116 required Mg2+, while PK60 and PK53 were Mn2+-dependent. PK46 and PK42 were active with either cation, but were more active with Mn2+. Prior to cell extraction, chromaffin cells were treated with IGF-I (10 nM), PDBu (1 μM), diacylglycerols OAG or DOG (100 μM), nicotinic agonists (carbachol at 100 μM; DMPP at 30 μM), muscarine (100 μM), bradykinin (10 μM), and 55 mM K+. All of the reagents caused a 2-fold increase in MBP phosphorylating ability of PK42 and PK46.
