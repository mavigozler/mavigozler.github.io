Evaluation of Low Energy CID and ECD Fragmentation Behavior of Mono-Oxidized Thio-Ether Bonds in Peptides

Bottom-up (expression) proteomics involves protein isolation and comparison of its tryptic peptide masses obtained by MS to a database set of sequences for high confidence identifications. In the process, proteins must be reduced to break -S-S- bonds whose reformation is blocked with an alkylating agent in a standard nucleophilic substitution, with other reagents being Michael acceptors. The product has a thioether bond (-R-S-R′-). If iodoacetamide was used, a neutral fragment is lost during low energy MS/MS with formation of dehydroalanine (CH2=C(NHR)-COR′) at the residue. The part of the side chain lost is a sulfenic acid (RSOH, where R is the rest of the structure depending on blocking agent used).

For this study, two C-containing peptides (SEVAHRFKC [Pep1] and CHWKQNDEQM [Pep2]) were synthesized on ABi 431-A, one with N-terminal and other with C-terminal cysteines. Pep1 was treated with iodoacetamide then with 3% H2O2 15 min, desalted with C18 ZipTip, vacuum dried and injected to ESI-FTICR MS (Bruker Daltonics 7T APEX Q-FTICR, with CID spectra calibrated using substance P, bradykinin, and Pep1) in 49% MeOH/2% acetic acid solvent with a 20 µl/h nanospray. A [M+2H]2+ ion at m/z 575.2794 representing oxidized alkylated peptide was passed as a parent to the collision cell with the trap voltage altered to -1V, -6V, -9V, and -12V. At -6V (low energy collision) a peak at m/z 521.7770 shows at high intensity with predicted neutral loss of RSOH (where R=58.0292). Accurate mass calculation shows this neutral fragment mass (RSOH, C2H5NO2S) to be 107.0042 with the observed(measured) being 107.0048, an error of 5 ppm. At -9V the ion also is at high intensity, thus at an energy low enough that no other fragments but the neutral loss of RSOH occurring. At -12V, the relative intensity was even greater for the ion.

Besides iodoacetamide, other blocking agents were studied. Maleimide modifies thiols through Michael addition at a reactive alkenyl bond. 3-maleimidpropionic acid was used to modify Pep1. A [M+2H]2+ ion at m/z 631.2848 is observed and collisional trap voltage altered. Ions at m/z 521.7758 and 537.7613 appear, the latter its intensity increasing with collision voltage, while the former had a low intensity that did not change with increasing voltage. Thus there is a loss of R + H2 from oxidized peptide, whose calculated mass is 187.0480 and whose measured mass is 187.0470.

4-vinylpyridine is another Cys-modifying agent. It differs from being maleimide-type in that there is asymmetry in the alkenyl bond. Presence of an ion at m/z 599.2939 shows the [M+2H]2+ vinylpyridine-modified Pep1. With increasing collisional trap voltage, an ion at m/z 537.7590 increasing relative to base peak with increasing voltage indicates neutral loss fragment R + water. Also increasing in relative intensity with collisional voltage increase is an ion at m/z 1074.5105 (appearing strong in -9V spectrum, 1074.5125 in -12V spectrum), representing a +1 peptide with R + H2 loss. Also present to a small degree was the m/z 512.7590 ion, showing a RSOH loss.

The N-terminal Cys peptide Pep2 was also used to see if it had the same effect. Peptide mass was checked for modified (and unoxidized) peptide. In addition an electron capture dissociation (ECD) was used to generate fragments (features a hot hollow cathode brought to 1.8-1.9 amperes and located outside ICR cell, with side kick trapping voltage from +6V to -6V, and electrons accelerated with 3 eV and 200 ms electron injection time). Because Pep2 also has Met, it gets oxidized with the peroxide treatment. The 3-maleimidopropionic-modified and oxidized peptide shows [M+2H]2+ ion at m/z 760.2837. Increasing collisional voltage produces ion at m/z 666.7595 representing 2+ ion with R + H2O neutral loss.

The presence of Met as a sulfoxide is not different than an oxidized thio-ether bond off the Cys side chain. -15V spectra were obtained on oxidized and untreated peptides and showed similar fragmentation patterns, indicating that the Met sulfoxides do not fragment in the same way as oxidized thio-ether modifications of Cys. ECD spectra of oxidized iodoacetamide-treated Pep1 generated three c ions, c6, c7, and c8 (SEVAHR.F.K.C, where c6 is SEVAHR, etc.). Also present is an [M+H]+ ion at m/z 1149.57 and 1091.57, the latter representing loss of iodoacetamide from modified and oxidized peptide. Thus R+H2 and RSOH losses are seen, but at energies that also cause peptide fragmentation. Fragmentation patterns using ECD were similar for maleimide-treated oxidized Pep2 and iodoacetamide-treated oxidized Pep1. Generally with ECD based spectra there is more fragmentation of the peptide backbone, and in iodoacetamide-treated there is RSOH and R + H2O neutral losses, but with maleimide treatment on R + H2O losses are seen.

The mechanism of gas phase β elimination of oxidized thio-ethers involves a coordination of an acidic (highly positively polarized) hydrogen (β to the sulfur atom and/or carbonyl group) to a non-bonding electron pair on the oxygen atom forming the oxidized thio-ether. Arrangements of the bonds breaks the thio-ether bond to the R group, leaving -CH2-S-OH sulfenic acid on the side and restoration of the R group. The sulfenic acid group is highly unstable, and leads to water loss to produce -CH=S sulfide (relatively more stable). This accounts for the R + H2O loss.

In the other mechanism, the other thio-ether bond oriented to the side chain amino acid is broken (rather than the one connecting the R group modifier). This is because the negative polar oxygen atom instead bonds to the H atom on the α-carbon of the peptide backbone, with a concerted breaking of the -C-CH2-S(=O)- thio-ether bond to produce dehydroalalanine and the RSOH leaving group. The modifying group will dictate which mechanism dominates, if one dominates at all.

Bayesian Methods for Proteomics

REVIEW. In tossing a coin, it is expected that for a proper coin, either of one of two outcomes is equally likely. But if a large count of heads begins to appear, this additional information is used to determine the predictive power. Bayesian methods are a set of principles that incorporates external information into the data analysis process. This process starts with an established probability distribution called the prior distribution. Then one adds the information obtained by experience or polling or surveys to create a posterior distribution.

Independence is a term used to describe events that occur indepedent—or unconditionally—of other events. In testing for a disease or disorder, what is the probability of having the disease given a test result indicating the disease, which is measure of the sensitivity of the test? Suppose that the probability of a test being positive for a disease where the disease is actually present is P(T+|D+)=0.95. This means that the probability of the test being negative when the disease is present is P(T-|D+)=0.05. Suppose also the probability of the test being negative when there is disease is P(T-|D-)=0.95, and that it is negative when the disease is present is P(T+|D-)=0.05. Additionally it is known that the disease is present in 1% of the public, and so the probability of finding the disease is P(D+)=0.01. Of interest to the clinical scientist is the probability that the disease is present given the test is positive:

P(D+|T+) = {P(T+|D+)×P(D+)} / P(T+)

Note that P(T+) = {P(T+|D+)×P(D+)} + {P(T+|D-)×P(D-)}

The computed result is:

(0.95 × 0.01) / (0.95 × 0.01 + 0.05 × 0.99) = 0.161.

This seems low, but because the disease is present in only 1% of the population, this is the case. Suppose a second test (T2) after a first test (T1) is done. What is the probability of the disease presence given both a positive first and second test result:

[P(D+|T1 ∩ T2) = {P(T1+|D+)×P(T2+|D+)×P(D+)]/P(T1+ ∩ T2+)

Exploring the Intensity Fading Phenomenon in the Study of Noncovalent Interactions by MALDI-TOF Mass Spectrometry

The noncovalent interactions studied here include bovine carboxypeptidase A (CPA) with potato carboxypeptidase inhibitor (PCI) and trypsin with the bovine pancreatic trypsin inhibitor (BPTI). Leech carboxypeptidase inhibitor (LCI) was also obtained. LCI, PCI, CPA, and aprotinin were dissolved in 20 mM ammonium acetate. Trypsin was dissolved in MilliQ water. A fraction of proteins between 3-5 kDa using size-exclusion chromatography from Hirudo medicinalis was lyophilized and dissolved in MilliQ water. To measure any inhibitory activity on CPA in this fraction or elsewhere, the CPA reaction with substrate N-(4-methoxyphenylazoformyl)-Phe-OH at 350 nm was done.

Changes to relative intensity of PCI (m/z 4298) are studied after adding its interaction partner CPA with matrices SIN, DHAP, CHCA, ATT, 2,5-DHB, and HABA using dried droplet technique. 2,6-DHB, SIN and CHCA were also prepared using the thin-layer sample prep method. The thin-layer technique dissolves matrix in acetone at 20 mg/ml (2,6-DHB at 100 mg/ml), spots a 5 µl aliquot of matrix on to target, then 0.5 µl of sample solution is deposited on top of the dried matrix layer. After drying, sample is washed with 2 µl MilliQ water for a few seconds, with the water removed. In general, the dried droplet technique gave better results.

When increasing amounts of CPA (100, 200, 400, 600 fmol) are added, the PCI peak at m/z 4298 disappears in sinapic acid. Other peaks are clearly noticeable but do not fade as PCI does. A CHCA/ammonium citrate matrix gave similar results, as did ATT/ammonium acetate microcrystalline deposit matrix.

ATT is a less acidic matrix (Table 2 shows the pH values of the various matrix solutions, reproduced here)

With ATT, two different crystal patterns developed. At the rim of the sample spot thin macrocrystalline long needles are prominent with the center covered with homogeneous microcrystalline layer (especially with ammonium acetate). When the laser is targeted on the macrocrystalline needles, the PCI peak does not diminish (with or without ammonium salts), but there is a diminution of signal in the microcrystalline layer.

High spot-to-spot signal variability is observed when the laser focus on the long needle crystals. In some positions, signal is lost quickly with just a few laser shots. Increase of laser fluence or number of laser shots left the inhibitor unaffected: it has same relative signal as the uncomplexed control. With 2,5-DHB, PCI signal never diminished even with 2-fold molar excess of CPA addition, indicating the matrix interferes with binding. An important note: There were no signals observed at m/z ~34,200, for free CPA or at m/z ~38,500 predicted for the CPA-PCI complex when the mass range was adjusted under these conditions. Use of sinapic acid, CHCA, and 2,6-DHB did not fade the PCI signal.

However, detectable signals were found with 7 pmol PCI + 10 pmol CPA using the dried droplet method with various matrices. CPA signal alone was observed in the macrocrystalline ATT needles, about 4-fold above background. CPA and CPA-PCI complex (5% as strong as CPA alone) gave good signals with sinapic acid. Signals were barely detectable with HABA, with CPA-PCI about one-third to one-half of the CPA signal. The CPA-PCI complex actually had as stronger or even stronger a signal than CPA alone, although both these were weakly above background. With CHCA, both signals are strong, with the PCI-CPA signal about one-third that of the CPA signal. With 2,5-DHB, the CPA signal was barely above the noise of background, although detectable.

Adjustments to laser fluence (energy per unit area) had no effect on fading. There was no first-shot behavior in this intensity fading process: relative intensity is reduced to the same extent after 200 laser shots compared to the first, with the exception of the ATT macrocrystalline areas. Switching from linear to reflectron configuration in positive ion mode does not influence fading.

BPTI has no affinity for CPA. In the results above, CPA-PCI interaction was clearly affected by a particular matrix in a particular buffer. PCI, CPA, and BPTI were mixed in 20 pmol equal amounts and spotted. The CPA-PCI complex was detectable only with sinapic acid (in ammonium dihydrogen phosphate). With ATT/ammonium acetate, a peak at m/z ~41,200 would calculate to be a BPTI-CPA complex, very nonspecific. There was no fading effect seen at all with either the PCI-CPA in SIN or BPTI-CPA in ATT. Strong PCI, and BPTI peaks alone were seen with SIN, and with ATT, there was a weak PCI peak, very strong BPTI peak, and even a [2BPTI]+ peak. Use of DHAP gave signals for CPA, BPTI, and PCI alone. With HABA, addtion of BPTI caused fading of the CPA signal. Use of time-delay extraction in linear and reflectron config (increasing from 120 - 500 ns) had no discernable effect.

The trypsin-BPTI interaction was also tested using the 7 matrices and 2 spotting techniques. In a 1:1 interaction, intact complex is detected with SIN, DHAP, CHCA, HABA (w/ and w/o ammonium salts), and high variabiliyt is seen with ATT and 2,5-DHB.

Trypsin is not bound by LCI and these were mixed 1:1 and spotted. LCI forms a nonspecific complex with trypsin in all matrices, even more so with DHAP. Important here though is the absence of signal fading, but this is true for BPTI and for LCI. When intensity fading MS is done in the sub-picomole range, the results show a contrast: BPTI will specifically fade when tryspin is added, while LCI and PCI signals are unaffected. But as with CPA-PCI, the BPTI-trypsin complex is not observable in its mass range, unless in the picomole range.

Why is the intensity fading effect not seen when picomolar amounts of protein-peptide interactions used? This may not be due effects of analytes on higher amounts of proteins, but rather the sensitivity of microchannel plate-type detectors. With each laser burst, lower mass ions present strike the MCP first and cause a saturation, reducing the count of higher molecular weight ions. This has been confirmed using superconducting tunneling junction (cryodetectors).

Conclusions.

  1. Detection of a true biomolecular interaction using MALDI MS cannot be determined using a single solution alone in which the predicted mass signal is found: if the interaction is true, there should be a intensity fading effect in the spectrum when one component is titrated against the other in several solutions. Multiple matrices may need to be tried.
  2. The best results obtained were from homogeneous microcrystalline layers obtained using dried droplet technique. No fading could be observed with titration in which laser was focused on macrocrystalline areas of ATT and 2,5-DHB or with thinlayers of CHCA, 2,6-DHB, and SIN.
  3. Protein-protein interaction did not seem to be dependent upon matrix pH for the proteins used in this study, although that may not always be the case.

Microwave-Assisted Protein Preparation and Enzymatic Digestion in Proteomics

The LCQ Deca XP ion trap MS from Thermo-Finnigan and Voyager-DE Pro MALDI ToF MS from ABi were used. LC parts from Upchurch. Columns are 5 µm 300 Å from Merck for C18, and for SCX from PolyLC. BioRad's MiniProtean II used for minigels. Microwave is a Whirlpool VIP271 with 850 W max power. All LC reagents are Merck, and trypsin, PMSF, and CHCA are Sigma.

BSA was suspended 1 mg in 1 ml 25 mM ammonium bicarbonate pH 8.5, divided into 200 µl aliquots and five digestion protocols done: (1) reduce in 10 mM DTT 57° 1 h, alkylate room temp in 55 mM iodoacetamide in dark for 30 min, digest 1:50 w/w in trypsin 37° overnight. (2) sample is placed in 0.6 ml conical tube, and reduce and alkylate as (1), and then microwaved (see below). (3) Processed like sample (2), except that no alkylation step is done. (4) Processed like sample (2), except the DTT reduction is done at 100° and for only 5 min and the sample is not alkylated. (5) This sample is like (2), but it is not reductively akylated at all. The microwave procedure involves placing 1000 ml of water next to the sample vials; this acts an an energy sink. The microwave is turned on at max power for 1 min. After irradiation, the vials are removed and processed for MS analysis. Trypsin is 0.5 µM and ammonium bicarbonate is 25 mM. For the in-gel digestion protocol, a standard in which 10 mM DTT for 1 h, 55 mM iodoacetamide 30 min, 30 min dehydration in 100% MeCN, 30 min rehydration in 0.5 µM trypsin and 25 mM ammonium bicarbonate, and then 16 h (overnight) is done. Peptide extraction is twice for 30 min in 5% formic acid with 50% MeCN (total time about 20 h). The microwave extraction takes only 25 min: the DTT/iodoacetamide steps are omitted, 5 min dehydration in 100% MeCN, 5 min rehydration in 0.5 µM trypsin and ammonium bicarbonate, 5 min digestion using microwave, and then twice for 5 min each the extraction reagents.

Results show that the MS spectrum of samples 1 and 2 were almost identical. The only difference is that sample 2 is done in 1% of the time! Samples 3 and 4 looked similar. 100 fmol from samples 1-4 were run on a single LC column to MS, and the number of identified proteins were similar. MS/MS sample identification for sample 5 was significantly reduced. With a 1 minute digestion protocol, the need for alkylation is unnecessary since disulfide bond reformation is unlikely within that period.

Ultrafast Coelectrophoretic Fluorescent Staining of Proteins with Carbocyanines

Protein staining in gels must balance considerations such as recovery of any fixed proteins or alterations by staining that affect compatibility with MS analysis. Fluorescent dyes such as Sypro Ruby and Deep Purple do not always show compatibility with MS as well. Zinc and imidazole staining have severe problems with linearity although sensitivity is not an issue. Nile Red staining is rapid and requires no fixation but the stain is photolabile (presents difficulty in extended imaging and excision) and there is a sensitivity limitation. Sypro tangerine needs only a single bath in saline but there is a cost and limited sensitivity issue.

In fluorescent detection, a covalent labeling strategy can be used as with the use of fluorescamine and MDPF; there are improved techniques in a recent report on multiplex visualization. Sensitivity is a problem since there are limited sites.

A second approach is the noncovalent association of the fluorophore which alters its excitation/emission properties. Ruthenium complexes and epinococconone are examples.

Another approach is when the fluorophore is affected not by a direct association but depends on the environment in which it is. Nile Red and the SYPRO family of dyes depend on this. The SYPRO dyes are not really as sensitive as claimed and cost substantially more. Nile Red has more sensitivity but very limited solubility and severe photolability.

The ideal fluorescent probe should combine high absorptivity, an important differential quantum yield between hydrophilic and lipophilic, and still be water-soluble. It should also show photostability.

Carbocyanines show these characteristics, but staining of separated proteins gave no promise. The infusion of dye into a gel for co-electrophoresis has been described for CBB and so it was tried here. Use of water or water-alcohol-acid to provide contrast after electrophoresis. The acid presence causes an intense background to develop in the low MW region because of the presence of carrier ampholytes. Lowering SDS concentration works a previous report but results in severe tailing of the 2D spots.

The carbocyanine structure has a nucleus and can have long arm side chains. The side chain can help to increase interaction with lipophilic environment and thus effect a strong fluorescence, while there is a disadvantage in that it can induce self-aggregation (and increase the background fluorescence thereby) as well as reduce solubility.

200 µg protein from J774 cell extracts were separated in a pH 4-8 gradient/Tris-taurine 2DE system and stained with different carbocyanine dyes (oxacarbocyanine with diethyl, dipentyl, diheptyl, and dioctadecyl lengths). The diethyl side chains are too short and lack sensitivity, while the dioctadecyl is insoluble and cannot reach optimal concentrations. A 3 µM concentration was found to be best: lowering it decreased sensitivity while increasing it did not enhance staining.

Fragmentation of protonated dipeptides containing arginine: Effect of activation method

Collisional activation in quadrupole ion traps is a very slow activation with activation times approaches fractions of a second and low energy transfer to reactant ions, and with similar characteristics to sustained off-resonance irradiation (SORI) used in FT-ICR. This provides for a great deal of rearrangement to occur before fragmentation actually occurs. Rearrangments have low activation energies but also have unfavorable entropies of activation. Fragmentation reactions however have higher activation energies and are more favorable entropically. Collisional activation of CID type in quadrupole cells (in triple quad and hybrids) are of the beam-type. Ubquitin gives more fragments in collision cell CID than in QIT CID. Farrugia and O'Hair reported that singly protonated dipeptides Arg-Gly and Gly-Arg give identical spectra with QIT CID: they proposed a mechanism in which there is time for rearrangments to form a common intermediate which is a mixed anhydride, NH2CH2C(=O)-O-C(=O)-CH(NH2)-(CH2)3NH-C(=NH2+)NH2, with intermediate salt bridge structures. In MS3 studies fragment ions from two peptides gave identical mass spectra. Gronert and coworkers have reported QIT with alkali metal complexes of dipeptides can be rationalized as formation of cationated mixed anhydride. Other Gly-basic amino acid dipeptides show the same reaction (Gly-Xxx, where Xxx = Lys or His), although there were different relative abundances. Fragmentation of [M+H]+ forms of Gly-Arg and Arg-Gly, in addition to fragmentation of their fragments, using beam-type activation and cone-voltage CID or collisional activation in a QqTOF instrument were studied. Cone voltage CID is where CID is between the atmospheric pressure source and the quadrupole mass analyzer.

Peptides are dissolved in 50% MeCN + 0.5% formic acid (1:1 MeCN:1% HCOOH). The spray is introduced at 30 µl/min. Nitrogen is used for nebulization and desolvation. For the hybrid QqTOF the solution is 50% MeOH + 0.05% HCOOH and flowed at 80 µl/min; N2 is used at nebulizing, desolvation, and collision gas (in collsion cell). MS2 and MS3 were done with Bruker Esquire 3000+ QIT, with 1 mM solution in 50% MeOH at 10 µl/min. Precursors are isolate in 1 Th window and a resonant excitation pulse (Vac) of 10 ms duration is used. With MS2 precursors are positioned at qz = 0.25 (low mass cutoff ~ m/z 60) and optimum frag efficiency with Vac = 0.75 V. A higher Vac = 2.25 V gives more energetic dissociation. A larger trapping potential qz = 0.38 was used to obtain good trapping efficiences, and increasing low mass cutoff to m/z 93. The data will reflect three different type of CID: cone voltage CID, Q-ToF CID, and QIT-CID.

Data for Gly-Arg and Arg-Gly shows that CV-CID and QTOF-CID produce more fragments, although in low intensity (abundance). A comparison of the CV-CID spectra for Gly-Arg and Arg-Gly shows very different fragment ion spectral intensities for several species. This is in contrast to QIT-CID spectra. There are also difference for the QTOF-CID fragment spectra too. From the masses, elemental compositions are determined mathematically. In contrast, spectra from QIT-CID for Arg-Gly and Gly-Arg under conditions of 0.75 V peak-to-peak excitation voltage and qz = 0.249 with 10 ms excitation time shows identical spectra. This confirms that rearrangements occur to a common intermediate just before fragmenation ensues. However, when the excitation voltage is increased, requiring an increase in the low mass cutoff and the inability to observe certain ions, there is fragmentation spectra with more ion species.

What about low excitation in beam-type collisions. MH+ ions in a QqTOF were set at 12 eV collision energy, near fragmentation threshold, and spectra normalized to m/z 215 [M + H - NH3]+. At low CE values, fragmenation products are [M + H - NH3]+, [M + H - 2 NH3]+ (m/z 198), [M + H - guanidine]+ (m/z 173) and a product at m/z 172. This products also occur with protonated Arg and Arg-containing peptides, but do not help in supporting an isomerization of MH+. For Gly-Arg, products are [M + H - H2O]+ (m/z 214), [M + H - NH 3 - H2]+ (m/z 197), ArgH+( m/z 175), and [M + H - glycinamide]+ (m/z 158). For Arg-Gly, the dominant signal is m/z 157, the Arg b1 ion. The a1-y1 mechanism for amide bond cleavage without prior rearrangement explains the path to product. For Gly-Arg, the y1 ion is formed, explaining the m/z 175 product, and for Arg-Gly, it si the m/z 157 ion. These findings show that even for low energy beam-type collisions no rearrangement has occurred. The Gly-Arg product ions in beam-type can be observed in CID-QIT if the excitation energy is set high.

For the Arg-Gly spectrum, the low energy collision produces m/z 157, a b1 ion which is Arg itself. α-aminoacylium ions lose a neutral CO as they cannot form stable oxazolone structures. If there is nucleophile on side chain, cyclization using side chain might occur. This is true for b1 ions derived from Lys, His, Met, and Arg.

Gly-Arg fragments are numerous in product species, but the Arg-Gly shows domination by a m/z [C4H8N]+ species. Common fragmentation route for the GlyArg and ArgGly is the product protonated guanidine (m/z 60). This also forms in fragmentation of ArgH+. In addition the charge can transfer to the complement fragment instead of guanidine to produce m/z 173 ion. Protonated Arg also undergoes neutral losses of NH3 to form a m/z 158 ion, loss of guanidine (m/z 116), and then to proline imimium wit m/z 70.

With the m/z 215 [M + H - NH3]+ in the QqTOF fragmentation is isomer-dependent. A scheme was produced that relies on accurate mass of product ions and on what is known about neutral losses. Six paths are described, with moiety lost shown and the product ion m/z:

  1. -NH3, m/z 198 → -(H2O + CO) m/z 152
  2. -HN=C=NH, m/z 173
  3. -HN=C=O, m/z 172
  4. -(CH2=NH) + CO, m/z 158 → -(H2O + CO) m/z 112
  5. -C2H5NO (CH3C(=O)-NH2?), m/z 156
  6. -C5H9NO2, m/z 100

The initial NH3 to form the m/z 215 likely comes from the guanidino group, as shown for ArgH+ fragmentation. Elimination of a neutral loss being HN=C=O (isocyanic acid?) to form the m/z 172 occurs in the QIT-CID. For Gly-Arg, we see loss of CH2N2 probably carbodiimide. This dipeptide also loses methyleneimine (CH2=NH) and CO for the m/z 158 ion. And Gly-Arg produces the m/z 100 ion, which might be the fragment [H2N-CH2-C(=O)-NH=C=NH]+ + proline, formed from the m/z 215 which is where the Arg side chain has cyclized with the α-amino-N of Arg. It should be noted that dipeptides of the type Xxx-Arg do involve the neutral loss of a 115 Da size, which would correspond to proline. This is not the case for m/z 215 ion derived from Arg-Xxx dipeptides. Note that the m/z 156 ion comes directly from m/z 215 ion and also from m/z 173 ion, the latter de-guanidinated. Since they give rise to two distinct structures and the neutral losses do not have the same moleculear formula, the m/z 156 ion is probably two peak signals that cannot be resolved from one another in the QqTOF. With QIT-CID, m/z 173 fragmentation from the Gly-Arg shows m/z 156 as dominant (ammonia loss) and also m/z 70, while QIT-CID for m/z 173 from Arg-Gly showed also these two ions with same intensity but also a minor m/z 172 ion, probably a loss of H from another isomeric intermediate. Salivary Proteomic and Genomic Biomarkers for Primary Sjögren's Syndrome

Sjögren's syndrome (SS) is chronic autoimmune disorder described in 1933, characterized by dry mouth (xerostomia) and dry eyes (keratoconjunctivitis sicca). The disease occurs in women primarily (9:1 over men) and affects up to 4 million Americans (~1 in 100).

MS/MS of Protonated Polyproline Peptides: The Influence of N-terminal Protonation on Dissociation

Polyproline forms two possible helical structures (not the traditional α-helix) called ProI and ProII. ProI is right-handed in solutin with 3.3 residues per turn and peptide bonds are cis in orientation. ProII is left-handed with 3.0 residues per turn and all peptide bonds trans. Helix type depends on solvent. Low protic solvents (butanol, propanol) favor ProI, and ProII is usually in more protic solvents (water). If polyPro is put into gas phase from propanol, ProI helix features persist, as shown for different lengths of the chain. ESI however from water did not show ProII helix type features persisting, suggesting helix is not stabilized without solvation. One feature of interest regardless of solvent is H-bonding between protonated N-terminus and 2nd proline. This bonding cannot occur for internal Pro residues as they lack amide N hydrogens. Molecular modeling with (Pro2+H)+ shows the lowest energy structure has hydrogens on the N-terminal N atom and H-bonding with carbonyl O atoms on 1st and 2nd residue. In peptides, normally y ion abundance is significant because cleavage occurs at the N-terminal side of Pro; proton affinity for proline and the strained structure of the b-type ion have been proposed as reasons for selective formation of y ions.

Experiments were done using Bruker Esquire 3000plus Quad ion-trap MS with positive ion mode ESI. Helium is collision gas. MSn is done with CID, using 2.0 Da isolation window and qz ~ 0.25 for parent ion. Activation time is 40 ms. Amplitude energy optimized to minimize parent ion intensity, maximize product ion, and minimize ion ejection (this value usually 0.50 V for this instrument). Double resonance is done with modified Esquire LC QIT. Stored waveform inverse fourier transform (SWIFT) is used to eject product suspected fo being reaction intermediate. Pron-nh2 (n = 4-10) peptides and ProAlaPro8-nh2 peptides are synthesized with Fmoc on Rink amide MBHA resin (successful to get C-terminal Pro). Other peptides are from AnaSpec. Solvent is 50% MeCN + 1% acetic acid.

The CID spectrum for [Pro8-nh2 + H]+ is exclusively of y ions, although there are differences in abundances. The y4 and y6 ions are major, resulting from loss of even numbers of proline residues. The sodiated ion spectrum [Pro8-nh2 + Na]+ is altogether different, showing y ions of type [yn-H+Na]+ where n=3-7. Sodiated y4 ion is not very intense, while protonated y4 is the most intense. Also there is no y7 ion in the protonated peptide, but there is in the sodiated (cleavage between residues 1 and 2). Sequential loss from C-terminal is also not observed since the COOH is amidated (blocked); with unmodified polyPor cationized with Na, rearrangements were observed such that a peptide one shorter than the parent is seen. A plot of the relative intensities of yn-1, yn-2, yn-3, and yn-4 ions against the number of Pro residues n (where n = 4-10) in a peptide reveals that the base peak for n = 4-7 is the yn-2 ion, and when n = 8-10, the base peak is the yn-4 ion. yn-1 and yn-3 ion intensities increase as n decreases, although not reaching base peak intensities. The plot of protonated polyPro and sodiated is similar: a Gaussian shape centered at yn-2 and yn-3 ion is seen.

The second position of Pro10-nh2 was substituted with Ala to see if pairs of residues in protonated even-number loss required the residue be Pro. The most intense ions are y8, y6, and y4, with low intensity peaks at y3, y7, and b9. The data indicate that for a peptide n residues long, yn-2 ion forms even if cleavage is Xxx-Pro where Xxx does not have to be Pro.

What role does the proton play in favoring dipeptide fragmentation? Arg was placed at the N-terminus as it is well known to sequester mobile protons. ArgPro9-nh2. Most intense product ion is a water loss. There are y type ions as with other peptides, the most abundant of these being y5. Intensities of y8, y6, and y4 are not more significant than y7, y5 and y3 ions. ArgPro4-nh2 shows formation of y4, y3, and y2 in approx equal intensity; the base peak is [M+H-H2O]+. The proton can not be sequestered on a basic residue for formation of yn-2 and yn-4 ions to be major fragmentation pathway. Without the strongly basic residue, a proton cannot be held. The most basic site would be the N-terminus. This is so for polyPro, since protons H-bond with 1st and 2nd residue carbonyl O atoms. With collisional activation, the proton would move to other sites, usually amide N atoms in preparation for fragmentation. Acetyl-Pro10 CID spectra show an entire series of y ions, with y9, y7, and y5 being most abundant. The intensities are not distributed symmetrically; instead from y9 to y4, the intensities alternate between high and low. MS3 on y9 ion shows losses of even numbers of Pro residues, and y5 and y7 formation exists through a y9 intermediate. This y9 ion is analogous to [Pro9-nh2 + H]+ although C-termini are different. Identities of product ions in MS3 of [AcPro10 + H]+ → [y9]+ → products is same as MS/MS of [Pro9-nh2 + H]+, but relative intensities are different between y7/y5 (yn-2/yn-4). The y7 ion is base peak in the MS3 spectrum while y5 is base peak in MS/MS spectrum. Thus yn-2 and yn-4 ions are formed even if dissociating ion is an intermediate which came from parent with blocked N-terminus.

For polyPron for n > 6 we see yn-2 and yn-4 ions. A question remains whether yn-2 is a precursor to yn-4. Applying double resonance, the y5 is forcefully ejected to eliminate it being a precursor (i.e, immediately when product is formed). This eliminates the possibility that y3 will formed from it if it is formed from y7 directly. If y3 abundance is altered, that means that some or all of it is produced by y5.

Mass Spectrometry-Based Metabolomics

REVIEW. The technology of genomics, proteomics, and transcriptomics have made significant strides but tools for comprehensive examination of the metabolome are still emerging (Bingo et al, 2004). Metabolomics is closest to the phenotype. No single instrument can analyze all metabolites. Bibliographic searches shows that metabolomics is fairly new, with 147 articles having keywords in 2003, and 203 in 2004. Beecher (2003) estimates that there are 2000 major metabolites in human, quantity and quality dependent on dietary state as much as other factors. When secondary metabolites considered, the number is vastly larger. Weckwerth (2003) figures that there are 200,000.

Two complementary approaches are used in metabolomic investigations. Metabolic profiling focuses on analysis of a group of metabolites either related to a specific metabolic pathway or a class of compounds. Examples include quantitative analysis of fatty acids and their methyl esters by GC-FID or analysis of amino acids. In most cases profiling is hypothesis-driven: metabolites are selected for analysis and specific analytical methods developed for their determination. Advances in technology have enabled the ability to quantitate metabolites simultaneously in a single analysis. While a single biomarker determination can be as complex as profiling related key metabolites in a given biochemical pathway, the latter will provide a more complete, detailed description of metabolic perturbations than a single biomarker. One disadvantage of profiling is that it is not global or a true omics approach. But when multiple profiling methods are used on the same condition or sample and data combined, the results will make metabolomics quite powerful. Metabolic fingerprinting is not about a rigorous systematic identification of metabolites as profiling is, but is involved with comparisons of patterns in response to changes in conditions or treatments, such as the occurrence of disease or exposure to toxin. The workflow for fingerprinting is pretty much standard: collect samples from the group of interest and another group representing the proper control, prepare samples by extraction (LLE, SPE, etc) and include internal standards, perform the instrumental analysis usually with derivatization (GC-MS, LC-MS, CE-MS), process the data typically by multivariate analysis (e.g. PCA) and perhaps produce score plots for groups and loading plots for markers, and identify markers using libraries, MS/MS, NMR, etc. When the pattern analysis of metabolites is done on conditioned cell culture medium, the method is called metabolic footprinting. Fingerprinting can be used as diagnostic tool; it can be used to establish a treatment baseline and look for altered patterns.

Both fingerprinting and profiling can be used to discover new biomarkers.

Insofar as instruments, NMR, FT-IR, and MS coupled to separation techniques using direct flow injection can be employed. NMR is insufficiently sensitive for low abundance metabolites although it can used for high-throughput fingerprinting. MS-based metabolomics offers quantitative with high selectivity and sensitivity with respect to identification. Separation allows resolution of metabolites (in a time dimension). MS work requires sample prep which can result in metabolite loss. In general parallel application of several techniques is done (GC- and LC-MS) in order to get a comprehensive assessment of the metabolome. MS-based studies far outweight NMR-based work.

Sampling & Quenching. An analysis of statistical power should be done to ensure that sufficient samples in the same analytical group and to reduce biological variability for obtaining validated data. For human studies, confounding factors include diet, gender, age, genetic factors. Fingerprinting requires large sample numbers. Representative quality control samples such as sample and analytical replicates and bland including method blanks are to be analyzed. To thwart continuing metabolic processes or degradative techniques in sample containers, freezing by liquid nitrogen or freeze clamping, acid treatment, or quenching in salt with aqueous MeOH at low temperature should be considered. Cold-buffered MeOH kept at −40° to −50° has been used to analyze microbial forms (E. coli, S. cerevisiae, A. niger). Note that addition of buffer salts (like HEPES) might cause ion suppression in ESI-MS. There might be a great deal of data variability when times and methods vary with respect to biofluid sampling and low temperature treatment.

Sample Preparation. The preparative phase will extract the target analytes of interest from a complex biological matrix in an attempt to eliminate or limit interference with analysis. In the case of low abundance metabolites, a preconcentration step will bring sample to sufficient to reach detection limits. Sample prep methods include but not limited to direct injection, liquid-liquid extraction, solid-phase extraction, supercritical fluid extraction, accelerated solvent extraction, microwave-assited extraction, protein precipitation, and membrane methods (dialysis, ultrafiltration, diafiltration), and ultracentrifugation.

Volatile organic compounds (VOCs) are often found in the urine, plasma, and exhaled air metabolome. Classes include alcohols, aldehydes, furans, ketones, pyrroles, terpenes, and others. In GC separations, extraction solvents can often interfere/overlap with important analyte peaks, or reducing their range of quantitation; analytes are cut off at the solvent delay in GC-MS. Headspace sampling with gastight syringe in combination with cold trapping in temp-controlled cold injection system and GC-MS for analysis of volatile metabolites in urine samples. 34 compounds were found in MS libraries and reference compounds. Another technique that is rapid and uses no solvent is the headspace-solid phase microextraction (HS-SPME), which uses a silica fiber coated with stationary phase for sampling. The fiber is housed in syringe-type assembly, and several different stationary phases made of non-polar to polar materials are available as fiber coating. Analyte distribution between sample and gas phase is affected by analyte-matrix interactions. Desorption of analytes from fiber achieved by thermal energy of hot injector of GC or by solvent for coupling with HPLC.

SPE is a go-to method which provides a non-equilibrium exhaustive removal from the mobile phase/matrix. Compounds are retained by van der Waals interactions, dipole-dipole interactions, H-bonding and electrostatic forces, depending on analytes and sorbent. Insofar as sorbents, silica, alkylated silica (e.g. C18), carbon-based sorbents, ion-exchange media, polymeric materials, and restricted access materials (RAM) will remove macromolecules. C18 reversed phase silica will not retain polar analytes and show a stability at high pH, and analyte losses occur when the sorbent runs dry after equilibration step: these cautions should be noted. As alternatives to silica-based sorbents, funtionalized polymeric resins with polar functional groups on a nonpolar polymeric backbone give good retention of both polar and hydrophobic analytes. They have a wide pH range resistance and if run dry, will be less prone to analyte loss. Wang et al (2003) used C18 SPE to remove salts from serum after ultrafiltration on a 5000 Da cutoff filter. The Oasis HLB system is a functionalized copolymer of divinylbenzene and N-vinylpyrrolidone, and was used to extract urine metabolities for LC-ESI-MS for screening.

Mixed phases are also being used for SPE sorbents. Polymeric phases contain strong and weak cation- and anion-exchange material to bind ionic metabolites simultaneously. Metabolites are recovered in organic solvents, which gets neutrals and protonated acids, and then a second pH adjustment recovers basic compounds.

Liquid-liquid extraction. It is a choice method in tissue extraction. Tissues are frozen in liquid nitrogen, ground into fine powder with mortar and pestle, mortar grinders, vibration mills, or by mixing with beads of inert material, or by homogenizaiton of material in blenders with a solvent. High or ultra-speed supernatants are then shaken with organic solvents. Polar metabolites are extract with 2-propanol, ethanol, methanol, acidic MeOH, MeCN, water, or MeOH:water mixes. Any solvent can be mixed. More lipophilic metabolites are extracted with chloroform or ethyl acetate. Water:MeOH:CHCl3 or ethyl acetate extractions generate an upper organic phase, which is rotary evaporated and resuspended for derivatization or direct analysis. In one experiment with plant tissue metabolites were extracted with vibration mill with chloroform (200 µl) followed by addition of 800 µl MeOH/water (75:25) to form a monophase. Weckwerth, Wenzel, and Fiehn (2004) developed a multiple solvent extraction for sequential extraction of metabolites, proteins, and RNA from Arabidopsis tissue. Other extraction protocols have included high temperature extraction with EtOH or MeOH, MeCN/water, 0.2% formic acid (50:50), acid or alkaline treatment, cold MeOH, acidic MeOH-CH2Cl2-ethyl acetate. In a systematic comparison of extraction reagents, one group tested at least six or more systems and found that pure methanol (not hot) was the most suitable for extracting polar intracellular yeast metabolites and perchloric acid was the least suitable.

Direct injection for fingerprinting of urine on to LC-ESI-MS is reported. No metabolites are list, but high salt in urine causes ion suppression and adduct formation, and nonvolatile residues deteriorate performance. Nanoelectrospray minimizes ion suppression and decreases source contamination. It is also useful to divert parts of the run, such as the start of a run where salts are highly likely, away from the ESI interface to reduce likelihood of contamination. When protein content is high, metabolite separation can be poor and column backpressure might increase.

Metabolic profiling in metabolic disorders. Inborn errors of metabolism can be assessed by looking at urinary metabolome, especially using ESI-MS/MS. Bloodspots dried on filter paper can be extracted with MeOH, with addition of stable isotope-labeled internal standards, evaporation of solvent, derivatization in acidifed butanol. The butyl esters are analyzed using FIA with ESI(+)-MS/MS with neutral loss of butyl formate (102 amu) for detecting neutral amino acids, neutral loss of 119 amu for ornithine and citrulline, and loss of m/z 161, for Arg. Acylcarnitines are measured using precursor scan of m/z 85. Piraud et al (2003) developed a method based on LC-ESI-MS/MS for analyzing 79 small molecules, mainly amino acids related to metabolic disorders; underivatized amino acids and related analytes were the objective for examination in a single run. Interferences with isobars and in-source fragmentation made the use of LC separation necessary.

Lipidomics. Non-polar lipids have been routinely analyzed using GC separation coupled to EI-MS. Lipids can be methylated or silylated to expand the range of analyzable molecules. Of course this is limited to thermally stabile substances which can be volatilized at injection. Triglycerides are amenable to high temp GC work, but complex lipids (phospholipids, etc) require pre-sep of lipid classes, then hydrolysis, FFA derivatization, then GC-EI-MS or GC-FID. The method is very quantitative but labor intensive and structural information is lost.

Besides hot split/splitless injection, analytical pyrolysis (Py) is used in which nonvolatile sample is thermally degraded in inert gas prior to GC injection. Snyder et al (1990) used Py-GC-IT/MS to differentiate bacterial species based on lipid profiles. Basile et al (1998) used in situ thermally assisted hydrolysis and methylation (THM) to differentiate bacteria. Py is coupled to GC-MS or for direct inlet to TQ/MS. Quaternary N-alkylammonium salts (e.g., tetramethylammonium hydroxide [TMAH]) form tetramethylammonium carboxylates with organic acids and methylation takes place upon heating. Ester-bound fatty acids are saponified and trans-methylated, but unsaturated fatty acids can undergo isomerization and recoveries are incomplete.

Solid-state ion sources (such as FAB) and liquid-phase ion sources such as ESI or APcI allowed direct lipid analysis. A lipidome analysis was developed that is fully automated method making use of LC-ESI-MS quantitative for 100 polar lipids (primarily phospholipids). The shotgun lipidomics approach uses crude tissue extracts obtained by Folch or Bligh-Dyer extraction methods. Using both positive and negative mode ESI sample is directly injected. In ESI(+) mode acylcarnitines, phosphatidylcholine, lysophosphatidylcholine, phosphatidylethanolamine, and sphingomyelins. Free fatty acids, phosphatidic acid, phosphatidylserine, phosphatidylinositol, phosphatidylglycerol, and phosphatidylethanolamine are ionized in ESI(-) mode. ESI does not ionize triacylglycerols/neutral lipids; they can be detected as NH+, Na+, and Li+ adducts in ESI(+) mode. In MS/MS instruments phospholipids with their head groups show characteristic CID spectra allowing identification. For sphingolipid analysis (sphingolipidomics) neutral loss and precursor-ion scans are used to identify lipids in samples as well as assess masses. In ESI(+) precursor ion scans for m/z 184 ([C5H15NPO4]+) or neutral loss scans for 59 amu (N(CH3)3) will identify [M+Na]+ and [M+H]+ ions corresponding to PtdChol and lyso-PtdChol. Precursor-ion scans for m/z 164 ([NaHO3POC2H4NH3]+) can bue used to identify [M+Na]+ and [M+H]+ ions of PtdEtOHNH3+. In ESI(-) neutral loss scans are done for 153 amu ([HO3POCH2C(OH)CH2] for identifying precursors of Ptd-glycerol. The peak m/z 184 is characteristic of sphingomyelin and ions m/z 264 and 266 are used for ceramide identification in precursor scans.

Regio-chemistry of phospholipids is based on intensity of carboxylate anions originating from sn-1 and sn-2 psoitions: intensity of fragment ion derived from sn-2 acyl group is greater than product ion from sn-1. Branched saccharides of higher order sphingolipids (e.g., gangliosides) require MS/MS/MS experiments to look at structure. Some class of lipids can be analyzed in one go: automated detection of over 800 phospholipid species by direct injection ESI-MS/MS of cell extracts has been reported using a lipid array (semi-quantitative data output) similar to DNA microarrays (see Forrester et al, 2004). Balazy (2004) recently reported on eicosanomics, the profiling of eicosanoids; these derives from arachidonic acid usiing enzymes like cyclooxygenase, lipoxygenases, cytochrome P450s, and oxidations not enzymatic. LC or GC separation is done to separate structural isomers, then MS-analyzed. Several groups have reported LC-ESI(-)-MS/MS studies (Margalit et al 1996, Newman et al 2002, Schmelzer et al 2005).

Metabolic Fingerprinting. Fingerprints are described by their data, which includes mass spectra data (m/z and intensities) and by retention times when a chromatographic step is included. These data re then subjected to sample classification using multivariate data analysis. In many cases the metabolites are not identified (structures not known). Fingerprinting, unlike profiling, refers to an unbiased analysis of the entire detectable metabolome even if semi-quantitative data are generated.

Direct injection. This kind of fingerprinting involves no prior separation by chromatography. Samples are injected with ESI or APcI, is high throughput, and samples analyzed in a few minutes. Short analysis time permites intersample reproducibility and accuracy of the cluster analysis. Smedsgaard et al (1996, 1997) examined fungal extracts using ESI(+)-single quad MS. Castrillo et al (2003) looked at intracellular yeast extracts by ESI(+)-TQ MS. Higher resolution TOF and FTICR (rez = 6000-17,000 with 0.003-0.005 amu mass accuracy). Q-ToF MS has been used for screening analysis of cell or plant extracts (see Vaidyanathan et al 2002., Scholz et al 2004). FT-ICR-MS has resolution up to 105-106 and mass accuracy down to 0.0001-0.001 amu, detection limits to femtomole to attomole range, and MSn capabilities. Aharoni et al (2002) infused directly crude ripening strawberry fruit extracts (MeOH:water 50:50 + 0.1% formic acid, or 100% MeCN) using ESI(+), ESI(-), and APCI(+) and APCI(-). A total of 5844 unique 12C z=1 ions screened. Hirai et al 2004 have used FTICRMS for fingerprinting Arabidopsis to understand plant response to nutritional stress. Injections for ESI are done by continuous flow or by loop injection of 10-20 µl sample at 5-50 µl/min flow. NanoESI has also been used and systems like Nanomate (Advion Biosciences) are devised with liquid sampler and ESI chip of array of nanoelectrospray nozzles (Scholz et al 2004). Drawbacks: Isobars may not be distinguished for chemical isomers (need chromatography). Also products might be in-source fragments or adduct forms. With ESI ion suppression is the big problem. With many crude extracts, both signal suppression or enhancement can occur.

LC-MS. Use of LC removes elements that cause ion suppression resulting from coeluting compounds, isobaric interferences in case of low resolving mass analyzers, and they usually separate isomers. C18 reversed phase narrow bore columns with 3-5 µm particles usually used in metabolomics. Use of monolithic silica-based C18 capillary used in plant metabolomics (Tolstikov et al 2003). Many samples require better separation than can be achieved. Smaller particle size (1.7 µm) used in UPLC are now possible. Sub-2 µm particles are very highly resolving and improve detection. A 2.1 × 100 mm, 1.7 µm C18 colum with gradient elution provides peak widths on order of 1.8 s (base width) in mouse urine, resolving 250 peaks in a 10 min period (Wilson et al 2005a, b). Pressures of 10-15 kpsi are needed to provide this separation. RPLC provides separation of medium polar and nonpolar substances, whereas polar elute with void. Tolstikov and Fiehn (2002) used HILIC for highly polar compounds. HILIC is orthogonal to RPLC and uses silica or derivatized silica, with groups being amino, diol, amide, polysulfoethyl aspartamide, or polyhydroxyethyl aspartamide with low-aqueous/high organic solvent systems. Similar to normal phase HILIC requires much water in mobile phase where polar analytes are partitioned and retained. In contrast to normal phase, HILIC uses water-miscible polar organic solvents such as MeCN or MeOH. Naidong (2003) has reviewed HILIC in bioanalytical LC-MS/MS. Triple quad or QToF enable MS/MS work for structural breakdown of biomarkers. Accurate mass measurement of precursor and product ions. Ion traps allow MSn work. MS/MS can be used to probe metabolome for compound classes, characteristic ions and neutral losses, for example CO2, 44 amu neutral loss in negative mode CID indicates R-COOH.

In doing LC-ESI-MS, both positive and negative ion mode should be done for broad coverage. Mass analysis can be done in a single run with positive and negative mode detected simultaneously, but it depends on mass analyzer used (Idborg-Bjorkman et al 2003). This eliminates time of analysis and any variability due to injection errors. LC-MS fingerprinting has been done for toxicity in urine (Idborg-Bjorkman et al 2003, Lafaye et al 2003, Lenz et al 2004a,b; Williams et al 2005), genetic studies (Plumb et al 2003a), plasma samples (Wang et al 2003), plant extracts (Tolstikov & Fiehn 2002, Tolstikov et al 2003).

MS data adds 4th dim. New quadrupoles have scan speeds of 10,000 amu/sec and are less expensive alternative for detection.

Also used nucleotides and CoA compounds, which provide comprehensive coverage. Their MS was a single quad with scan range of 30 amu, which maximizes signal for low abundance metabolites. The capillary is packed with a stationary (often monolithic) phase. The mobile phase flow is caused by EOF, having plug-like flow usually in CE (not parabolic), with highly resolved narrow peaks. There is alos a pressure-assisted form of CEC, and CEC is interfaced to MS (see Klampfl 2004). Use of CEC in protein, peptide, amino acid and saccharide analysis has been done.

Flux Analysis. When measuring metabolite concentrations, what is measured is the steady state levels or snapshots of these concentrations. These may not vary over time, even though the flux might change dramatically (flux is the rate of reactant-to-product formation). Thus steady state concentration measurement does not show underlying biological processes and describe phenotype (see Fig 1). Phenomics is the phenotypic analysis of genomic information or entire mutant collections with goal of understanding relationship between genes and higher levels of organization in the cell (Sauer 2004), and metabolism-wide flux measurements are a basis of this. Flux analysis is done using isotope-labeled precursors, which were actually used to determine biochemical pathways initially. Accelerator MS (AMS) is used to quantify measurements in attomole range and monitor very low amounts of 14C and other isotopes. The in vivo flux of human folate (Lin et al 2004) and beta carotene (Dueker et al 2000, Lemke et al 2003) have used AMS. Buchholz et al (1999) have used HPLC-AMS to follow xenobiotics such as atrazine. AMS odes not elucidate structures of unknowns, but something like chromatographic coeolution of labeled compounds with reference standard can be used. AMS is more suited to looking at flux in specific pathway than for global flux analysis. The setup is costly, and it is better to send to a service or collaborate.

Use of 13C or other stable isotopes can be detected with usual NMR or MS, but their sensitivity is far less than AMS. They provide structural information about labeled atom positions (positional isotopomers) and also the number of labeled atoms (mass isotopomers). MS provides better sensitivity and higher throughput (Wittman 2002, Sauer 2004), and it detects all isotopic forms. Special data analysis methods are required to interpret data. Wittman 2002 has provided overview of flux analysis in MS. GC-EI-MS is most often used since fragmentation spectra provide isotopomer distribution information and looks at metabolic fluxes. Raw data usually stored as time-based MS spectra. Continuous signal MS data are processed to centroided forms to reduce spectral complexity before peak identification. Software handles both ion chromatograms and scanned (full spectra) time points. Data extraction starts with transforming MS spectra into vectors of uniform length. Binning of data is similar to that used in NMR metabolomics (Holmes et al 2001, Viant 2003): it is compatible to nominmal mass resolution as no information is long and bin count is kept below 2000 with bin size = 1 amu (rounding masses to integer values), with insertion of missing mass values and zero intensity assignment. Binning of hi-rez MS data is disadvantageous because small drifts in mass measurement can occur: if accurate mass of metabolite is close to a bin cutoff, a centroided peak from different data files may occur in different bins by binning algorithm. Noisy data and false positives without sample randomization and where instrument drift is a big problem. Continual external calibration controls drift and enables instantaneous correction. Drift can be circumvented by increasing bin size can be increased to nominal resolution which sacrifices high mass resolution. Binning sizes too low produce huge data sets although few bins have information; signal splitting between bins is also likely.

Metabolites usually have C, H, N, O, and some S, and P atoms. H and N atoms increase the decimal part while O, S, and P decrease it (their fractional masses are on one side of the 0.500 line). O- and P-rich metabolites (like fructose 1,6-bisphosphate, C6H14O12P2) should have a mass slightly less than nominal integer mass value. Being small molecules metabolite accurate masses will rarely exceed 0.4 amu in the decimal portion for those majority of metabolites with masses below 500 amu. Except for heavy lipids, the optimal low resolution binning algorithm would be [mnom − 0.1 amu, mnom + 0.4 amu] and [mnom + 0.4 amu, mnom + 0.9 amu]. In one binning example using MarkerLynx (Micromass) to obtain metabolic fingerprints of cell culture media in m/z 100-1200 range, 1008 metabolite accurate masses were obtained to show a good histogram. Scholz et al (2004) have dsecribed approach to build a density function of masses weight by intensities by combining all acquired spectra. Masses at high intensity density are determined as maximum of density function. Mass intensity vectors transformed to uniform length means entire dataset obtained by direct inject (single scan) analysis is represented as 2D m × f matrix, where m is length of uniform mass vector and f is number of files. When a separation step is included, multiple scans are added to the dimension, so it becomes m × s × f, where s is number of scans or retention time.

These approaches are good for sample classificatin or discovery of abundant metabolite peaks. In other software a composite gel is made as a representation of detected spots rather than pixel-wise averaging. No background correction is done in computing the average gel.

2. Denoise the average gel using wavelet shrinkage

Wavelet denoising is used to remove white noise from the image; it has been routinely used in signal processing. This smoothes out small irregularities but retains larger signals that represent true spots. Removal of the irregularites reduces the count of computationally detected false positive spots.

The undecimated discrete wavelet transform (UDWT) is used, as implemented by the Rice Wavelet Toolbox v 2.4. Denoising is done in three steps:

  1. Compute coefficients for the average gel, given a choice of wavelet basis. The coefficients represent frequency-location decomposition in both dimensions of the image. The dyadic wavelet transform (DDWT) is more efficient, but UDWT gives translation-invariant results which means denoising is the same even if the image is shifted or cropped in either dimension, the denoising being more effective. Results are rather dependent on wavelet basis: by default, the Daubechies wavelet with four vanishing moments is used.
  2. Hard thresholding is applied to the wavelet coefficients: this sets all coefficients below a threshold φ=δσ to 0, leaving coefficients >φ unaffected. The parameter σ is the robust estimator of the SD (see Donoho and Johnstone) in using the median absolute deviation for highest frequency wavelet coefficients divided by 0.6745, and where δ is a threshold parameter specified by user and where this parameter controls more or less de-noising. Values for δ from 5-20 work well for MALDI-MS, and for 2D gels where background white noise is not as strong, smaller values are better; a default of δ=2 is adequate.
  3. The denoised signal is reconstructed by applying an inverse UDWT to the threshold wavelet coefficients. Thresholding works because white noise is equally distributed among all wavelet coefficients, while the signal is focused on a small number of coefficients. Thresholding zeroes out the large number of wavelet coefficients of small magnitude which correspond mostly to noise, leaving the small count of coefficients of large magnitude representing signal.

3. Detect pinnacles on the wavelet-denoised average gel

With de-noising completed, spot detection is done on wavelet-denoised average gel using pinnacles. A pixel location contains a pinnacle if it is a local maximum in the horizontal and vertical dimension and with intensity greater than a threshold which by default is the 75th percentile of the gel. A list of pixel coordinates is produced marking the pinnacles in the average gel that index spots of interest in the given gel set.

4. Combine any pinnacel within a specified proximity

If pinnacles are found within a given 2k1 + 1 × 2k1 + 1 square surrounding another pinnacle, pinnacles will be combined with one that has the highest intensity, removing spurious double peaks, and will accommodate imperfect alignment. It is rare to see two pinnacles which are ≤ 5 units from each other with a particular scanner resolution. With a 1024 × 1024 image, a k1 = 2 is used.

5. Quantify each spot for each gel by taking maximum intensity within spec ified neighborhood of the pinnacle in the average gel

Each spot is now quantified for the gel by taking the maximum intensity within the 2k2 + 1 × 2k2 + 1 square formed by taking the corresponding pinnacle location in the average gel and extending out ± k2 in the horizontal and verical dimension in the individual gel. Width k2 should be as small as the proximity k1 in the previous step. By default k2 = k1. This permits finding the maximum pinnacle intensity for a corresponding spot even when the individual gel alignment is not perfect. Alignment accuracy is thus determined by spots being ± k2 within the horizontal or vertical dimension.

6. Apply background correction filters and normalize spot quantifications

Background correction and normalization is done in the final step. A global minimum intensity representing the entire gel is sufficient when the background appears relatively uniform; if it appears to vary spatially, a windowed minimum is then used. Because pinnacle intensities rather than spot volumes for quantifications, background is estimated only for pixel locations with pinnacles and the calculation proceeds quickly. Default window is ± 100 pixels in horizontal and vertical. The window must be large enough to extend beyond each spot region and avoid attenuation of quantified pinnacle intensities.

Normalization is done by dividing each pinnacle intensity on a given gel by mean pinnacle intensity for the given gel. Wavelet de-noising can be done to individual gels before quantification; generally it is not done because it makes little difference in result.

With N individual gels and p spots, a matrix N × p protein expression levels with no missing values remains. During profiling or group comparison studies, the matrix is analyzed to find which of the p spots is associated with factors of interest and worthy of further study.

The system Z3 (Smilansky, 2001) was the first hybrid 2D gel alignment algorithm incorporating pixel info, comparing rectangular image regions each having a few detected spots. The Robust Automated Image Normalization (RAIN) algorithm is intended to answer certain questions, such as removing systematic geometric deformation and expression bias in image domain for full automated aligntment. The main steps in RAIN are:

  1. A smooth tensor-product B-spline surface is used to warp source gel until all features are brought to correspond with those in reference gel. Volume-invariance is used to ensure expression is not amplified under dilatation, not attenuated by contraction.
  2. Simultaneously, additive and multiplicative bias field (modeled as smooth B-spline surfaces) are fitted between images to account for systematic regional changes in background and staining. Thus alignment is guided by underlying expression pattern but not influenced by expression bias and background variation.
  3. The sum of squared residuals (SSR) is calculated to assess image correspondence, which is pure pixel-based similarity measure. SSR assumes data with homogeneous variance, and so an approximate arcsinh transformation is appled to raw pixel values before alignment.
  4. Gradient descent is used to find maximum of similarity measure with respect to paramters of the warp and bias-fields. A multi-resolution approach is required to find a global optimum. First coarse deformations are corrected on subsamplied images, then progressively finer deformations are eliminated to on progressively more and more detailed images.
  5. RAIN is implemented using GPGPU (general purpose computation on graphics cards—http://www.gpgpu.org/ for performance.

Smooth volume-invariant B-spline warping. B-splines are shortest possible polynomial splines with global continuity, chosen for efficiency in optimizing geometrical transformations for image registration. These are smoothly connected piecewise polynomials where additional smoothness constraint imposes continuity of the spline and its derivatives up to order n−1, effectively with only 1 degree of freedom per segment. For numerical computation purposes the following recursive definition is useful:

β(t) = ∑i=1 n+1 PiBi,k(t)

where tmin ≤ t < tmax, and 2 ≤ k ≤ n + 1 and where position along the curve is given by t between start tmin and end tmax, Pi are position vectors of the n+1 vertices and Bi,k are normalized B-spline basis functions. B-splines are linearly separable and thus have a simple tensor-product extension to 2D. Standard warping techniques correct for optical distortion and thus introduce protein overexpression after dilation and underexpression during contraction. Deformation in 2DE is a physical manifestation, and to preserve volume-invariance the intensity of transformed pixels must be normalized, achieved by consdering the set of first-order partial derivatives of the mapping with respect to input coordinates. The B-spline warp in RAIN is third-order to ensure C2 continuity in intensity normalizaiton as well as provide smooth deformation for spot modeling. Thus accurate quantification can occur on warped gels.

A global shift-invariant linear systematic bias can be accounted fro by normalized cross-correlation, as used in MIR. If systematic bias varies spatially over each image without correction, image-based registration will be erroneously guided towards its elimination by geometric means. It is necessary to model and correct for disparity orthogonally so geometric alignment can focus on true underlying expression levels. A novel shift-variant linear correction by bias-field modelling is presented as a means of robustly modelling expression bias and background variation in 2-DE. Smoothly varying multiplicative and additive bias-fields as single-valued third order B-spline surfaces are modelled in this study. Multiplicative bias represents staining nonlinearities and is analogue of normalization in microarray studies. Additive bias models residual background variation and thus provides background correction between gels. By ensuring that bias-fields are smoothly varying, their correction remains a robust and well-posed problem. Finding a maximal clique is a classical NP-hard problem and thus exact algorithms are guaranteed to return solution only in a time which increases exponentially with number of vertices in the graph. Exact solution smethods have limited performance on large datasets. The graph can be decomposed into reduced subgraphs and then SAP in corresponding reduced search spaces determined. Search space is reduced by partitioning graph in all connected components (i.e., maximal connected subgraphs). Edges with weight of 1 are suppressed because it is assumed they are not sufficient to belong to a pseudoclique when searching the connected component; these cannot represent alignment of size 2 (which contains 2 spots). Suppression of edges does not distort results because if spots are really in alignment, they would have connectivity with other spots of the alignment and would be restored later in process.   • uninduced biological variation: changes in metabolic concentrations under identical experimental conditions
  • statistical noise: systematic and random errors in the experimental setup

Comparative modeling is one approach in protein structure prediction, where evolutionarily related homologous templtes are identified by sequence or sequence profile comparisons. High resolution models are are generated by copying framework of the template structures. Proteins from different evolutionary origins may have similar structure, and the threading methods are designed to make the query sequence directly on to the 3D structures of other solved protein with the goal of recognizing folds similar to the query even when there is no evolutionary relationship to the query and template protein. In another approach ab initio modeling is done but with limited success on only small proteins, < 120 amino acids. Ab initio methods modeling uses evolutionary or knowledge-based information for getting spatial restraints and identifying local structural building blocks. I-TASSER (iterative threading assembly refinement) is an example of a composite approach and ranked best for automated protein structure prediction in the last two critical assessment of protein structure prediction (CASP) experiments. Usefulness of a prediction algorithm is measured on accuracy: high resolution models with root mean square deviation (RMSD) values in range of 1-2 Å generated by CM using close homologous templates, meet the highest structural requirements and suitable for computational ligand-binding studies and virtual compound screening. Medium resolution models have 2-5 Å RMSD, generated by threading and CM using distantly homologous templates, and used for finding spatial locations of functionally important residues (active sites, disease-related mutations). Loop regions have functional importance but high structual variability, although scaffold is conserved. The lowest resolution models, usually ab initio or based on weak hits from threading, have uses include protein domain boundary identification, topology recognition, and family/superfamily assignment.

Problems that arise are cases where absence of structural features or homology nonetheless produce similar or identical function. Many protein folds and families are functionally promiscuous, with different folds performing the same function. Because of the success in online prediction, the steps for submission of structure to I-TASSER server are offered here. The method is done in four stages:

  1. Stage 1: Threading. Threading is a bioinformatics procedure for identifying template proteins from solved structure databases that have a similar structure or similar structual motif as the query protein sequence. The query sequence is matched against a non-redundant sequence database by position-specific iterated BLAST (PSI-BLAST) to identify evolutionary relatives. Sequence homologs are assembled in a multiple alignment and a sequence profile created, and PSIPRED is along brought in for secondary structure prediction. With both sequence profile and PSIPRED results, LOMETS is then used to thread the query through the representative PDB structure library, a locally installed meta-threading server combining seven threading programs: FUGUE, HHSEARCH, MUSTER, PROSPECT, PPA, SP3, and SPARKS. Within the threading programs, templates are ranked by sequence- and structure-based scores. Top template hits from each program are selected for further consideration. Statistical significance is used to judge the quality of template alignment (the Z score), defined as energy score in std dev units relative to statistical mean of all alignments.
  2. Stage 2: Structural Assembly. Continuous fragments in threading alignments are excised from template structures, then used to assemble structural conformations of sections that have aligned well with aligned regions, typically loops and tails, which are built by ab initio modeling. I-TASSER uses a reduced model to represent the protein chain and improve conformational search efficiency. Each residue described by the α-C atom and side chain center of mass. Regions not aligned during threading process have lower modeling accuracy results, and so structure modeling in these regiions confined to a lattice system of grid size 0.87 Å, and this reduces entropy for conformational search. Such a grid size introduces considerable uncertainty of conformational representations in CM (error range usu 1-2 Å), but there are no effects on ab initio modeling, whose error range is 4-6 Å. Thread-aligned regions have higher accuracy. Modeling is off the lattice and the template fragments are kept rigid during simulations to maintain high resolution in these regions. Fragment assembly is performed using modified replica-exchange Monte Carlo simulation technique (Zhang et al Proteins 48: 192, 2002). This implements several replica simulations in parallel at different temperatures, temps periodically exchanged between replicas, energy barriers flattened by hyperbolic function to speed jumps of simulations between different energy basins. Overall simulation guided by composite knowledge-based force field, including (1) general statistical terms derived from PDB (Cα/side-chain correlations, H-bonds, hydrophobicity); (2) spatial restraints from threading templates; (3) sequence-based contact predictions from SVMSEQ (Zhang et al PNAS 103: 2604, 2006). Conformations generatd in low temp replicas during refinement simulation are clustered by SPICKER (Zhang & Skolnick, J Comput Chem 25: 865, 2004) to identify low free energy states. Cluster centroids obtained by average 3D coordinates of all clustered structural decoys. Readers should read Wu et al (BMC Biol 5: 17, 2007) and Zhang & Skolnick (PNAS 101: 7594, 2004) for the on-and-off lattice system, Zhang et al (Biophys J 85: 1145, 2003), Zhang (Proteins 69: 108, 2007), and Zhang (Proteins 77: 100, 2009) for force field development, and Zhang et al (Proteins 48: 192, 2002) for the MC search engine.
  3. Stage 3: Model Selection and Refinement: Fragment assembly simulation is performed again starting from selected cluster centroids. The inherent I-TASSER potential remains unchanged in the 2nd run, and external constraints are pooled from LOMETS threading alignments and PDB structures which are structurally close to the cluster centroids, identified by TM-align. The 2nd iteration removes steric clashes and refines the global topology of cluster centroids. Decoys generated in 2nd round of simulations are clustered again, and lowest energy structure selected as input for REMO, which generated final structural models by building all-atom models from Cα traces through optimization of H-bonding networks.
  4. Stage 4: Structure-based functional annotation. The function of the query protein is inferred by structually matching predicted 3D models against proteins of known structure and function in the PDB. Three structure/function libraries were constructed independently and updated every 2 weeks. A library of 5798 nonredundant entries with known EC numbers, 26045 nonredundant entries with known Gene Ontology terms, and 19658 nonredundant entry of known ligand-binding sites. Global structural similariyt search is used to recognize proteins with similar global fold, and local similarity search is complementary, identifying analogs with different fold but which have similar function as there is conservation of active and binding sites. A template modeling score (TM-score), RMSD, sequence identity and coverage of structural alignment composite scoring is used. TM-score assesses topological similarity of protein structure pairs, ranging from [0,1], with higher score indicating better structural match. A TM-score < 0.17 statistically means a randomly selected pair with gapless alignment taken from the PDB, whereas a score > 0.5 means protein pairs of similar folds.

The quality of the prediction will be used by biologists to determine how they use the prediciton. The C-score (confidence) is the quality of the threading alignments and convergence of I-TASSER's structural assembly refinement simulations:

C-score = ln (M/Mtot × 1/⟨RMSD⟩ × 1/7 ∑i = 1 → 7 Z(i)/Z0(i)

where M is number of structure decoys in cluster and Mtot is number of decoys generated during I-TASSER simulations, ⟨RMSD⟩ is average RMSD of decoys to cluster centroid, Z(i) is Z score of best template generated by the ith threading in the seven LOMETS programs and Z0(i) is a program-specified Z-score cut-off for distinguishin between good and bad templates. The overall rate constant A → C will be k1k2/k−1 and a new RRKM expression becomes an approximation:

k(E) = σ[N‡ (TSBC)] ÷ [hρ(A)]

Participation of other isomerization/tautomerization steps with other TS values at lower energies than TSBC would not affect rate constant in the above kinetic scheme, but the rate equation will not hold if k3 << k−2 does not hold. If C → D + E is rate-determining, then N‡(TSCD) is a better calculation than N‡(TSBC). If the molecular internal energy increases, then TSBC is better because it is a tight TC with zero of negative ΔS‡ than TSCD with a loose TS having positive ΔS‡. Note that the difference is related to time scale on the reactions: some are nanosecond-to-microsecond while other are millisecond-to-second.
