Methods for Measuring Iodine in Urine

Excerpts from

J. T. Dunn, H. E. Crutchfield, R. Gutekunst, A. D. Dunn
Int. Council for Control of Iodine Deficiency Disorders. © 1993.

About 1 billion of world’s population at risk of iodine deficiency disorder (IDD). The ICCIDD was formed in 1986 with a cooperative mission with other UN agencies to eliminate this problem by year 2000. IDD can result in preventable mental retardation.

Clinical evaluation of IDD centers around estimating thyroid size, typically by palpating for goiter, and laboratory techniques, namely urinary iodine determinations. 90% of iodine is excreted in the urine and its level reflects the dietary intake of the mineral. Urine is obviously easier to collect than serum. The mineral is stable within the medium. Procedures with radioiodine and serum determinations of TSH and other hormones are more complex. This book includes a total of 7 different methods in which iodine can be evaluated in urine medium.

The 24 h urine sample is the most reliable, since iodine concentrations fluctuate in samples collected at random. This is obviously not practical.

Expressing the iodine as a normalized value (expressed usually relative to urinary creatinine determinations, since it is usual for individuals to excrete a constant amount of creatinine) is a way of understanding the value given in a sample. Others think iodine should be expressed merely as a concentration since creatinine is likely to be low in cases of malnutrition, which are areas likely to have IDD; it has been shown that iodine levels expressed in terms of creatinine appeared normal but were indeed in samples with true iodine deficiency. It is probably ideal for several collections to be taken during a 24 hour period to compensate for changes in urinary volume, dehydration, and fluid consumption which might affect.

The emphasis on measuring the urinary iodine is on measuring iodine levels semI − quantitatively in a large number of samples rather than focusing on precise determinations in small sample numbers. Correlation between low urinary iodine levels.

IDD can be characterized in three stages related to determinations of urinary iodine and correlated to presence or absence of goiter and related disorders. Stage I (mild) may show goiter presence (prevalence is 5-19.9%) with 5.0-9.9 µg I/dL median urinary iodine. The need for correction is important. Stage II (moderate) is often seen with goiter (prevalance 20-29.9%) and a presence of hypothyroidism and a median urinary iodine of 2.0-4.9 µg I/dL; the need for correction is urgent. Stage III (severe) will be seen with obvious goiter and hypothyroidism in the sample population (goiter prevalence at least 30% and more), and the presence of cretinism in numbers, with median urinary levels < 2.0 µg I/dL; the need for correction is considered critical.

It is recommended that urinary data be grouped as expressing proportions of the sample group for

  1. ≤ 2 µg I/dl
  2. ≤ 5 µg I/dl
  3. ≤ 10 µg I/dl.

It is thought that this manner of presentation gives a clearer picture of potential iodine deficiency.

Iodine in urine is usually present as iodide anion (I–). It is typically absorbed in the gut as that form. Present in the blood, the thyroid gland is the only organ which requires all of it, uptake being physiologically regulated by TSH and converted to thyroid hormones. The tissues serving as targets of thyroid hormones (T3 or T4) take up the hormone and destroy it to release the iodide. The urine is the principal excretory medium, but breast milk and feces make up the balance.

Most methods for analysis of iodine involve preparing the urine sample by digestion in strong acid or by ashing at high temperature. Iodide appears to function as a catalyst in the reduction of ceric ion to cerous ion with arsenite serving as reducing agent (the Sandell-Kolthoff reaction, Mikrochemica Acta 1 : 9-25, 1937).

2 Ce 4+ + 2 I − → 2 Ce 3+ + I 2

I 2 + As 3+ → As 5+ + 2 I −

Iodide is effectively a catalyst since it is not consumed in the reactions. Ce 4+ is yellow in solution while the Ce 3+ ion is colorless, and the reaction is monitored spectrophotometrically by a change (reduction) in the absorbance at the appropriate wavelength. The sensitivity of this technique is down to several nanograms. The methods discussed are only variations on this particularly chemistry. Interferences with iodate, chloride, and bromide are considered to be minimal (they also function as catalysts). Sulfuric acid and chloride are necessary: the sulfuric acid affects the speed of the reaction and chloride protects against the oxidation of iodide to iodate. Arsenite should not be limiting, with recommendations that Ce 4+ :As 3+ be 1:20.

The reason for preparing urine (by digestion or ashing) is due largely to the elimination of other substances which can interfere with the reaction by causing the reduction or oxidation of ceric or arsenite ions (substances such as nitrite, thiocyanate, or ferrous ion). Thiocyanate can accelerate the reaction (artificially elevated values), and is likely to be present in urine where cassava is consumed. Cigarette smokers also have high levels of urinary thiocyanate, as high as cassava eaters. One method (F) does not make use of such a procedure.

The 7 methods (A through G) are now compared and the essential features described.

  1. Removal of interfering substances . All methods but F have a step to compensate for the effects of interfering substances. Most use acid digestion, while one employs alkaline ashing. Chloric acid digestion is mild in two methods, more vigorous in three others; the vigorous methods were adapted from the methods used to analyze blood iodide, but the more vigorous digestion does not seem to produce values different from the more mild treatment.
  2. Precision . Reproducibility has never been really a problem. The use of automated equipment in some methods eliminates the potential for human (transcription) error. Precision may be greater for methods eliminating interfering substances, and is also affected by technical skill
  3. Speed . The simpler the method, the faster. Three methods show a high rate of assay completion time.
  4. Technical Demands . The three methods shown to be the fastest are the easiest to learn and to perform.
  5. Instrument complexity . Two methods require rather expensive automated equipment, and the basic essential is something to measure color changes.
  6. Safety . Steps using chloric acid digestion represent the danger here. The digestion causes formation of perchlorate salts, which are explosive in their properties. It is important to vent samples being digested with these acids.
