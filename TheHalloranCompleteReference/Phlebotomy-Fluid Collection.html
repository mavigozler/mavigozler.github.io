Acid-Citrate-Dextrose (ACD)

This whole blood support medium had been used at one time for preservation of donated blood. Blood stored 14 days had an RBC survival rate of 85% or more, and 70% or more for up to 24 days.

Bulk Solution

Preparation

The following recipe can be used to preserve and prevent clotting of 500 mL of whole blood (1 unit for transfusion/banking purposes). The water quality for making the solution should be at least Type I grade, and preferably water suitable for cell culture should be the ideal.

The following equipment and materials which will make the preparation process easy are:

  • Glassware
    • 125 or 250 mL beaker (cleaned as indicated below)
    • 100 ml graduated cylinder (cleaned as the beaker)
  • Teflon-coated magnetic stir bar (fits into 100 mL beaker) with stirrer
  • Electronic balance sensitive to 0.001 g measurements
  • Weighing boats or glassine paper
  • Stainless steel weighing spatula(s)

Weigh out the following into a clean 125 or 250 mL beaker (rinsed with Type I grade water) containing 75 mL of stirring cell culture grade (or Type I) water:

  1. 2.2 g trisodium citrate (dihydrate) (Na3C6H5O9 • 2 H2O or
    CH(OH)(COONa)C(OH)(COONa)CH(OH)(COONa) • 2 H2O)
  2. 0.80 g citric acid (monohydrate) (C6H8O9 • H2O or
    CH(OH)(COOH)C(OH)(COOH)CH(OH)(COOH) • H2O)
  3. 2.45 g dextrose [D-glucose] (monohydrate) (C6H12O6 • H2O)

When dissolved, bring the volume up to 100.0 mL with water solvent in a clean 100 mL graduated cylinder. Pour it back into the beaker to further mix it again. Filter it through a 0.22 µm sterile filter into a clean storage container for the volume prepared: if preparing larger volumes of ACD, a vacuum filtration setup may be used and fitted with a 0.22 µm membrane. The receiver flask should be meticulously cleaned as all containers which hold the ACD solution! (Perhaps such containers could even be dedicated to preparing this solution or solutions requiring special cleanliness, if this is to be a routine activity) . For small volumes, serial filtration using a large syringe (10 ml) fitted with a membrane cartridge/cassette is convenient. The type of membrane used to filter ACD does not matter most likely: most membranes are compatible (unreactive) with a solution like ACD; polysulfone or nylon (N-66) might be preferable, but nitrocellulose will probably work just as well, if the blood is not be used for transfusion purposes. As a precaution, a little of the ACD solution can be passed through any filter membrane first and this initial filtrate discarded with the idea that it may have liberated any impurities or substances that might negatively affect the whole blood constituents.

If a unit of whole blood is to be collected, use 67.5 mL ACD added to a 500 mL (1 unit) collection bag.

Storage

If the ACD is not to be distributed immediately into blood collection tubes, the bulk solution can be stored in a cool place or even refrigerated (at 2-8° C) for an indefinite period (as much as three years).

As with even the most stable solutions however, it is wiser to prepare only the amount necessary that will last a half-year to a year. A formal process requires that you keep information about the date of preparation and even assign the preparation a lot number for reference.

Use in Blood Collection

Distribution to Collection Tubes

The general rule is that 1 volume of ACD is mixed with 6-8 volumes of whole blood to produce the properly iso-osmotic/isotonic blood draw. Indeed, it is quite important to obtain the proper blood draw since ACD is used to preserve blood cell survival, and proper concentrations are essential. The table below lists the volumes of ACD necessary to add to the standard blood collection tubes and the amount of the blood draw required to obtain proper mixing. It is important that it be emphasized to the phlebotomists to make the necessary exact blood draw volume: a label or mark placed on the tube to specify exactly how much blood is to be added to properly fill the tube is essential.

Note that by convention, ACD tubes are fitted with a yellow-colored stopper. If the tube is fitted with a stopper of another color, it is absolutely necessary to label the tube to indicate that it really contains ACD as a whole blood preservative.

Storage of Collection Tubes

Long-term storage of ACD-filled tubes is identical to the long-term storage of bulk solution. Refrigerated products probably last longer intuitively than those stored at room temperature; it is probably not necessary to freeze any ACD solution, no matter what the container. In general, tubes can be stored in a cool place if stored in ambient temperature.

One product manufacturer of ACD-containing transfusion unit bags indicates an shelf life of three years for the product (the need for certain storage conditions were unknown). It is safe to say that ACD is quite stable for at least that period.
