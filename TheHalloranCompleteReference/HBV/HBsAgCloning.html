Experimental Plan: Coding Sequence Construction, Cloning, and Expression

The experimental plan will include a plan and backup plan (Plans A and B). The goal is to obtain the coding sequence for the S, M, and L forms

The roadmap will involve these steps:

  1. Using PCR assembly with oligos optimally chosen by the Gene2Oligo bioinformatics software, a total chemical synthesis of:
    1. a consensus coding sequence for the S protein
    2. a consensus coding sequence for the M protein N-terminus including the N-terminus of the S protein up to a restriction site in S sequence
    3. a consenus coding sequence for the L protein N-terminus including the N-teminus of the M protein
  2. Ligations and transformations into blue-white E coli using pUC19 vector:
    1. pUC19 + synthetic S coding sequence
    2. pUC19 + synthetic M-Nterm/S-Nterm + S Cterm digest product
    3. pUC19 + synthetic L-Nterm/M-Nterm + M Cterm digest product
  3. Characterization of cloned products
  4. Plasmid extraction, digestion, cloning into expression vector and expression host

LHBs Message PCR Assembly

The Two-Step PCR Assembly Method

  1. The volume of the reaction is 50 µl. Into a PCR tube, place the following:
    • 10 nmol of each dNTP (final 200 µM)
    • 5 pmol of each oligo and primer to be used (final 100 nM)
    • 125 nmol of MgCl2 (final 2.5 mM)
    • Add the Taq polymerase buffer in the appropriate volume.
    • Taq polymerase in appropriate amount
      Either 0.5 U Promega polymerase or 1 U of NEB Vent DNA polymerase are recommended
    • Add Type I water to a final volume of 50 µl
  2. Set the thermal cycler program as follows:
    • Initial step: 95° for 2 min
    • Denaturation: 95° for 30 s
    • Anneal: 52° for 30 s
    • Elongation: 72° for 1 min
    • Termination: 72° for 5 min
    • Number of cycles: 30
  3. Second PCR. In a PCR tube, in a final 50 µl volume, 3 µl of the PCR assembly mix is added to a mix with a final 200 µM each dNTP, 1 µM primers, the appropriate amount of Taq polymerase and its buffer and 2.5 mM MgCl2. The thermal cycler program is as for the first PCR, except that the annealing temperature is 5° less than the lowest primer Tm. Note that primers as long as 40 nt have been used: 30-35 nt of template complement, 6 nt of restriction site, and a 6 nt extension for restriction enzyme foothold.

The LHBs translated sequence is exactly 400 amino acids, or 1200 bp long in oligo form. The consensus sequence was optimized for codon usage in bacteria (E coli, B subtilis). It might need to be optimized to prevent possible tRNA exhaustion. The 1200 bp sequence was divided into three fragments (LHBs F1, LHBs F2, and LHBs F3) and each fragment was submitted to processing by Gene2Oligo. Oligos and primers for gene synthesis are to be chemically synthesized and purified using at least gels or HPLC (we don't want to risk just using desalted oligos, which may contain shortened fail synthesis oligos).

The following steps will be performed to synthesize the LHBs message:

  1. Premxing synthesized oligos and primers. Perform the first step PCR assembly with reactions for the three LHBs fragments. The oligos and primers for each of the LHBs three message fragments assemblies are in the Gene2Oligo section: LHBs message fragment 1, LHBs message fragment 2, LHBs message fragment 3. It is vital to mix oligos and primers properly. It might be necessary to resuspend oligos and primers in distilled water and then mix them in a large tube, then lyophilize the tube down, and resuspend in smaller volume of water for the reaction. For instructions on how to do that, see the box at left.
  2. First Step Assembly of three LHBs fragments. Now do the first step of the PCR assembly for 30 cycles according to the protocol at the top. The second PCR is done normally with just primers to eliminate the bases added for doing PCR assembly which are not part of the sequence. This step is omitted, because now we have to assemble the three LHBs fragments using overlapping oligos and the LHBs 5′ sense/forward and 3′ anti-sense/reverse complement ends only. This is also PCR assembly but with fewer fragments.
  3. Joining three LHBs fragments By Another PCR reaction. Mix into a PCR tube the following oligonucleotides and primers (final 100 nM each):
    • L1L2fo
    • L1L2ro
    • L2L3fo
    • L2L3ro
    • LHBsfp
    • LHbsrp
    It might be necessary to form a premix, and dry it down using lyophilizer or centrifugal evaporator, then resuspend in water and place in the reaction.
  4. To the synthetic sequences, add 3 µl of each of amplification reactions for Fragments 1, 2, and 3 of LHBs reactions. Add the PCR components (master mix, or dNTPs, buffer, magnesium, and enzyme) and run the program. The annealing temperature should be set at 60°. Times and temperatures for other steps and cycle steps should be as for the PCR assembly. Use 30 cycles.
  5. Examine using agarose gel electrophoresis about 10 µl of this amplification with 10 µl each of the PCR assembly reactions of Fragment 1, 2, and 3 amplifications. You are looking for 1200 bp exactly for the fragment joining reaction, and bands of 420, 420, and 360 bp for Fragments 1, 2, and 3 respectively.
  6. If the 1200 bp band is not detected, perform a standard PCR (not assembly) reaction using the forward and reverse primers of LHBs (LHBsfp and LHBsrp). Verify that a 1200 bp band is present. It might be a good idea to perform this reaction anyway.
Commercially obtained primers will often be purchased in x nmol amounts, where x is usually 25 or 50. Since the primer and oligos per reaction are about 5 pmol, which is one ten-thousandth of the amount you have, it will be a good idea to premix the PCR assembly reaction oligos and primers, dry them down, and resuspend in the volumes needed. Since there are about 30-40 oligos and primers per mix, and we can comfortably pipet 5 µl, giving a total of 150-200 µl, we will put the mix in a 500 µl tube.

In addition, for each 5 µl per oligo or primer, let's use 50 pmol, enough for 10 such reactions. Therefore, you want to resuspend the commercial tube of x nmol to a concentration of 50 pmol/5 µl = 10 pmol/µl. If you have x nmol = 1000x pmol, then this is 1000x pmol × 1 µl/10 pmol = 100x µl = (x/10)ml.

The problem is that most vials delivered are about 1 ml maximum in volume. If the value x/10 is > 1, then it is not possible to resuspend that amount. So instead, do the following:

  1. Add 500 µl water to the tube containing 1000x pmol of oligo or primer, giving a concentration of 2x pmol/µl.
  2. Now take 500/x µl of 2x pmol/µl, and add to (100 − 500/x) µl of water, 100 µl of 10 pmol/µl.
  3. To the 500 µl tube, add 5 µl (total 50 pmol each) of the oligos and primers to mix them.
  4. Lyophilize the tube down.
  5. In resuspending, you can resuspend in water so that each reaction is in a 5 or 10 µl volume, as you wish: this means the total volume in the resuspension will be 50 or 100 µl, since there are 10 reactions.

Nomenclature

For purposes of distinguishing DNA sequences created here for expression cloning, it is suggested that the sequences have the format [L|M|S]HBs[Cns|Nat][0-9][0-9]. With the [ ] brackets and separated by the | character will be one character of L, M, or S depending on the message. In the case of a sequence generated as a consensus and not spliced segments from sequences found in nature, the Cns (for sequence) designation is used. If the sequence could basically generated from joining segments of DNA that could be cut from natural sequences, the Nat designation is used. Finally two digits are used ordinally for each sequence created. It is vital to keep a catalogue with the proper name of the sequence connected with the sequence.

For the consensus LHBs message created for this project, the name shall be LHBsCns01 and it should be included in the naming of other products including/inserting it.d

Overlap-Extension PCR Cloning

We will try this cloning technique. This is an alternative traditional use of restriction enzyme cutting and ligating inserts into vectors. As a backup, we will have materials and methods for performing the traditional cut-and-ligate technique (see the next section).

OE-PCR saves steps that can affect yields and time of processing. It requires special high fidelity and processive thermostable DNA polymerases. Those polymerases that work as well as NEB Phusion DNA polymerase are used. The general principles for this technique were described by Bryskin & Matsumura (2010).

PCR Amplification of Insert With Vector-Bridging (Overlap) Sequence

  1. To 200 µl PCR tubes or tray wells, add 0.1 fmol to one tube and 1.0 fmol of 1200 bp LHBs coding sequence to another tube. Use in calculations a molecule mass of 79.2 pg/fmol for the LHBs, and that 1 AU260 = 50 µg/ml purified ds DNA. The DNA should have been cleaned using PCR clean-up kit or standard phenol-chloroform-isoamyl alcohol with EtOH precipitation.
  2. Add 10 pmol of OE-UC19-LHBs-fp and OE-UC19-LHBs-rp primer pairs to the reaction. (This is 2 ul of a 5 pmol/µl primer stock dilution.)
  3. Add ice-cold PCR components except, preferably as a master mix. Keep on ice-water bath. Use a final 1.5 mM MgCl2 concentration, and 0.2 mM each of the dNTPs. Use a standard Taq DNA polymerase in amounts specified by the product vendor (usually 0.5 to 2 U).
  4. Add distilled or Type I water to a final 50 µl
  5. The PCR program starts with 98° for 2 min. Then 30-32 cycles of:
    • denaturation: 94° for 30 s
    • annealing: 60° for 30 s
    • polymerization (extension): 68° C for 120 s (2 min)
    The final extension is 68° for 10 min.
  6. Pour a small 0.6% agarose gel and load the amplification reaction. When the bromophenol blue marker has reached to the middle of the gel, examine the gel in the transilluminator: prepare a photo and turn on the transilluminator only long enough to get a photo, then turn it off. Rapidly inspect the photo to look at the separation. When you identify a 1246 bp fragment, that is mosly likely your amplicon.
  7. Gel-purify the fragment after you cut it out of the gel. Use a gel extraction kit to recover the product and estimate recovery and concentation using A260. Suspend the fragment in a minimal amount of TE, and store remainder that is not used in the next process below at −80° in an O-ring screw cap tube. This PCR product will have the name LHBs-pUC19flanked for the purposes of this document, and you can label it so.

Backup Method In Case of OE-PCR Failure

A classic cut-and-ligate will be done on the insert and vector in the event of failure of OE-PCR. First the LHBs message will be extended on the 5′ and 3′ ends using PCR to include restriction sites for enzymes in pUC19 and already ordered for this project for other uses; the sites will be EcoRI and XbaI. Then the amplicon and pUC19 plasmid will be digested with the enzymes. This will be followed by mixing the digests with high insert:vector ratios in the presence of T4 DNA ligase. The ligation reaction is then immediately used to transform competent E coli cells, and transformants detected using blue-white screening.

Before starting the procedure, make sure the following have been prepared:

  • 100 mm petri dishes with X-gal + IPTG (be economical): make no more than 5 plates beyond what you estimate that you will need. Store unused plates in proper place (add wet towels wrapped in plastic bag to keep hydrated).
  • Chemically competent E coli using Inoue method
  • Pure plasmid prep of pUC19: see genomics methods for multiple methods of making pure plasmid prep

Perform the following steps:

  • Placing Vector Restriction Sites on Inserts Using PCR
    1. In a 200 µl PCR tube, add 50 pmol each of primers cutlig-UC19-LHBs-fp and OE-UC19-LMSHBs (final 1 µM in a 50 µl total volume reaction). Place 0.01 fmol (10 amol) of LHBs message. Perform the PCR using a standard method. Include an amplification in which there is no LHBs message.

Constructing Expression Systems for HBsAg Forms

Expression of the recombinant proteins is the most difficult part of the project. It is made more complicated by the fact that attempts to produce HBsAg in the various have proved difficult because HBsAg is likely toxic to the E coli host or it forms inclusion bodies of insoluble protein product that is difficult to solubilize, or the processes involved in solubilization denature the protein and make it difficult or impossible to re-fold the protein in the proper form for restoring immunoreactivity, necessary to make an ELISA product with the protein product. We are trying to maximize the yield of protein, so even after we find one system that expresses product enough to get started, we must optimize the system, which means continue testing expression systems that produce even more protein

We will initially use a bacterial host. This is generally Eschericia coli, but there is a Bacillus subtilis system to be tried too. Expression in bacterial hosts is usually sufficient for protein to be used for medical diagnostic kit (ELISA, etc) production. The advantages are that bacteria can be grown rapidly at very low cost compared to other expression systems. The disadvantage is that they lack the post-translational modification processes such as glycosylation, particularly for many secreted or membrane proteins that are to be cloned.

Intracellular Expression vs. Secretion. Bacteria can secrete proteins as well as express them intracellularly. When possible, secretion should be preferred since it means that cell extraction is not required, which could eliminate the need for multi-step purification as well as make it possible to express the protein with the use of fusion or chimeric tags, which even of short length might affect the function of the recombinant if the tag is present. In cases where intracellular expression is done, it will likely be tried with a tag for an affinity purification step.

The Takara Bio Systems document describes the step-by-step protocol to use. The first and long set of steps are the construction of the plasmid library.

Add 2 µl of the 5× In-Fusion HD Enzyme Premix, then bring up the final volume to 10 µl with sterile distilled water. As a control, perform the reaction without addition of any the SP DNA mixture. If the volume of plasmid and the volume of SP DNA > 7 µl, then double the 5× Premix volume, then add water up to 20 µl. If the insert were about 1.0 kb, and the vector is 5.8 kb, 100 ng of a 6.8 kb plasmid is 22 fmol.
  4. The mixture is incubated 50° for 15 min then placed on ice. At this time, start thawing the E coli HST08 competent cells.
  5. Gently mix the thawed cells and transfer 100 µl to 14 ml round bottom test tube but do not vortex.

  7. Leave on ice for 30 min.
  8. Shock in a 42° water bath for 45 seconds
  9. Incubate on ice for 1-2 min.
  10. Add 37° pre-warmed SOC medium to final volume of 1 ml

Cold Shock Expression: pCOLD

Incubating E coli at lower temperatures causes the organism to express a set of genes regulated by a cold shock system. This may be useful for recombinant (heterologous) proteins that cause toxicity or which may show unwanted insolubility within the intracellular space (cytoplasm) of the cell. In general, protein expression rates are reduced at cold temperatures except for one system, the cold system. One gene expressed in particular is cspA (cold shock protein A). The DNA elements necessary for transcription and translation of the cspA gene have been isolated and created in a series of vectors called pCOLD and marketed by Takara Bio/Clontech. These elements are in 5′→3′ order:

  • cspA promoter
  • lac operator
  • the 5′ untranslated region of the mRNA
  • the translation-enhancing element (TEE) (depends on vector)
  • a His-tag sequence (depends on vector)
  • a Factor Xa cleavage site (depends on vector)

Immediately following are the MCS and then the 3′ untranslated region containing transcription termination elements.

In addition the vectors contain:

  • lacI gene to code for the repressor which binds to the lac operator
  • the ColE1 origin of replication
  • beta-lactamase gene for ampicillin resistance (selection)
  • M13 IG

All of these are 5′ to a multiple cloning site featuring unique restriction sites of common enzymes. The features of the series of vectors are as follows:

  • pCOLD I (4407 bp, GenBank AB186388) contains the TEE, His-tag, and Factor Xa protease site in 5′→3′ order just 5′ to the MCS.
  • pCOLD II (4392 bp, GenBank AB186389) omits the Factor Xa protease site in vector pCOLD I.
  • pCOLD III (4377 bp, GenBank AB186390) omits the Factor Xa and His tag elmenents (just the TEE).
  • pCOLD IV (4359 bp, GenBank AB186391) which omits all three of the elements

All four vectors can be purchased (Cat #3360) at 5 µg each, or each vector can be purchased separately at 25 µg each (Cat #3361 through #3364 for vectors pCOLD I-IV).

Factors to control in cultivation and induction are the medium and temperature of culture, aeration, timing of the induction, inducer concentration, and induction time period.

The general protocol for creating expression-competent recombinants is

  1. Construct the expression plasmid using selected pCOLD vector using standard tools: restriction enzyme, ligases, polymerase, etc. The MCS sequence is 60 nt long containing 10 restriction enzyme sites (6 nt each): 5′-CATATGGAGCTCGGTACCCTCGAGGGATCCGAATTCAAGCTTGTCGACCTGCAGTCTAGA-3′. These are NdeI, SacI, KpnI, XhoI, BamHI, EcoRI, HindIII, SalI, PstI, XbaI
  2. Transform into proper strain (BL21), plate on ampicillin selection medium
  3. Select a transformant and inoculate into amp-containing medium (50-100 µg/ml), and culture at 37° with shaking.
  4. When OD600 ~ 0.4-0.5, refrigerate at 15° quickly. Let stand about 30 min.
  5. Add IPTG from 0.1 to 1.0 mM and continue shaking at 15° for 24 h
  6. Get the cell pellet and extract. Check for expression on SDS-PAGE minigels.

Reverse Translator-Generated Sequences for S, M, and L Proteins

Oligonucleotides are to be chemically synthesized that will produce coding (translated) sequence of the S, M, and L proteins that are the three forms of the HBsAg. First the S coding sequence will be entirely chemically synthesized. The S coding sequence used here is not known to be a naturally occurring sequence in any of the various the eight genotypes of HBV, but it is instead a consensus sequence created by the multiple alignment analysis of the reference amino acids of the S coding sequences considered in one paper to be the genotypes of HBV. Why does this make sense? Because if there are multiple sequences available, the alignment would probably show an idealized structure that represents the evolution of the virus with respect to the protein. It is hope that the consensus sequence product is broadly reactive to/against all genotypes, although it is likely that we may need to create recombinants coding exactly for naturally occurring S forms in particular genotypes of interest.

After the consensus sequence was determined, several reverse translations sequences for the SHBs, MHBs, and LHBs products of HBV were generated using the Reverse Translate online tool. The several sequences were necessary because expression will be tested in different host organisms. Codon usage tables provided through a link on the Reverse Translate tool were selected in order to perform the reverse translation. All of the amino acid sequences used for reverse translation of the S, M, and L proteins are determined as a consenus based on the multiple alignment analysis of sequences considered by Bartholomeusz & Schaefer to be reference sequences for the eight HBV genotypes.

Proposal Notes

The source for the HBV genome is to be infected cell line cultures PLC/PRF/5 (ATCC CRL-8024) containing adw2 type and Hep3B cells (ATCC HB-8064) with the HBV genome integrated. Growth is in RPMI-1640 with 10% FBS in culture bottles where the cells adhere to the surface.

RNA is isolated using the guanidinium method and then the lysate extracted with acid phenol chloroform. The aqueous phase is washed with 99% then 70% EtOH in series. After the pellet is dry it is resuspended in RNase-free water and stored at −80° if necessary.

The total RNA is mixed with oligo(dT)24 primer with MuMLV reverse transcriptase. 2nd strand synthesis is done using PCR with primer containing restriction sites. Amplified product is cleaned using PCR kit. The cleaned product is then run on 2% agarose gel (in TBE with EtBr). The target will be about 1200 bp long. Purified product is treated with EcoRI and HindIII along with pGEM-3Zf(+) vector, which are then both purfied on gels. Material is quantitated for the ligase reaction using T4 DNA ligase.

Ligated product is then transfected into E coli JM109 using PEG-DMSO and heat shock (method of Chung, Niemela, Miller PNAS 1989). Bacteria are plated to SOC agar with 100 µg/ml ampicillin. Transformants containing inserts are selected with SOB top agar medium containing X-gal and IPTG with an overnight culture at 35°. White colonies are amplified with M13-20 and M13-26 primers to reveal a 1450 bp product to be sequenced.

For sequencing, the 1203 bp HBVgp2 region must be amplified in segments of 450-500 bp using PCR.

Pilot expression. Several different systems will be used to control expression: T7-lac, Trp-lac, lacUV5 and cspA cold shock with arabinose will be promoters that are studied. These systems to be tested are pET28a, pCold, pBAD/His, and pET-SUMO. Cultures will be induced with either IPTG or arabinose for 6-18 hour periods.
