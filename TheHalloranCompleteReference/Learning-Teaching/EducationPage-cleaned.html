University of California A to G List

The A-to-G list refer to seven categories made by the University of California to classify academic courses at the high school level. The UC system requires students to show a balance in order to qualify for matriculation and graduation from the UC. This is a guide for other universities. The seven categories are given a letter classification: A, B, C, D, E, F and G.

  • A: History. Courses in "civics" (the understanding of how the U.S. government works); the history or history and geography of the United States; the history or history and geography of the world; study of world cultures
  • B: English. Courses in English language, composition, and literature; in literature, emphasis on British and/or American literature is noted. Literature of the world (translated into English) also acceptable; expository reading and writing
  • C: Mathematics. Algebra 1 and 2, and Plane Geometry
  • D: Laboratory Science. Biology, Chemistry, Physics, Earth Science.
  • E: Foreign Language. French, German, Spanish usually, but others should be acceptable.
  • F: Visual/Performing Arts: Drama and Dance
  • G: Elective Courses. Includes all courses found in A-F categories that are not part of the A-F designated courses. In mathematics, it includes calculus; in laboratory science, includes any subject not used to meet the D requirement. In foreign language, includes any non-English foreign languages not used to meet the E requirement. In Visual and Performing Art, can be music courses, studio art, advanced levels of dance or theater.

Levels of Mathematics

In applying to universities, many high schools have different standards or course-naming practices, or both. In California, students at the late elementary (middle school: 7th and 8th grades) and in high school (9th through 12th grades) take mathematics courses in a proper progression from simple arithmetic even to introductory calculus (often a first-year university level mathematics).

The University of California selects several categories for classifying mathematics courses for which they give no particular explanation or meaning, and so it is essential to be clear as to what the meaning is. As a student, you must examine the concepts you learned in the math class, then decide what category they belong to.

  • Algebra 1. Concepts taught, usually in this order are:
    • Pre-algebra skills: integers, absolute value, order of operations, distributive property, evaluating algebraic expressions, simplifying algebraic expressions
    • Properties of Real Numbers: real number line, absolute value, operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication) of real numbers, distributive property,
    • Solving Linear Equations: use of addition, subtraction, multiplicaton, division; variables on both sides, ratios and rates, percents
    • Graphing Linear Equations and Functions: coordinate plane, graphing linear equations, horizontal and vertical lines, lines with intercepts, slope of a line, using slope-intercept form of a line for graphing
    • Writing Linear Equations: slope-intercept form, point-slope form, determining equation from two points, modeling with linear equations
    • Solving and Graphing Linear Inequalities: using binary operations, multi-step inequalities, solving compound inequalities involving AND and involving OR, solving absolute value equations, solving absolute value inequalities, graphing linear inequalties in two variables
    • Systems of Linear Equations and Inequalities: graphing linear systems, solving linear systems by substitution, by linear combinations; using problem solves;
    • Exponents and Exponential Functions: multiplication properties, zero and negative exponents, graphs of exponential functions, exponential growth and decay functions, scientific notation
    • Quadratic Equations and Functions: square roots, solving quadratic equations by finding square roots, simplifying radicals, graphing quadratic functions, using the discriminant, graphing quadratic inequalities
    • Polynomials and factoring: adding and subtracting polynomials, multiplying polynomials, solving quadratic equations in factored form, factoring ax2 + bx + c for a = 1 and a â‰  1, factoring special products and cubic polynomials
    • Rational Expressions and Equations: proportions, direct and inverse variation, simplifying rational expressions, multiplying and dividing rational expressions, addining and subtracting like and unlike denominators, rational equations
  • Geometry. This is namely plane geometry fundamentals. Study of measurement, properties and relationships of points, lines, angles, triangles, quadrilaterals, circles and the nature of inductive and deductive proofs.
  • Algebra 2. Continues from Algebra 1: fractional exponents, quadratic inequalities, binomial theorem, sequences, functions, exponential and logarithmic equations, complex numbers
  • Algebra 2/Trigonometry. More advanced mathematics without pre-calculus: trigonometric functions, right triangle trigonometry, radian measure, graphing trigonometric functions, trigonometric identities and formulae, solving trigonometric equations, law of sines and cosines
  • Integrated Math 1. A mixture of mostly Algebra 1 and Geometry. expressions, linear equaations and graphs, inequalities, systems of linear equations, functions, polynomilas, quadratic equations, rational expressions; introduction to geometry: coordinate geometry, triangle congruency, parallel lines, planar figures, solid geometry
  • Integrated Math 2. A mixture of Geometry, Algebra 2, and Statistics; a bridge to Algebra II (Algebra 2), Discrete Math or Statistics. Inductive and deductive reasoning, triangles, quadrilaterals, polygons, similarity, transformations, right triangles, trigonometry. Two-column proofs about similar and congruent triangles. Linear systems, functions, and quadratic functions are revisited from Algebra 1 and IM 1. Introduction to probability and statistics.
  • Integrated Math 3. From the California Content Standards (Warning: MS Word document) blueprint adopted Oct 2002 by State Board of Education. it is a combination of (Plane) Geometry (8%), Algebra II (2) (86%), and Probability and Statistics (6%).
    • Geometry: prove and solve problems regarding relationships among chords, secants, tangents, inscribed angles, and inscribed and circumscribed polygons of circles
    • Algebra II: solve equations and inequalities of the absolute value; solve systems of linear equations and inequalites (in 2 or 3 variables) by substitution, using graphs, or with matrices; operations on polynomials, including long division; factor polynomials representing differences of squares, perfect square trinomials, sum and difference of two cubes; show relationships of real and complex numbers arithmetically and graphically (plotting complex numbers as points in the plane); add/subtract/multiply/divide complex numbers; analyze quadratic formula and determine maxima, minima, and zeroes (roots) of function; understand inverse relationship between exponents and logarithms and use in problem solving; judge validity according to properties of real numbers, exponents, and logarithms; use binomial theorem and expand expressions
    • Probability and Statistics. Learn definition of conditional probability and used to solve probabilities in finite sample spaces; compute standard deviation and variance of a distribution of data
  • Statistics. Probability and statistics, collecting data, sorting data, analyzing data, binomial distribution and probability, linear regression, combinations, permutations, and normal distributions
  • Pre-Calculus. First begins with review of conic sections, factoring, polynomials, functions, and inequalities. Trigonometry is focused on functions of cricles and ratios of the sides of triangles. Radian and degree measure, graphs of trignometric functions, and methods of solving trigonometric equations. Exponential and logarithmic functions are re-introduced. Vectors and their properties are examined.
  • Advanced Mathematics. Consider this to be included in the following categories:
    • Accelerated Pre-Calculus. Combines Pre-Calculus and Calculus A (Calculus BC is completed in the following year). Trigonometric functions, equations, and applications, including identities. Introduction to polar coordinates and complex numbers, 2- and 3-dimensional vectors are all done in first semester. In the second semester, differential calculus and intro to integral calculus: curve sketching, related rates, optimization, implicit differentiation; indefinite integral, definite integral, area under the curve, volumes of revolutions and of cross sections.
    • Calculus AB. All the introductory concepts of calculus: limits, derivatives, integration; techniques and applications of the derivative and integral. Introduction to differential equations. Functions are studied graphically, analytically, numerically, and by writing. A graphing calculator is part of the course work.
    • Calculus BC. Review of limits, derivatives, and integration concepts. Study with polar, parametric, and vector functions. Series and Taylor polynomials studied.

IB (International Baccalaureate) mathematics is at the college level. There are three levels (the lowest is SL Studies), but only the highest two are described.

IB-SL (Standard Level) is a less intense course work requiring the application of mathematics without requiring an understanding of the theoretical basis. This mathematics is for scholars intending to go into fields such as chemistry, psychology, economics and business administration. Topics include statistics and probability, circular functions, trigonometry, vectors, matrices, and calculus.
