The Statistics of Population Proportions

Testing A Single Population Proportion

If there is a desire to know whether a certain proportion, p0, of a population possesses or is characterized by a particular property of interest, then the test to perform involves determining whether the difference between an observed proportion, px and the hypothesized proportion, p0, falls beyond the critical limits of signficance on the standard normal distribution. The assumptions for use of parametric statistics (viz. normally distributed proportions when randomly sampled, subjects free of interdependence) must hold for this test to work. The standard error is:

σp = ( (p0)(1 – p0) )½
n

For a null hypothesis H0: p = p0 with an alternative H1: p ≠ p0, the critical limits for p will be:

p0 ± (zα/2) (σp)

for level of significance α (a two-tailed probability).

For a null hypothesis H0: p ≥ p0 with an alternative H1: p < p0, the critical limits for p will be:

p0 – (zα) (σp)

for level of significance α (a one-tailed probability).

If the null hypothesis is H0: p ≤ p0, then the sign on the z value is negated, so that the limit is calculated as

p0 + (zα) (σp)

Example

It is believed that fewer than 25% of a population smokes 5 or more cigarettes per day (suppose this is the definition of “regular smoker”). The null hypothesis is therefore H0: p ≥ 0.25 and the alternative is H1: p < 0.25. 120 people are drawn randomly and 20 report themselves as regular smokers: px = 20 / 120 = 0.167. The standard error σp is the square root of [ (0.25) (1 - 0.25) / 120 ] = 0.0395. For a level of significance of 0.05, the critical value for the one-sided test is z = –1.645. Therefore,

0.25 – (1.645) (0.0395) = 0.18

Since 0.167 < 0.18, the null hypothesis is rejected, and so it is possible that fewer than 25% of the people are regular smokers.

An alternative way to calculate is to determine the z value for observed proportion:

z = px – p0 = 0.167 – 0.25 = –2.03
σp 0.0395

Since –2.03 < –1.645, H0 is rejected just the same.

Testing Differences Between The Proportions of Two Population

It is often of interest to test the difference between two population proportions, to see if p1 and p2 are equal.

The distribution of the difference in population proportions is normally distributed, with a mean μp1–p2 and standard deviation:

σp1–p2 = ( p1(1 – p1) + p2(1 – p2) )½
n1 n2

If the hypothesis is that p1 = p2, then it is possible to say there is a common population proportion, p, which can be estimated by pooling fractional values of the two populations:

p = x1 + x2
n1 + n2

Note that x1 is the number in population 1 having the characteristic of interest, while n1 is the total of population 1. The standard deviation of the pooled population proportion is:

σp1–p2 = ( p (1 – p) + p(1 – p) )½
n1 n2

Note that the pooled standard deviation is effectively the standard error of the pooled proportion, similar to the standard error of the mean when the parameter at the central limit is the mean.

The statistically significant limits for the difference between p1 and p2 is expressed by the formula:

0 ± zα/2 ( p (1 – p) + p(1 – p) )½
n1 n2

z is the critical value on the standard normal distribution which represents the two-tailed probability that determines whether the null hypothesis, H0 (i.e., that the difference between p1 and p2 is zero) can be rejected. It incorporates the level of significance, α, which is typically 0.05 by convention. zα/2 ≈ 1.96

Therefore, for a level of signficance α = 0.05, the limits are:

0 ± 1.96 ( p (1 – p) + p(1 – p) )½
n1 n2

Example

Suppose a researcher is studying two groups, one receiving a medication and one receiving a placebo. There is a question raised as to whether the proportion of the sexes is different in each population; signficant differnces in the number of men and women could be a confounding variable that the researcher wants to avoid.

The treatment group has a total size of 100 and has 23 males, while the placebo group has a total size of 120 with 32 males. Is the proportion of males different in the groups?

p = 23 + 32 = 0.25
100 + 120
σp1–p2 = ( (0.25) (0.75) + (0.25) (0.75) )½ = 0.06
100 120

The limits are calculated using these values as 0 ± (1.96) (0.06) = ± 0.12 for a level of significance α = 0.05. The proportion p1 is 23 / 100 = 0.23 and the proportion p2 is 32 / 120 = 0.27, with a difference being p1 – p2 = 0.23 – 0.27 = –0.04. Since the observed difference is contained within limits of signficance, the null hypothesis (H0) cannot be rejected and therefore it is possible that there is no difference between the proportions of males in the two populations.
