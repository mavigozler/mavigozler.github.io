General Laboratory Practice

Note that this document was originally composed for scientists in proteomics although its principles apply to anyone in a laboratory doing chemistry, biochemistry, and advanced chemistry (of all kinds). There may be references to the word proteomics, but the word can be dropped and the meaning should generally apply. A revision may come later.
This document actually should be called General Laboratory Experiences because a great deal of what is written here is an opinion about how to do things in the laboratory. Different people have different ideas of what works for them. Take this into consideration when reading the content below.

A great deal of the advice and comment presented here might be considered to fall under the category of common sense, but given the often high-pitched disagreement about so-called common sense, perhaps judgment should be reserved about what is considered intuitively obvious.

First Principles

[This section was inspired by, if not entirely plagiarized from Chapter 2 Common Apparatus and Basic Techniques in Vogel’s Textbook of Quantitative Chemical Analysis, 5th Ed., GH Jeffery, J Basset, J Mendham, RC Denney, eds., (Longman Scientific & Technical: Essex, UK), 1988, pp. 71-123. Recommended reading for the scientist.]

The proteomic scientist should adhere to a basic set of principles which probably fit partially into the category of common sense, since these are principles that work outside the laboratory as much as inside.

Cleanliness and Bench Cover. The bench must be clean. A bench cloth (plastic-backed absorbent paper is common) should be used to remove spills of solid and liquid chemicals (solutions) immediately.

Glassware should be scrupulously clean. If left standing (in cabinets or other storage) for any length of time, it should be rinsed with distilled or de-ionized water and allowed to drip-dry or air-dry before use. A cloth can be used to dry the outside (or parts not contacting chemicals) with a lint-free glass cloth, but this should never contact the inside.

Labeling. While the memory of scientists is often remarkable, all containers that will contain any chemical substance or solution, whether for a day (in an assay) or for months or years (as stored product), should be labeled, and preferably before the chemical or solution is added. A good time to label containers is during the preparative stage of the experiment in assembling equipment, apparatus, and when preparing reagents. It is good to have a permanent, write-on-glass, does-not-wash-off-with-water in the laboratory coat breast pocket. The Sharpie is a reliable brand of this type of marker. One pen with a fine and one with a large tip are useful. Gummed labels are useful for containers which will be dedicated to storage of a particular reagent.

Workspace. The bench area ideally should be as wide (1.5-2 m) as the scientist's arms extended right and left, and a 1 m in depth...the workspace. The workspace where the experiment or assay will be done should be free of everything except equipment and supplies necessary to do the job. Items not immediately used should be placed behind those being used now.

Use and Store. When a stock reagent bottle is used to prepare a solution or dilution, it should be used/sampled, and then immediately returned to the location where it is typically found or expected to be found. This prevents a type of clutter that usually occurs during work and which can create confusion.

Replicate Determinations. Statistical significance of results is a goal of the scientist, and so when doing assays, the scientist must account for the trustworthiness of results. Use of duplicate or replicate (two or more) determinations of samples is one way to persuade your colleague that the results you get are reproducible.

Record of Observations/Thoughts/Results. It is still a wise idea to record all calculations (or at least the results of those made in the head) in a bound, standard laboratory notebook, using pen whose ink does not run when any aqueous solution is spilled on it. New experiments or assays should be given a descriptive title, and the date (and possibly time) recorded as well. For results recorded electronically, it is advisable to get a printout of data if it is available and fix (tape) it to the notebook.

Quantitative Practice: Making Calculations

The greatest struggle of inexperienced—and even some experienced—scientists is calculating reagent concentrations, volumes, and masses.

This section will provide guidelines for calculating everything related to solutions and reagents, as well as tips for making solutions and reagents.

Setting Up A Calculation: The Numerator-Denominator Principle

A calculation is really an equation, and an equation must have an equal sign in standard mathematics.

On one side of the equal sign is the answer, the result of the calculation (usually on the right side of the equation for people who think left-to-right).

On the other side of the equation are the factors: all the numeric values that will be used to make the calculation. By defintion, factors are multiplied to one another, and that is the case for pretty much 99.9% of all calculations in this setup. In rare cases, you will use algebra where you set up numbers that are added or subtracted from one another, but they become quantities (values enclosed in parentheses) either in the numerator or denominator of a factor.

All the factors will be represented in a numerator or denominator fashion. That is, if a factor is used in a division operation, then it will appear as its inverted value (that is, if value x is used to divide the factors in the calculation, it will be represented as 1 / x and multipied to all factors).

The equation will then have the form that looks much like this:

w dim1 × x dim2 × dim3 × z dim4 = a dim1 [1]
dim2 dim3 y dim4 1

The dimn actually refers to the name of a dimension or unit, such mg (milligram) or µl (microliter). The values w, x, y, z are numbers expressed in any form (real or floating point or decimal; integers; scientific notation); they are the factors in this product. The product itself is the value a. Note that the value of y is in the denominator, that is the factor is 1/y.

But what is important here is not the values w, x, etc. It is the dimensions dimn. The values were merely arranged in this style in order to arrange the dimension to produce the desired final dimension with its value. Note how dim2, dim3, and dim4 all cross out, leaving dim1 in the final answer. One can remove the dimensions and then just be left with multiplying all numbers in the numerator, then multiplying all in the denominator, and finally dividing the product in the numerator by the product in the denominator to get the final answer.

In constructing the equation (calculation):

  • It is essential to know what one really wants as the answer, which is a value and possibly a dimension. For instance, if a volume is asked for, then the dimension must be in units that represent a volume (µl, ml, L). If the target answer is a mass, then it should be in some form of grams (mg, g) or possibly moles. For concentrations, this is really solution density, or mass units per volume units.
  • Write down all the factors available, even if they will not be used in the final calculation.
  • The missing factors in a calculation are typically conversion units. That is, the conversion of one expression of the volume into another. Thus there are 1000 µliters (µl) in 1 milliliter (ml), and 1000 milliliters in 1 liter (L). Know your conversion units by reflex. Note that these are not constants: a constant is value reflecting a fundamental physical or chemical property in nature or the universe. Planck’s constant is 6.62 × 10−34 J s, and not a conversion of one value into another form.
  • Constants. A typical constant in solution density determinations is the molecular or atomic weight of a substance, which is the amount of grams in one mole of the substance. This is typical missing factor whenever one factor is expressed in grams and the answer sought in some form of moles.

Lots of Examples

These are some real-life examples of where you will encounter these calculations in proteomics.

1. Make up just enough, but not really more than necessary, of a DTT and iodoacetamide reagent for adding to a complex protein mixture prior to digestion with trypsin. The following facts are available:
  1. The protein mixture in the 500 µl tube is in a 25 µl volume.
  2. You will need to make the mixture slightly alkaline, which promotes the reaction of DTT, iodoacetamide, and trypsin; we use ammonium bicarbonate (NH4HCO3) at a final 50 mM for this purpose. The NH4HCO3 has the added benefit of being volatile under a vacuum, which means it can be removed by a centfriugal evaporator.
  3. DTT will be added to a final volume of 10 mM to the alkalinized protein solution, and then left for 20-30 minutes; the reaction is promoted at a warmer temperature (say 30-37°).
  4. Iodoacetamide is added to 50-55 mM (calculate for 50 mM). It is added at 5 × the amount of DTT because some of it will react with DTT.
  5. Finally trypsin will be added. It comes lyophilized in a 20 µg amount. The manufacturer provides a resuspension buffer, which is 50 mM acetic acid, and recommends that 200 µl be added to resuspend the material. Our trypsin treatment will be at 10 ng/µl, based on the literature.
The first thing to add to the protein mixture will be the NH4HCO3, and must be at a final 50 mM. There are two ways to calculate.
  • Quick and dirty. Generally, if a stock reagent is prepared that is 10 to 20 times greater than the final concentration, it is as simple as dividing the volume of the solution to which it will be added by the fold-difference in the concentration between the reagent concentration and the final concentration. Suppose the DTT reagent stock is 500 mM (0.5 M). With a 25 µl protein solution, the amount of reagent to add is thus:
    25 µl × 50 mM = 2.5 µl [2]
    500 mM

    Some things to note about the equation above.

    1. Equation [2] is a classic demonstration of the conservation of mass. How? The concentration of a solute in a solution multiplied to the volume of the solution gives the mass of the solute in the solution. Thus concentration × volume = mass. And using algebra, mass / volume = concentration (because mass divided by volume is also an expression of density, this equation is also called solution density).
    2. Note that equation [2] uses the format of doing calculations in formula [1] above, arranging the dimensions so that they always divide out and yielding a dimension that we want in the end (a volume).

    So you add 2.5 µl of the 500 mM NH4HCO3 to the 25 µl of the protein solution to get 50 mM NH4 as a final concentration.

    Actually, that's not entirely true. Because 2.5 µl of the reagent is added, it contributes to the final volume as well, and so the final volume must be taken into account. To get the true final concentration, one must divide the actual mass of DTT added by the final volume:

    2.5 µl × 500 mM = 45.4 mM [3]
    (25 + 2.5) µl

    Is this acceptable? Many scientists can live with the difference between 45+ mM and 50 mM, about a 10% difference from the desired. Suppose a 1000 mM (1 M) NH4HCO3 reagent stock was prepared and used in instead; this is a 20× concentration.

    25 µl × 50 mM = 1.25 µl [4]
    1000 mM
    1.25 µl × 1000 mM = 47.6 mM [5]
    (25 + 1.25) µl

    Of course the difference will be less with use of higher stock concentrations as expected. The problem is in using stock reagent volumes too small to pipet accurately. I like to use no less than 2 µl although it is possible to pipet 0.5 µl (500 nl) accurately with some sophisticated measuring devices. Be mindful of how small a volume you can pipet confidently.

  • The Truly Accurate. In order to get that exact 50 mM ammonium bicarbonate concentration, one must use algebra to compensate for the added volume. All these equations are conservation of mass equations, and this one is no different. An unknown value x represents the volume of the reagent to be added, and the equation must reflect the fact that a final 50 mM concentration is desired.

    (25 µl × 0 mM) + (x µl × 500 mM) = (25 + x) µl × 50 mM [6]

    Note that one has mixed operations here. Addition and subtraction of numbers can only occur when the dimensions are the same, and dimensions are serialized with multiplication and can be deleted with division.

    The equation is distributed:

    (25 × 0) µl mM + (500x) µl mM = (25 × 50) µl mM + 50x µl mM [7]

    All the units are the same now, so all operations can be done with the numbers. So looking at just the numbers, we re-arrange the equation to get x on one side:

    500x − 50x = 25 × 50 [8]

    x(500 − 50) = 1250 [9]

    x = 1250 / (500 − 50) = 2.78 [10]

    Note that the 25 × 0 drops out of equation [7]. In equation [9] we distribute out the concentration as a quantity out of the unknown volume x. Both sides of the equation are divided by the quantity (equation [10]), and the result is the volume of 2.78 µl.

    We verify this calculation using the equation [3] (also [5]) form:

    2.78 µl × 500 mM = 50.0 mM [11]
    (25 + 2.78) µl
Thus if you have a very accurate pipet and the time to do the algebra, you can get the very accurate concentration.

The next step is to add the DTT. From here on, it will be assumed that the Quick & Dirty method was used to calculate volumes to add, and that the stock reagents are all made up as 10×.

Assuming that 2.5 µl of NH4HCO3 was added, then the volume is 27.5 µl. If we add about 3 µl of 10× DTT (100 mM), then one will have very close to 10 mM. And, yes, the ammonium bicarbonate will be diluted out again, and be less than the 45 mM already calculated, but that's okay. An alternative might be to prepare DTT in ammonium bicarbonate, but that is probably not a good idea since it tends to chemically activate (make reactive) the DTT.

One thing left out is the preparation of those NH4HCO3 and DTT stock reagents. In proteomics, it is generally best to prepare many reagents fresh from the solid/powder. Ammonium bicarbonate is volatile, and so any stock is probably best frozen or again, just made fresh. DTT stock can be frozen, but after a year (and some think 6 months), it is probably better to make up new reagent.

In our real life example, we had several tubes and added up all the volumes for the NH4HCO3 we would need, and came up with 200 µl. At 500 mM, we want to calculate how many milligrams to weigh out:

200 µl × 500 mmol × . . .  = x mg [12]
1 L

Equation [12] is how anyone would first set up such a calculation, and follows a numerator-denominator approach so that it is easy to cancel out dimensions, and be left with the final dimension. Note that the final dimension is mg, and so we know that a mg value must occur in the numerator at some point. We also see that µl stands alone in a numerator value and will have to be canceled by another µl in the denominator. Likewise for the mmol and the L value. Following the principles above, we know that the missing factors in the equation are dimension conversion values and also a constant, which is the molecular/formula weight of NH4HCO3. The rest of the factors (symbolized by the . . . in the equation) are then added in order to do these cancellations of dimensions and end up with the right value:

200 µl × 500 mmol × L × 79.06 mg = x mg [13]
1 L 106 µl mmol

We have added two factors. A conversion factor for the volume, and the molecular weight for ammonium bicarbonate. Notice that the dimensions cancel each other out in a numerator-denominator like fashion, and we are left with mg.

Someone might be confused by looking at the molecular weight, and say, isn’t the molecular weight actually 79.06 grams per mole (79.06 g/mol)? Well, yes it is. And it is also 79.06 mg/mmol, and 79.06 µg/µmol, 79.06 ng/nmol, and so on. Notice that these conversions are made instantly in the numerator and denominator, in each case dividing by 1000 in the numerator and denominator to get the dimensions shown. These sorts of conversions can be made in one's head.

But supposed that this conversion stumps the neophyte. In that case, one adds the factor as follows:

200 µl × 500 mmol × L × 79.06 g × . . .  = x mg [14]
1 L 106 µl mol

It is necessary to add the . . . because clearly there are missing factors. These are now added:

200 µl × 500 mmol × L × 79.06 g × 1 mol × 1000 mg = x mg [15]
1 L 106 µl mol 1000 mmol g

Now all the dimensions cancel out to give the right value, and all we have by adding the conversion factors is divide by 1000 in the numerator and denominator (the 1000 values will cancel themselves out). The final value in mg is easily calculated:

200 × 500 × 1 × 79.06 = 7.9 mg [16]
1 1 106 1

So to make 200 µl of 500 mM NH4HCO3, 7.9 mg needs to be weighed out.

When the chemical was actually weighed out, more than 7.9 mg was weighed. Actually 10.4 mg was weighed out into the 500 µl conical tube in which it was to be prepared. Rather than spend too much needless time trying to weigh out exactly 7.9 mg, it was decided to use the 10.4 mg. Why? First we had 1 kg of the material (that is 1 million mg!) and the cost of this is just tens of dollars (maybe just 10 dollars). The time spent trying to weigh out exactly 7.9 mg could buy another kilogram of NH4HCO3. So instead we re-calculate the amount of water to add to dissolve the salt to the correct concentration, because it is much easier to pipet microliters of water than milligrams of salt:

10.4 mg × mmol × L × 106 µl = 263 µl [17]
1 79.06 mg 500 mmol L

Notice that in going back in the other direction in calculating microliters from the milligrams, we invert the factors that were involved in calculating milligrams from microliters. We wanted 200 µl and calculated 7.9 mg, but had 10.4 mg and calculated it should be in a total of 263 µl. Note that we since we calculated 7.9 mg needs 200 µl for 500 mM, then 10.4 mg would need 200 × 10.4 / 7.9 = 263, an obvious way of calculating proportions.

After the addition of NH4HCO3, DTT is added. DTT is rather expensive, so a 10× stock is made up in a small volume and whatever is ununsed is frozen away at −20°. Since 10× is 100 mM, we want to make 3-5 ml in a 15 ml conical tube. The molecular weight of DTT is 154.3. So we calculate the range from a minimum of 3 to a maximum of 5 ml:

3 ml × 100 mmol × 154.3 mg × L = 46.3 mg [18]
1 L mmol 103 ml
5 ml × 100 mmol × 154.3 mg × L = 77.2 mg [19]
1 L mmol 103 ml

The first three factors are the essential data we need (the 3rd factor is actually a constant, the molecular weight), while the last (4th) factor provides a dimension conversion. Again, when all the dimensions cancel out, then you can do the math on the numbers alone.

At the balance, 51.7 mg of DTT powder was added to a tared 15 ml conical tube. So again, it is now necessary to calculate the water to add to dissolve the DTT:

51.7 mg × mmol × L × 103 ml = 3.35 ml [20]
1 154.3 mg 100 mmol L

So with a P-1000 pipet set at 1,000 µl, I pipet water three times, then set it to 350 µl and pipet it once. When ready, I add 27.5 / 10 = ~2.8 µl of the DTT stock to the alkalinized protein solution.

After 20-30 minutes, I figure that I have to add (27.5 + 2.8) / 10 = ~30 µl / 10 = 3 µl of a 10× iodoacetamide solution. Since the final concentration of iodoacetamide in 50-55 mM, the concentration should be 500-550 mM.

If it is calculated that one needs 150 µl of 500 mM iodoacetamide, then add enough (usually 15%) for pipetting error, which means one needs about 170 µl. The molecular weight of iodoacetamide is 184.96.

170 µl × 184.96 mg × 500 mmol × L = 17.3 mg [21]
1 mmol L 106 µl

So we try to weigh out as close to that number as possible, since iodoacetamide will not keep in solution and we don't want to throw out too much in the end.

Weighing Out Chemicals

Balances

[Parts of this section on balances may come from Vogel's].

Most balances the scientist work with are preparative, that is, the scientist uses them to weigh out chemicals for the preparation of reagents and solutions and mixtures. Analytical balances are used to make gravimetric determinations in analytical chemistry laboratories, that is, they are used to determine the mass of a sample (typically solid) that is the result or product of a reaction or analysis.

In most cases, one should have preparative electronic balances in the proteomics laboratory, although occasionally a beam (knife-edge) pan balance is seen and still useful. With electronic balances, make sure that the balance is level: balances feature leveling bubbles with balance feet adjustable to level with balance, required for accurate weighing. Balances should be located in a room with no air currents or drafts, or at least away from ventilation ducts blowing directly on them or near to them.

Absolute care must be taken to weigh chemicals, particularly strong mineral acids or bases, that might damage or corrode the balance. It is best to pour or spoon out chemicals away or off the balance, and then place the container in briefly to determine weight.

The cleaning of balances is typical, since chemicals get spilled on to the pan easily as well as off the pan. The modern balance has easily removable pans and surfaces usually, which allows for cleaning the chemicals by washing them off the balance parts into the sink (or appropriate waste for very toxic materials).

Balances need to be calibrated from time to time. They usually come with set of weights should be kept clean in their cloths or boxes; they should not be handled with the bare hand (some use ivory-tipped special forceps). Weights are usually a nonmagnetic alloy (80:20 nickel:chromium) and U.S. official classes are M (reference standards, for work of the highest precision), S (working reference standards, typically used), S-1 (precision analytical weights for routine analytical work), J (microweight standards). Follow the manufacturer's directions for calibrating the balance. Some balances, particularly analytical, may need a relible professional service with experience doing the calibration.

Weighing

There are several problems with those trying to weigh out chemicals.

One is that chemicals get contaminated from those trying to weigh out chemicals in containers not intended to be weighing containers, such as cut-up pieces of copier paper. This paper wastes more chemical than it weighs out, and moreover, fibers in the paper get put into solutions and reagents.

The best weighing containers are those plastic weigh boats that come in various sizes from extremely tiny to huge. They are very lightweight so as not to contribute to the weight limits of the balance regarding tare plus weight. Moreover, if weighing out simple inorganic salts, they can be extensively rinsed with water and re-used. Glassine paper designed for weighing also certainly acceptable.

When proper official weighing containers are not available, try actually weighing it out in the container in which the solution will be made and stored. Or, the lids of these containers can be used, and solvent carefully used to wash the contents from the lid into the container. Quite a few scientists routinely use the containers in which the solution will be stored to weigh out the chemicals, because it reduces the risk that the chemical will be contaminated by substances on weighing or intermediate containers.

The sample should also be the same temperature as the balance. As a rule, wait 30-40 minutes to allow samples to reach temperature, unless the sample is unstable or loses its specific or biological activity.

Being Exact Not Required

If weighing out a single chemical to prepare a reagent stock solution of that chemical, it is absolutely not necessary to be exact in weighing out the chemical. Too many people struggle at the balance either to spoon out or to spoon back in chemicals in order to get the balance to read exactly as they have calculated.

This is often not necessary. If the chemical is part of multicomponent solution to be prepared, it is often better to prepare a stock solution than to weigh out the chemical as part of the process of making a multicomponent mixture; the exactness comes from pipetting out the correct volume/aliquot of the stock, rather than measuring out a mass in grams.

Do Not Try To Measure Masses That Cannot Be Measured

Ever see someone pull out a tiny Sigma brown reagent bottle and see that the substance inside weighed 0.1 mg (100 micrograms!), and they wanted 25 micrograms of it? The fretful look on their faces could be seen as they wondered how they were going to measure out 25 µg.

The fact is, when faced with the complexity of weighing out something that cannot possibly be weighed out, then believe what is written on the bottle and go with it. Thus add the solution in which the reagent will be resuspended directly to the vial. For instance, if the vial is at most 0.5 ml, and there is 0.1 mg, then add between 100 to 500 µl of the solvent in order to make 1 to 0.2 mg/ml of the reagent, respectively

Weighing Out Multiple Reagents Into A Single Container

Often it is not possible or prudent to prepare an intermediate stock concentration of a chemical, from which a small volume will then be used to prepare a multicomponent mixture. Often it is better or required to weigh out the chemical directly into a multicomponent mixture.

A few scientists prefer to weigh out multiple reagents into a single container because this minimizes loss and contamination of the multicomponent mixture.

This can all be done in a single container if one is careful to follow a few rules.

  • Work from small/low to big/high. This means that it is better to weigh the substances with lower amounts first and then the substances with larger amounts last. The reason is that the substances in larger amounts form piles that bury the substances with smaller amounts. From the top of the larger pile, excess reagent can generally be recovered and placed back in the reagent bottle without fear of contamination from the other substances.
  • Keep the loads/piles separated when possible. When weighing out reagents, especially those in small amounts, try to use parts of the container than have not come in contact with other chemicals.
  • Weigh Out Unused Areas. If using a lid of the container to weigh out, it is often possible to wash the previous chemical into the container with a small amount of solvent (typically water) to provide a clean area on the lid for the next chemical to be weighed out.
  • Keep It Simple. Do not make this complicated. If using glassine paper or weigh boats for the separate chemicals makes things less complicated or allays certain fears, then use them. To hell with filling the environment up with laboratory consumables.

Using Stir Bars

Selecting The Right (Correct) Stir Bar

Given that I have seen too many laboratory workers picking a tiny 3 mm stir bar to put in a 1 liter beaker, or individuals selecting an oversized stir bar that does not even rest flat (and thus move freely) in the container, it is not always clear about how to select the correct stir bar.

My own sense tells me that the stir bar should be just about half the diameter of the container. And so thus a decent range in terms of a minimum and maximum for the stir bar size should be one-third to two-thirds the diameter of the container. This should give decent mixing at almost any speed.

Of course, one has to work with what one often has. But if the place of work is a laboratory with a magnetic stirrer, there should be a good selection of stir bars in a wide range of sizes.

Retrieving Stir Bars

I personally do not use or like to use those magnetic stir bar retrievers. They often hang or sit in areas where they get dusty, and one never really knows where they have been. They are encased in a polyethylene mold that often becomes ragged and which makes it difficult to wash them. (You are going to wash the retriever, right? Since you don't know where it's been, right....?)

So how do you get stir bars out?

Well, if you must get them out of the container they are in, use another larger stir bar against the outside of the container to attract the stir bar out of the container. The fields are sufficiently strong to allow that. You can also fish out the bar from solution to catch it with a third stir bar (or your retriever) that you manipulate in your other hand.

Another less cumbersome, perhaps clever way is to pour out the stir bar. That is, if the solution just stirred is to be transferred to another container (storage bottle, etc.) then just pour the solution out into the other container with the lip of the container being poured close to the lip of the receving container. If the bar comes out, it is caught on the lip or falls outside the bottle, without falling into the next bottle.

Pipetting

The Gilson Pipetman for variable volume pipetting is a virtual standard in the laboratory. There are certain rules involved in using these pipettors, rules usually given by the manufacturer and not read and thus understood.

  • Vertical pipetting. Gilson says that pipetting should be done as vertically as possible, and that the pipettor should be held vertically as possible while there is solution in it. It is not necessary to be hyper about being vertical, but a general rule is the pipettor should not be held beyond a 45° angle from the vertical. Be sensible: holding it horizontal (90° from the vertical) is absolutely likely to cause solution to be delivered into the air hole on the pipet body. Don't do that.
  • Proper seals and no dripping. When the pipet tip is lifted out of the solution, there should be no dripping or loss of volume whatsoever from the pipet tip. That is, within reason: one could hold the pipet there for 10 minutes hoping to see a drop fall out (and it will), but that is beyond the performance criteria for a liquid handling device. If dripping is observed (call it dripping resulting in a >10% loss of volume within seconds), it usually means an improper seal between tip and body. Just firm up the seal by hand. If the dripping still continues, try using another tip to see if it disappears; if it does, it means the other tip has been deformed (or is the one bad apple in the lot). If dripping still continues, it could be that the seal between the plunger and body within the pipettor is worn and needs replacing, or has not been fit properly (particularly if it has just been replaced). Open the tip-holding body and check the seal and plunger (make sure they are clean). Use of inexpensive tips could also mean low-quality tips, with higher chance of finding more deformed tips that leak. Also make sure that the correct tip is being used for the correct pipettor.
  • Caustic reagents. It is better to avoid pipetting caustic agents with these pipettors (try using glass pipets with bulbs, or prefer the use of graduate cylinders of small size when possible). The plunger within many models (all?) of the Pipetman is made of stainless steel which can nonetheless corrode. Corrosion can cause loss of contact with the seal and eventual leaking. Replacement of the plunger will probably cost as much as just buying a new Pipetman. So after pipetting caustic materials—particularly mineral acids—
  • Quick check of calibration. If the pipettor is suspected of not dispensing the proper volume (being out of calibration), a quick check involves taking room temperature ultrapure water and pipetting it several times on an analytical balance (one that measures to 10 mg level accurately). Check the volume not just at the 100% capacity, but also 25, 50, and 75%, and do each 5-10 times to get an average and CV. The CV should be within 2% (or whatever the manufacturer states/claims). Thus if checking the calibration of P1000, check the pipetting at 250, 500, 750, and 1000 microliters.
  • Correct tips. The smaller pipettors (P2) require different tips from the P20 and above. Pipetting in the 0.5-2 µl range can be very inaccurate if not using a tip actually designed to pipet these volumes.

Reverse Pipetting

There are basically two ways to pipetting with the Gilson Pipetman and pretty much all pipettors. The usual or standard or normal way is perhaps called forward pipetting, if only because the other way is called reverse pipetting.

In forward pipetting, the following steps are taken:

  1. the pipet barrel is brought to the first stop
  2. the tip is immersed in the solution from which liquid will be taken
  3. the barrel is brought back to its full length while taking the accurate volume into the tip
  4. the tip is transferred to the container to receive the solution in the tip
  5. the barrel is pressed to the first stop, and then past it to ensure expulsion of the volume

With reverse pipetting, the steps are as follows;

  1. the pipet barrel is brought to the first stop and pushed beyond it
  2. the tip is immersed in the solution from which liquid will be taken
  3. the barrel is brought back to its full length (note this does not represent the accurate volume)
  4. the tip is transferred to the container to receive the solution in the tip
  5. the barrel is pressed to the first stop and first stop only (!), with solution remaining in the tip; the accurate volume should have been dispensed

Some people say that more accurated repeated pipetting of especially small volumes is achieved by using reverse pipetting. The biochemist should know both methods, and determine for himself what technique gives him the best precision and possibly greater accuracy.

Radiation Safety

Although we do not work with radioisotopes in the Central Laboratory of AUBI (yet), and the biochemistry/molecular biology/biotechnology laboratory is seeing less use of radioisotopes in many methods in switches toward other forms of labeling (flourescence, chemiluminescence), it is still essential to remember certain principles of radiation safety.

The common radioisotopes and their forms of energy and safe use are shown in the table below:

Radioisotope Form of radiation and relative energy Half-Life Typical Use Safe Use Disposal

35S mid energy (168 keV) β 87.4 d labeling of DNA for sequencing thick (3 cm) plexiglas shielding of body and head Keep in shielded place for necessary half lives to bring to background
