Radiocarbon Dating in Archaeology and in the Earth Sciences

 14C dating with Accelerator Mass Spectrometry (AMS) has reduced the sample size by about 3 orders of magnitude. As long as only a few milligrams of carbon can be extracted from a sample, absolute ages for the last 10,000 years can be assigned with accuracies that can be as low as 50 years. The limiting factor for absolute ages further into the past is due to the tree ring calibration curve ending at around 10,000 years BP. A major effort of our research is the extension of the radiocarbon chronology range using lake varve chronologies. A result of this work has been a better fixing of the so-called Younger Dryas, an event that signifies the last major cold event of the last ice age. 14C is also measured in ocean water and sediments to improve the understanding of events associated with the termination of the last glacial. In some special cases it is not even possible to extract milligrams of carbon, e.g. from samples of polar ice cores, if a reasonable time resolution is still be desired. Recent developments have shown that samples of 50-100 micrograms can be prepared without a significant increase in preparation contamination.
