Animal Handling & Care

Cages. Caged animals must be given a comfortable environment. Cages must be constructed that allow each animal to move and make postural adjustments. Minimum space and height requirements are given in the table below, which may change as an animal grows if it is confined when first young.

Cage materials can be constructed from stainless steel, polypropylene and polycarbonate. Surfaces should be smooth and not allow deposits of dirt to build up, and should be cleanable and disinfected. Cages should permit access to food and water and be viewable without need for disturbing the animal.

Feed. Diets can be in pelleted, extruded or meal form with the proper nutrient requirements. Feed can be formulated from natural, purified or chemically defined ingredients. While the usual is to provide it ad libitum, restricted diets to reduce caloric intake may be more natural and increase life span.

Bedding. Animals can be kept in solid-bottom cages with contact bedding or suspended-wire cages with noncontact bedding beneath the cags. Contact bedding gives warmth, absorbs urine and water, and can be used by some species as nesting material. Bedding materials include ground corncobs, sawdust, pelleted peanut hulls, pelleted alfalfa, shredded paper, processed wood chips, and processed paper chips. As for wood-based beddings, aspen, birch, beech, and maple appear to be without side effects (unlike red cedar, or yellow and white pine).

Water. This must be provided at all times and be uncontaminated. Use of water than has been run through deionization, reverse osmosis, or ultrafiltration should be considered.

Sanitation. The frequency of cleaning the living environment (cage) depends on the type of caging and bedding used and the type of animal housed. Usually cleaning 2-3 times per week is to be done to prevent accumulation of waste and bacteria. Dirty bedding must be removed from the room where animals are housed. Cage cleaning and sanitation should be done once a week by either mechanical or hand washing. Daily mopping of floors with disinfectant should be done, and room surfaces should be cleaned at least once a month.

Off-hour care. Personnel should inspect the animals and call emergency numbers of veterinarians and personnel responsible for animal care.

Environment. Temperature, humidity, ventilation, and illumination must be controlled, especially to minimize variables of experimentation.

Gene Expression Troubleshooting

{Notes from PF Schendel (1998) Curr Prot in Mol Biol]

For recombinants in E coli hosts, the big problems are poor expression and recombinant protein insolubility (formation of inclusion bodies).

For low expression:

  1. Maximize the A+T content of the 5′ end of gene to reduce 2° structure of mRNA in this region; this often increases translation efficiency
  2. Determine if there is transcriptional terminator, and if not, insert one. This likely helps to reduce nucleotide drain and mRNA stability.
  3. Check that codon usage is maximized for E coli hosts. Four codons in series that are rarely used can decrease translational efficiency.

For insolubility problems:

  1. Alter culture temperature: more proteins are soluble at lower than at higher temperatures. In the inital studies, it might be effective to do a temperature study at 29, 31, 33, 35, 37, 39, and 41°, and see which does the best expression.
  2. Try different fermentation conditions. Different media and metal supplements (especially for metal dependent proteins) can be used.
  3. Expression rates can be controlled by use of plasmids whose copy number varies (using low copy plasmids will help). The pACYC family can affect gene dosage and thus solubility.

Information On Coating Immuno-Plastics

Principles of Adsorption to Polystyrene

Note from P. Esser (ThermoScientific Nunc Technical Bulletin 06a)

Binding capacity is not merely the count of molecules thta bind to a surface, but those that bind and still retain their biological function. Adsorption to polystyrene is based on van der Waals attractions rather than strong covalent or electrostatic bonds. These attractive forces involve both oscillating induced polarizations and non-oscillating. The oscillating kind (also called alternating polarities, AP) are generally stronger, and increase with the size of the molecule. The nonoscillating polarities (stationary polarities, SP) are because of the inherent polarities in the molecular components. The attractive for of AP is ∝ 1/r7. In general van der Waals forces are about 0.01 of ionic and covalent bonds.

Thermo Scientific Nunc PolySorp is modified to enhance hydrophobic interactions while the MaxiSorp brand is for hydrophilic groups. In aqueous medium, hydrophobic elements preferentially adsorb to PolySorb while hydrophilic molecules to MaxiSorp. It is generally the case with biomolecules that more binding events occur with MaxiSorp, and it tolerates a broad range of incubation conditions during coating. But it could also be likely that sites important for function are bound and thus impeded.

In developing an assay, check the sensitivity and specificity with MaxiSorp first. If function is lost, then try PolySorb. When configuring a PolySorb surface-based assay, higher concentrations and more demanding incubation conditions might be required. Proteins and peptides binding with equal affinity to either. Where there is higher lipid component, prefer PolySorb; where there is higher glycan content, prefer MaxiSorb.

Small molecule coating. Generally molecules must be above a certain size in order to adsorb/adhere noncovalently. Small peptides (especially synthesized epitopes) should probably be attached to a carrier protein or molecule.

Calculations. A spherical/globular antigen will pack in in a monolayer on the well surface with the most efficient packing (hexagonal arrangement) according to:

binding capacity Q (g/cm2) = 2/√3 × MW/N × 1/(2r)2

where r is the Stokes radius and this is

r = RT / [(6π)(η20)(D20)N].

The final form of the equation is:

Q = (6√3)π2(MW)N(2r)2 / [RT]2

If the constants are substituted N = 6.023 × 1014 / nmol, R = 8.314 × 107 g cm2 s−2 °K−1 mol−, T = 293° K (20° C), with a water viscosity of η20° C = 10−2 g cm−1 s−1 and a diffusion coefficient of the biomolecule with reference to water at 20° C being 4 × 10−7 cm2 s−1 for IgG, then an antibody coating of about 300 ng/cm2, or 2 pmol/cm2. Note the result is multiplied by 109 to get a value in ng/cm2.

An IgG molecule has from the Fab to Fc distance of 15 nm, a Fab to Fab span of 15 nm, and an effective depth of 3 nm. If adsorbed to Fc alone, it occupies a surface area of 45 nm2. This calculates to Q 3.7 pmol/cm2. If it packed effectively lying on its side, then the effective area is 225 nm2 per molecule, and this would give Q = 0.74 pmol/cm2. Therefore the packing of an IgG must be somewhere between lying down on the side and perfectly standing up.

If a molecule's weight is proportional to its size and volume, plots of Q vs. MW show that Q does not change significantly compared to molecular weight, meaning that compact globular molecules do not signficantly take more area with the increase in size.

Consider the differences in coating IgG with plastics of different types. IgG has glycan moieties on the Fc portion. Since MaxiSorp wells show a preference for oligosaccharides, it would be expected that coating yields better antigen binding if the IgG molecule is oriented as expected. In the experiment both types of plates were coated with dilution series using e starting concentration C well above saturation. Both specific and nonspecific antibodies were used. A sandwich assay was done with Ag and anti-HRP specific Ab in excess. Results of OD vs. log [specific coating Ab] will show two sigmoid curves: the one on the left will be for specific IgG and the one to the right of it is nonspecific IgG. Two points are computed from the results: these points are the intersection of the straight line formed from the upper asymptote of the curve and the slope of the line tangent to the midpoint of the sigmoid between upper and lower asymptotes. Assume A is the intersection of lines of the specific IgG and B is for the nonspecific IgG. The ratio between the left (S) and right (C) sigmoid curves, has the relationship of S / C = A / B. Thus, S = (A × C) / B.

The actual data reveals that QMaxiSorp = (AMaxiSorp / BMaxiSorp) × C × (V / F). This value was 650 ng/cm2 for the MaxiSorp, and 220 ng/cm2 for the PolySorb. The A/B ratio was 1/20 for MaxiSorp, and 1/60 for PolySorp. The max. IgG concentration C was 100 µg/ml, reactant volume V = 200 µl with a surface area F = 1.54 cm2.

The results indicate that the MaxiSorp seemed to obtain the theoretical binding of an IgG molecule fully upright. The PolySorp value of one-third of the theoretical capacity could be explained by an equal proportion of molecules lying flat and molecules fully upright. In the assay, antigens of different size were captured, AFP has a mass about half of IgG, whereas the other antigens (ferritin, fibronectin, thyroglobulin) range from 3-5 times more than IgG mass. A more dramatic reduction in the OD signal would be expected for small-sized antigens than for large-sized antigen.

The Surface/Volume Ratio in Solid Phase Assays

Note from P. Esser (ThermoScientific Nunc Technical Bulletin 10b)

With a larger surface area-to-volume ratio of a liquid in a plate well, it is understood that adsorbing molecules bind to surface faster when the liquid is exposed to more surface. This reduces incubation times if so. With a finite incubation time, a higher sensitivity is also gained.

The adsorption kinetics model can be described by the reaction and second-order rate equation:

A + C → B
dBt / dt = [ (A − Bt) / A ] × [ (C − Bt) / V ] × S × (kDt/π)½

where Bt is count of molecules bound at time t, t is elapsed incubation time for adsorption (hr), A = capacity of number of molecules that can be bound (saturating number), C = number of molecules in solution (concentration), V = volume of liquid in well (cm3), S = adsorbing surface (cm2), D = diffusion coefficient of IgG = 1.44 × 10−3 cm2 hr−1, and k = constant (dimensionless) determined by data simulation.

The first two factors derive from applying second order kinetics theory, and express adsorption rate at time t proportional to the instantaneous fraction of surface available for adsorption (first factor) and instantaneous concentration of dissolved molecules (second factor). The first factor is valid only if molecules can be adsorbed to form monolayer. The third/last factor is derived from diffusion kinetics in which molecules adsorb to surface by diffusion during incubation time. The third factor may not follow assumptions unless the liquid is stirred to maintain solution homogeneity: pure diffusion transport may occur only in a thin zone (layer) next to surface. But exact modeling of unstirred systems is more complicated.

At time t = 0, the rate equation looks like:

dB0 / dt = S × C/V × (kD/π)½ × (∂t)½ molecules/h

according to the rate equation, representing the initial rate, where for given concentration (C/V), the rate is proportional to the surface area S. Integration of the rate equation produces a saturation function

Et = Bt / A = (1 − Z) / (1/F − Z)

where Z = (F − 1) × 2P(kDt/π)½, the fraction, Et, of the surface area is adsorbed for a given time, F = C/A represents a supply/capacity quotient, and P = S/V is surface/volume ratio. This equation can be used to determine the time (tx) required to obtain a certain fraction (x%) of the maximum adsorption. Three equations are derived depending on the value of F:

tx {F < 1} = P−2 × (4kD/π)−1 ×(1 − F)−2 × [ln{(100 − x − F) / (100 − x)}]2
tx {F = 1} = P−2 × (4kD/π)−1 × [x / (100 − x)]2
tx {F > 1} = P−2 × (4kD/π)−1 ×(1 − F)−2 × [ln{(100 − x/F) / (100 − x)}]2

These equations show that tx is inversely proportional to the square of surface/volume ratio (P). Thus a 4-fold increase in P means that the incubation time is decreased by half. A plot of t90 (as a percentage of the maximum t90) vs the log F from F = 0.1 to 10 shows that the maximum time is when F = 1, and as F → 0, it has a nonzero minimum value. If C and A are equally large, the adsorption would take the maximum time.

Edge Effect in Microplates

Absorbance readings on identical samples from the wells in the center of the plate to the edge of it can vary. These have two sources:

  • Temperature differences during substrate conversion between central and edge wells.
  • Differences in illumination of the plate during substrate development.

With respect to temperature variations, this is typically due to failure to make sure reagents are warmed to room temperature before using them.

Another possibility is plate stacking: those in the center of the stack are somewhat insulated from heat that it greater at the edges of a stack or the top and bottom plates of the stack.

In other cases, there is an attempt to shorten incubation time either by using higher temperatures (or even at room temperature), and the development of the substrate is not distinct in critical ways.

To avoid edge effect:

  • Let incubations occur in the dark (or at least in low light conditions)
  • Place reagents and plates to be used should be placed in an incubator set at the temperate in which the assay plates will be incubated.
  • Plates should be sealed with adhesive tape or put into humid chamber to prevent evaporation, which also causes temperature differences as well as concentration changes.

Comparison of Blocking Agents for ELISA

Note from RP Pratt and B Roser (ThermoScientific Nunc Technical Bulletin 07a)

Various blocking agents were tested with flat-bottomed 96-well polystyrene Nunc Immuno Plate MaxiSorp F96. BSA, newborn calf serum (NBCS), and casein (as sodium caseinate, with 25 g casein powder dissolved in 800 ml 0.3 M NaOH stirred overnight at 37° then titrated to pH 7.0 with HCl and brought to 1.0 L) were used. Wells were incubated in PBS with a dilution series of the block agents overnight at 4°, then incubated 1:4 PBS diluted rat serum for 90 min 4°, and rinsed twice with PBS + 0.05% Tween 20. Then an anti-rat HRP-conjugated rabbit F(ab′)2 was incubated, followed by washing and substrate (peroxide/tetramethyl-benzidine). This tests how much Ig from rat serum became bound because of a failure of blocking agent to block. The sodium caseinate proved to be the best blocking agent.

In another test, either HRP or alkaline phosphatase were used to coat wells, followed by block with NBCS or casein, then incubated with dilution series of the other of the enzymes, followed by addtion of either peroxide/TMB or p-nitrophenylphosphate. Addition of substrate for the 1st stage enzyme detects displacement, while addition for 2nd stage detects penetration of the blocking agent. Results show that a failure to block is because of 2nd stage penetration of block rather than to displacement of 1st stage enzyme.

A TSK HW 55S column was used to examine casein and NBCS in PBS. Most of the species in NBCS were proteins > 60 kDa which casein represented a size heterogeneity > 60 kDa and < 10 kDa. Casein was ultrafiltered then on three membranes such that it occurred in three ranges: < 10 kDa, from 10 to 30 kDa, and > 30 kDa. Blocking was re-tested and it was found that the smaller class of proteins in the fractions gave the greater blocking efficiency.

Thus the more effective blocking agents are peptides of < 10 kDa, and particularly casein.

Detergents in Polystyrene ELISA

Note from P. Esser (ThermoScientific Nunc Technical Bulletin 8)

Detergent use ensures that nonspecifically bound components are not retained which can increase background signal or otherwise produce reduced signal interference. The class/kind/type of detergent and its concentration and particular point of use should be determined to ensure no unwanted effect occurs. Because detergents generally allow dissolution of solutes which are soluble in one phase with another phase, there is hydrophobic and hydrophilic parts. They can exert a blocking effect as well if incubated with the plastic before the biomolecule to be coated. Preincubation of detergents beore the coating molecule alters surface chemistry and thus subsequent coating chemistry, which may perform unexpectedly.

Five detergents were studied: Tween 20 and Triton X-100 (among non-ionics), SDS (anionic), DTAB (cationic) and CHAPS (zwitterionic). PolySorp and MaxiSorp plates were tested. The method involved using detergents at different steps in the assay:

  1. MicroWell plates with MaxiSorp or PolySorp surfaces treated with 5 µg/l swine anti-rabbit antibody (Dako Z196) in PBS overnight or with nothing.
  2. Washing was done three times with PBS + 0.2 M extra NaCl and with our without one of the five detergents added to the wash at 0.05%.
  3. For two hours, wells were treated with either HRP-conjugated rabbit antibody or HRP-conjugated swine antibody. Antibodies were at 1.3 µg/ml and in PBS. The solution was also with or without one of the five detergents at 0.05%.
  4. Finally another wash with PBS + 0.2 M extra NaCl and with or without detergent was performed.
  5. OPD and peroxide were then added to develop color, which was measured.

The following codes apply: − − − means that steps 2−4 never contained any detergent in the wash or treatment. A code of + − − means detergent was used only in 1st wash. + + − means detergent was used in 1st wash and the rabbit or swine antibody incubation. + − + means detergent was used only in 1st and 2nd washes. And + + + means detergent was used in every step.

  1. Detergents show blocking effect against nonspecific adsorption only if present together with conjugates: only in these cases are no significant signals from nonspecific adsorption.
  2. Tween 20 is an exception to the above generalization: when used in first wash, it helps to reduce nonspecific adsorption. It could be due to large size, which means it is not washed away easily. The larger portion of it is hydrophilic, and so it is not consistent that it has a blocking effect on the PolySorb surface (hydrophobic).
  3. DTAB has a large nonspecific background, possibly because it binds to conjugates nonspecifically.
  4. Tween 20 and DTAB enhanced signals if used in 2nd wash: it might be because of detergent presence in the substrate solutions. A control was done with HRP-Ab directly coated to the well with and without detergent presence in the substrate solution: with Tween 20 or DTAB present, there was an enhanced signal, indicating their effect on conjugate. SDS and CHAPS negatively interfered, and TX-100 had little to no effect.
  5. Tween 20, TX-100 and particularly SDS produce small specific PolySorp signals, and only SDS gave small signals with MaxiSorp. This is related to the harshness of the detergent during washing, and that PolySorp would bind less native antibody in stable way than MaxiSorp.
  6. Where no detergent is used at all, there is no difference between specific and nonspecific signal, nor between MaxiSorp or PolySorp. A control of [125I]Ab was used as the first layer and equal amounts of Ab were present in MaxiSorp and PolySorp wells. This explains equal signal qualities on both types of plates, but not equally large nonspecific signals. This may be due to nonspecific adsorption.

Results show that detergent is necessary to remove nonspecifically or loosely adsorbed substances which themselves can sterically hinder reactants. When no blocking agent is used, detergent presence in post-coating reagents will generally avoid unspecific adsorption. Tween 20 is unique in that it can act like a blocking agent (BSA, casein), so it might be possible to use it once and omit it thereafter. Ionic detergents (SDS, DTAB) are clearly undesirable because of their interference with assay reactants. Triton X-100 and Tween 20 nonionics worked well with MaxiSorp wells, while CHAPS might be a better choice for PolySorp.

Blocking Agent and Detergent

Note from P Esser (ThermoScientific Nunc Technical Bulletin 09)

Blocking agents in ELISA include non-specific proteins and detergents. In this study, three detergents (from Bulletin 8) Tween 20, Triton X-100 and CHAPS are used in combination with BSA and casein, using two-layer Ab sequence on MaxiSorp and PolySorp plates. In many cases it is preferable to use a protein blocking agent rather than a detergent since the latter might disrupt weak but specific interactions. BSA is commonly used as a blocking agent, but casein is heterogeneous and may be more effective. What about using blocking agent together with detergent?

(Binding) Activity of Adsorbed Antibodies

Note from P. Esser (ThermoScientific Nunc Application Note 11b)

When complex and large biomolecules adsorb to surfaces, do they lose their function (biological activity)? In Bulletin 10, the proportion or percentage of adsorbed capture antibodies with functional activity could be estimated using mathematical model fitting to the data. In this study polyclonal antibodies (pAbs) [IgG] were adsorbed to MaxiSorp F96 (439454) and PolySorp F96 (475094) polystyrene surfaces and the estimation of adsorption by simulation of target IgG bidnd data using the model modified for non-monolayer binding kinetics.

An affinity-purified swine anti-rabbit IgG antibody (Dako Z400) at 5 or 0.5 µg/ml in PBS (pH 7.2) was added to well in 200 µl volume as a capture antibody. Maximum antibody densities on the well surface have a value Q in ng/cm2, which have been determined in another bulletin study to be 650 ng/cm2 for MaxiSorp and 220 ng/cm2 for PolySorp under these conditions. A [pAb] = 5 µg/ml is effectively saturating and so the Q values will be attained. At [pAb] = 0.5 µg/ml, the Q′ will be less than saturating and have been found to be Q′ = 65 ng/cm2. After coating, a dilution series of rabbit IgG antibody (Dakeo A008) [target Ab, or tAb] were incubated to be able to estimate the density. This was followed by HRP-conjugated swine anti-rabbit IgG (Dako P217) in PBS + 0.05% Tween 20. Washings were with PBS + 0.2 M extra NaCl + 0.05% TX-100. Note that a 200 µ;l/well covers a surface of 1.54 cm2 and there is a surface area/volume ratio of 7.7/cm.

The rate equation for non-monolayer binding kinetics of the rabbit IgG antibody is as follows:

dBt/dt = [ (qA − Bt) / qA ] × [ (C − Bt) / V ] × [ S(kD/π)½ t½ ]

where Bt = number of bound tAb molecules at time t, t = elapsed binding time (in hr), A = theoretical maximum monolayer density of tAb molecules (~650 ng/cm2), q = fraction of monolayer density that can be bound, C = initial count of tAb molecules in solution, V = volume of liquid in well (0.2 cm3), S = covered surface area (1.54 cm2), D = IgG diffusion constant (1.44 × 10−3 cm2 hr−1), and k = 2π2.

When integrated through time:

Et = Bt / qA = {1 − exp(Z)} / {q/F − exp(Z)}

where Z = (F/q − 1) × 2P(kD/π)½ t½, Et = fraction of qA occupied at time t, F = C/A, and P = S/V (7.7/cm).

It is assumed that qA represents the monolayer of capture antibodies adsorbed and that tAb maximal binding to form the 2nd layer represents the active proportion of the 1st layer.

In determining the model, the active percentages were found to be quite low. For MaxiSorp at 650 ng/cm3, it is only 2%, lower than values determined by others in the range of 5-10%. For saturated PolySorb (220 ng/cm3) active fraction is 6%. When non-saturated (65 ng/cm2), MaxiSorp active percentage is 8%, while in non-saturated PolySorp (same value), it remains 5%. This might possibly be explained differnt ways the IgG molecules beds to the surface, which has been discussed here. In MS-650, molecules may be packed in tight palisades, while in PS-220, the packing is more loose, with less presumed steric hindrance. In the unsaturated, packing is much less and so is the reduced hindrance. The signal intensity and differences between plates in saturated and unsaturated is remarkable. Higher signals were seen in unsaturated which might be attributable to better access of substrate to enzyme, and the higher signals in MaxiSorp might be due to better bedding of the conjugate favoring enzyme accessibility. Thus using less than saturating concentrations of capture antibody will give better sensitivity in sandwich assay and reduce costs.

If plate drying for storage is likely to alter activity, then blocking with inert material (albumin) may prevent loss of activity. It was found that with increasing target Ab levels, there seems to be more binding to the capture Ab, which was not found with MaxiSorp, where more target did not bind more. This might be because capture Ab on the more hydrophobic PolySorp surface are pulled preferentially to a binding orientation, whereas with the hydrophilic MaxiSorp, the surface bonding energy is stronger and no such concerted pull activity is possible.

ELISA Validation

Note from Millipore

Internal standard recovery test

  1. Prepare the assay target in three concentrations (low, medium, high), and prepare a blank solution using the buffer containing the assay target. If the assay target is antigen (as for sandwich assay), prepare an antigen standard. If assay target is antibody, prepare antibody standard.
  2. Obtain 8 unknown (biofluid) samples, and aliquot each sample to four tubes labeled 'B' (blank), 'L' (low), 'M' (medium), and 'H' (high). If the unkowns are numerically labeled for the assay, then make it '1B', '1L', '1M', '1H', and so on.
  3. Add 60% (or appropriate adjustment) of a microplate well volume of unknown to each tube.
  4. To each 'B' tube, add 60% (or appropriate adjustment) of blank solution. To each 'L' tube, add the same volume of low concentration standard.
  5. Perform the ELISA: when it is time to add the sample, instead add the prepared sample + spiked standard to duplicate wells.
  6. Perform the assay at least twice to check reproducibility and compute a mean.

Check the recovery and determine if plate optimization is required. Acceptable recovery values should be between 80-120%.

Linearity Test

  1. Use the same 8 samples from the recovery test.
  2. Dilute each of the 8 samples to at least 3 concentrations, that is, the undiluted 1:1 (and maybe two or three more: 1:2, 1:4, 1:8).
  3. Perform the assay and determine expected vs. actual.
  4. Repeat the assay again or more (twice, 3 times) to get mean values and measure of variation

Values should occur between 80-120% of what is expected.

Intra-assay Variation

  1. Obtain 8 samples known to have varying concentrations.
  2. For each sample, use a whole row of 12 wells to assay that sample
  3. Determine the mean and CV. Acceptable variation is from 80-120%.

Inter-assay Variation

  1. Obtain 8 samples known to have varying concentrations.
  2. Perform duplicate well analysis on each sample using one plate.
  3. The next day using a new plate but the same lot reagents, perform the assay again.
  4. Repeat this a third day, different plate but same lot reagents.
  5. Determine mean and CV, and the acceptable range is 80-120%

Sandwich Assay Validation

  1. Obtain three 96-well plates from the lot.
  2. To each plate, perform the assay with blank, and low and high concentrations of the assay target, using a total of 32 wells per plate for each. That is for blank, columns 1-4 can be used; for low, columns 5-8; and for high columns 9-12 A CV of 10% or less is acceptable.

Analytical Sensitivity (Limit of Detection)

Do not confuse with clinical sensitivity.

  1. Perform the assay with blank and the lowest standard curve point in 20 replicates.
  2. Compute mean and standard deviation of the absorbance values for both blank and low standard.
  3. The sensitivity can be calculted as follows:
    sensitivity = ( mean absorbance of blanks × mean absorbance values of low standard ) + 2 × standard deviations of the blank values
    mean of absorbance values of low standard − mean of absorbance values of blank

Product Comparison

Run samples on the product against the products of other manufacturers using the same format. Compare results using correlational statistics.

Biofluid Matrix Correlation

Acid Digestion of Aqueous Samples and Extracts for Total Metals For Analysis by FLAA or ICP Spectroscopy

These notes are taken from the SW-846 manual of the methods of the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) of the U.S. Dept. of Interior. The method below is Method 3010: Acid Digestion of Aqueous Samples and Extracts for Total Metals For Analysis by FLAA or ICP Spectroscopy, Rev. 1, July 1992.

The digestion is not suitable for sample to be analyzed by graphite furnance atomic absorption spectroscopy: hydrochloric acid causes interferences during atomization (see method 3020A).

Materials required, recommended, or which may be of use include the following:

  • Griffin beaker, about 150 ml, or the equivalent
  • watch glasses, ribbed and plain, or equivalent
  • qualitative filter paper and appropriately fitting funnels
  • centrifuge
  • graduated cylinders, 100 ml, or equivalent
  • an electric hot plate that can maintain a temperature of 90-95°

Chemicals are to be reagent grade: these should conform to specifications of the Committee on Analytical Reagents of the American Chemical Society. Other grades are possible provided they do not lessen accuracy of determination.

  • reagent water
  • concentrated HNO3 (nitric acid)
  • HCl (hydrochloric acid), made up as a 1:1 HCl:water dilution.

Reagent water that is organic-free is best produced by passing tap water through a carbon filter bed with 2 kg of activated carbon. A water purification system will also deionize it. Boiling water 15 min will also remove volatile organics; subsequent to that maintain at 90° and bubble an inert gas through it for 60 min.

Samples are to be collected in containers that must be pre-washed with detergents, acids, and water. Plastic and glass containers are acceptable.

To a 100 ml sample placed in a 150 ml Griffin beaker, 3 ml of conc'd HNO3 is added. The beaker is covered with a ribbed watch glass or equivalent. Evaporation is done on a hot plate until the volume falls to a low volume (5 ml). The sample must never be allowed to go dry, or the sample must be discarded.

The beaker is cooled, and another 3 ml conc'd HNO3 is added. A nonribbed watch glass is placed on top and the sample is heated so that refluxing occurs gently. Digestion is continued (add more acid if necessary) until the digestate is light in color or the sample does not change appearance with continued refluxing.

Remove the watch glass and let the volume evaporate to 3 ml (but never to dryness). The beaker is cooled. 1:1 HCl is then added to 10% volume (10 ml per 100 ml material). The beaker is covered and then refluxed an additional 15 min.

The container is removed and then permitted to cool. The sample is then filtered or centrifuged, as necessary to remove silicates or insoluble material that might clog an instrument nebulizer. Prerinse all filters and apparatus with dilute HNO3 prior to use, to prevent sample contamination.

The volume is readjusted what it was (100 ml) prior to the hot acid digestion.

Controls. A blank of water should be processed in a like manner to assure quality of analysis. In addition, known standards might be employed.
