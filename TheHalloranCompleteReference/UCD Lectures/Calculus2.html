Finding the distance from a varying speed

Suppose a rocket is changing speed from the point of its launch, and the knowledge of the distance it has traveled at any point in time is desired.

If the speed is t2 feet pser second, how far does the rocket travel in, say, the first half second?

If the rocket were moving at a constant speed, the distance traveled could be known with certainty. But since the speed is continually varying, it cannot be determined exactly. Instead, we might estimate the least and the most it may have traveled.

After 3 seconds, the distance traveled may be 0 × 3 = 0 for when the speed is actually 0, or it might be 9 × 3 = 27 feet, which is the most it may have traveled.

If the time interval of 3 seconds is divided into half-second intervals and evaluated, perhaps the result has more accuracy.

During the first half second, that is from t = 0 to t = ½, the least speed it has moved would be t2 feet per second which is 0 × ½ at t = 0, and the most it has traveled is (½)2 × ½ = 1/8.

From t = ½ to t = 1, we know that at most the rocket has gone at least 1/8 foot, evaluated in the first, and (1)2 × ½ = ½ foot.

If the evaluation continues for half-second intervals up to 3 seconds, then after 3 seconds, the rocket has at least traveled:

0 + 1/8 + 4/8 + 9/8 + 16/8 + 25/8 = 55/8 = 6.875 feet

and has traveled at most

1/8 + 4/8 + 9/8 + 16/8 + 25/8 + 36/8 = 91/8 = 11.375 feet

Thus the range has been narrowed from 0 to 27 feet to being between 6.875 and 11.375 feet.

If we evalute that 3 second period in thirty 0.1-second intervals, we will eventually see that the rocket now has traveled at least 8.565 feet and at most 9.455 feet, an even more narrow range getting close to the actual value.

This process demonstrate the second most important concept in calculus, which is to evaluate the definite integral.

Functions, Graphs, The Slope of A Line

A set is any collection of objects or number whose members are called elements.
