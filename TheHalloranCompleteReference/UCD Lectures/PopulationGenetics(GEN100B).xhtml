Hardy-Weinberg Theorem (cont.)

Laws for Equilibrium

In order to have equilibrium, certain assumptions must be made:

  1. no mutation
  2. no migration
  3. no selection
  4. no small populations
  5. no preferential mating

The first three specify how allele frequencies and genotype frequencies change.

In reality, equilibrium is not obtained because these assumptions necessary for equilibrium are not met. The following specify the violations.

Violation of Equilibrium

Mutation. Mutation always occur because DNA replication dynamics is never observed to be perfect. Therefore, allelic frequencies will change.

Suppose two alleles A1 and A2. [equation]

The life cycle being with generation t with zygotes and allele frequency qt. [figure]

Random mating does not change allele frequency but mutations do. The rate of conversion between alleles is seen here. qt′ = qt + 1 because of gametic allelic frequency. [figure]

A mutation should be considered a rare event. Also qt′ does not differ from qt. Typical, the mutation rate μ will be 10−5 or 10−6, which is 1 event in 100,000 or 1 million events, or in the case of DNA, 1 base in 100,000 or 1 million bases.

Now suppose a mutation occurs mostly in one direction. If [math symbol], that is if A1 mostly goes to A2, and less from A2 to A1, then qt = f(A2) = 1. If the populations begins with all alleles being A2, or q0 = 1), then with a mutation rate still regarding A2 → A1, then q1 < 1.
