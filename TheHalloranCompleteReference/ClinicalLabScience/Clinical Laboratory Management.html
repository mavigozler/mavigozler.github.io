Current Issues in Laboratory Management

Management And Organizational Theory

Management or administration is the organization and control of the operations of a business or a sector of a business, or the working with and through people to accomplish a common mission.

Theories on management/leadership. One factor that impacts leadership success is management style. There are primarily five leadership theories:

  1. Tannenbaum and Schmidt theory. Styles can be plotted on a continuum from authoritative to democratic.
  2. McGregor X and Y theory. McGregor X theory relates to authoritative leader, whereas the Y theory relates to the democratic leader
  3. Blake and Mouton theory. Describes five types of management situations:
    1. impoverished management: low concern for people and production
    2. authority-compliance: low concern for people, but high concern for production
    3. middle-of-the-road management: medium concern for people and production
    4. country club management. high concern for people, but low concern for production
    5. team management: high concern for people and production
  4. Fielder theory. This theory indicates that the style used by the leader may vary according to the situation, with a very favorable or very unfavorable situation requiring a task-oriented leader, and a moderately favorable or moderately unfavorable situation requiring a relationship-oriented leader.
  5. Hersey-Blanchard theory. Four leadership situations:
    1. employee new to job
    2. employee has mastered some of the job, but needs supervision
    3. employee has mastered the job, but needs verification
    4. employee has mastered the job and is confident

Managers need following skills:

  • organizational skills
    • conceptualize and apply management process
    • systematize work flow
    • make decisions
    • communicate with coworkers
  • people skills: understand basic theories of human needs and work motivation
  • financial skills: effective use of and accounting for monetary assets of the company
  • technical skills: involves the synthesis of the first three skills above and the management of physical resources, namely supplies, equipment, and facilities into the operational parameters, which are products and services

Relevant management theories:

  • scientific management
    Proposed by Frederick Taylor, this is a systematic or scientific approach to studying the structure, function (goals/mission) of organization, applying a cause-and-effect analysis, breaking down each task into segments to improve efficiency
  • bureaucracy management
    Organizational structure and function of company is examined to explain how institutions function and improve structural process. The hierarchical chain of command is an example. Special attention is given to the bureaucratic characteristics as they relate to rules, regulations, impersonality, and division of labor.
  • behavioral science
    focuses on the performance and interaction of people within the organization by utilizing psychology and sociology, as well as management theories
  • systems analysis
    This is an outgrowth of management science, which views the organization as a continuous process interacting within itself and with its environment. This concept includes Peter Drucker's five basic management operations:
    1. setting objectives
    2. organizing
    3. motivating and communicating
    4. establishing standards, including measurements of performance
    5. developing people, including managers

The process of management is a concept of management as a continuous process of interacting functions, each dependent on the success of the other. The four main functions are

  1. planning
  2. organizing
  3. directing
  4. controlling

Management by objectives (MBO). This program embodies the four functions of management process described above (planning, operating, directing, controlling) that were introduced by Peter Drucker in the 1960s. MBO core is communications. Success or failure depends on how well employees understand the company's mission and objectives. Managers who allow employees to share in the decision-making process will have effective MBO programs. Managers must understand and be skilled at writing and monitoring objectives.

Motivation is the driving forces by which people act as they do. Could also be described by alternative words: incentives, motives, purpose, drive, desires, and needs. There aret hree major theories relevant to motivation:

  1. Maslow's hierarchy of needs. These are needs based on a physiological or healthy need (working allows personal survival: housing and nutrition), and this has priority over another need described by Maslow, which is a social need, in which the individual is motivated by achieving esteem or being loved. or love. This has priority over any social pressure that is a motivating factor.
  2. Herzberg's two-factor theory. Herzberg (1959) described two factors as components of motivation:
    1. those described by Maslow is the one part: basic needs, hygiene factors, corresponding with physical and social needs
    2. motivators corresponding primarily with Maslow's needs for growth
  3. Vroom's expectancy theory. Namely effort brings reward. The actual effort expended will be related to the perceived value of the reward. People make a choice between alternative courses of actions according to how they perceive the resulting benefits.

Professionalism

This is a judgment of the quality with which one practices the profession. It also includes characteristics such as altruism, honor, integrity, caring, compassion, respect, responsibility, accountability, scholarship, and leadership.

Professional ethics is the study of standards of conduct and moral judgments which provide a basis for right and wrong conduct. The American Society for Clinical Laboratory Science (ASCLS) has developed and published the Code of Ethics for Clinical Laboratory Science comprised of duties to patient, colleagues, and society.

Employee Hiring, Evaluation and Discipline

The job description defines the functions and purpose of a specific position a member of the organization. It should feature the following:

  • name of organization
  • job title
  • summary of duties for the position
  • essential functions required for the position
  • immediate supervisor (chain of command)
  • working conditions/environment
  • required qualifications and experience

The resume is a means for an applicant to demonstrate strengths, education, and accomplishments when seeking employment. The resume should include the following:

  1. applicant name (legal)
  2. contact information (including e-mail)
  3. career objective
  4. educational qualifications
  5. clinical experience
  6. military experience
  7. certifications
  8. professional society memberships
  9. honors
  10. publications/research activities/special projects
  11. volunteer work
  12. statement regarding references

A cover letter should accompany a resume to focus attention on the applicant. It is best to address the cover letter to a specific individual.

The purpose of the interview process is for confirming information is valid in resume and/or job application. It also assesses applicant's interpersonal communication skills. The interview also provides the candidate with the opportunity to discuss information in more detail and to ask questions pertinent to the specific position.

The selection process should be conducted after the interview and should include an evaluation of the candidate's resume/application and information from the interview. The manager is seeking to select the candidate with the appropriate skills, certifications, and personality to fit the institution and the position.

Employee evaluation process is a method that both recognizes excellence and determines areas for development and training. The job evaluation is directly linked to the job description and should be formative and summarizing. It should also be done frequently to provide feedback and direction for the employee.

  • Criteria-based evaluations indicate specific performance expectations that are linked to the job description.
  • Competency-based evaluations are used to validate competency of skills.

The purpose of corrective action is to provide ab employee with specific goals to enhance their effectiveness and performance. There are three steps:

  1. Step 1: verbal counseling alerts the employee to the situation and opens lines of communication
  2. Step 2: a verbal warning involves a statement of the problem, performance in question, and offering resources and assistance. This process should be documented.
  3. Step 3: a formal written warning that states the problem, expected improvement in performance, and a timeline for re-evaluation of performance. This document is signed by the immediate supervisor and employee. Failure to meet the stated requirements typically results in dismissal of the employee.

Note that most institutions have a policy that allows the employee to seek an appeal of the situation to a higher level.

Communication and interpersonal relationships

Communication is an ongoing process of interpreting symbols, decoding speech and phrases, interpreting ideas and feelings, and encoding that information to formulate an appropriate response. Communication typically serves the following purposes:

  • exchange of knowledge
  • expression of feelings
  • expression of ideas and thoughts
  • attempt to influence another's thoughts and actions
  • meeting social expectations

Barriers to effective communication are:

  • nonverbal expressions; body language
  • cultural differences; unfamiliar phrases
  • paraverbal issues: tone and pitch of voice
  • environmental–temperature, neatness of appearance
  • overcommunication: too much information offered at one time
  • undercommunication: not enough information provided for understanding the issue
  • time issues

Team building is essential for optimal employee performance and effectiveness of the section, department, and institution. Tuckman (1965) suggests the following phases of team development:

  • identifying the team members
  • team members selecting their roles
  • determining standards of behavior and expectations
  • working together towards a common goal

Quality Assurance

Quality assurance (QA) is a comprehensive set of policies, procedures, and practices that are followed to ensure that a laboratory's results are reliable. QA evaluates the quality of the services provided. and it provides a way to prevent problems as well as deal with problems that occur.

It differs from quality control (QC) in that QC is included as part of a QA program. QC ensures that a particular test method is working properly and that results of that test are reliable.

QA includes record keeping, maintenance and calibration of equipment, proficiency testing, quality control, and training of personnel. QA is a commitment to quality. Physician and patient relations are a part of this plan.

Quality Assurance Plan

A QA plan has the following:

  • All policies and procedures are in writing and available.
  • Staff performance is routinely evaluated.
  • Continuing education is used and documented
  • A safety program is in place for both staff and patients.
    • Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) standards are met.
    • Hazardous materials are identified and labeled.
    • Material Safety Data Sheets (MSDS) are available for each hazardous material.
    • Employee safety training is documented.
    • Records of any accidents (e.g., needle stick) are kept and reported to appropriate authors.
    • Hazardous waste is properly discarded.
    • Universal precautions are strictly followed
    • Safety equipment (e.g., fire extinguishers, fire blankets, safety showers, goggles) is available to staff, and training in the use of this equipment is performed and documented.
    • Electrical checks of all equipment are routinely performed.
  • Specimen collection and handling (pre-analytical) as well as correct patient preparation follows written guidelines and is documented.
  • A quality control (QC) program is in place for lab testing (analytical). This ensures that the instruments, reagents, and personnel are functioning properly. A QC program comprises several areas:
    • A written QC program is required that specifies:
      • frequency of performing controls
      • number of controls to be used
      • type of controls
      • acceptable limits for control results
      • corrective action required if controls are out of range
    • QC plots of control results are used to detect:
      • shifts: a sudden upward or downward change of four or more consecutive values
      • trends: the tendency of results to gradually increase or decrease over a period of time
    • Equipment maintenance is performed and documented.
    • temperatures of incubators, certain instruments, and the room are controlled and documented.
  • Checklists are used to ensure that scheduled activities and duties are performed.
  • The laboratory must participate in and document results of proficiency testing (PT). This allows comparison of results from a number of laboratories. Specimens from PT sponsors are to be treated exactly like patient specimens.
  • The laboratory must be accredited by an appropriate agency. This gives official approval and states that the laboratory follows all guidelines set forth by the accrediting agency. Inspectors representing the accrediting agency view records, documents, and visit the laboratory.

Laboratory Regulations

Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments of 1988 (CLIA 88)

CLIA 88 is intended to establish regulations for all laboratories, regardless of size or location, where clinical testing is performed for the purpose of diagnosis, treatment, or monitoring of patients' health. The law was published in the February 28, 1992 issue of the Federal Register, and became effective for all laboratories on September 1, 1992. Modifications to the law list the complexity of tests. Additional modifications define alternative routes for personnel qualifications.

Laboratory Accreditation. All laboratories must have a CLIA certificate to perform testing and receive federal reimbursement. Certification is based on the level of test complexity.

Sites performing only waived tests comprise 58% (105,138) of the approximately 180,000 laboratory testing sites in the United States. Waived testing performed in these sites is often wellness testing, screening tests, or other critical testing that introduces a large population of persons into the health care setting. Although the testing performed in Certificate of Waiver sites accounts for <10% of the total U.S. testing volume, this percentage has been increasing each year since the CLIA program began. Types of direct, unprocessed specimens suitable for waived testing are listed below:

  • whole blood (fingerstick or anticoagualted blood collected via venipuncture)
    glucose, cholesterol, prothrombin time, infectious mononucleosis, erythrocyte sedimentation rate, hemoglobin-copper sulfate method, spun hematocrit, HIV antibody
  • urine
    dipstick urinalysis and pregnancy test (hCG)
  • throat swab
    Group A streptococcal antigen
  • nasopharyngeal swab
    influenza
  • stool
    occult blood
  • saliva
    alcohol
  • oral fluid
    HIV antibody
  • gastric biopsy
    H. pylori

Provider-performed microscopy (PPM) includes:

  • wet mount, including preparations of vaginal, cervical or skin specimens
  • potassium hydroxide (KOH) preparation
  • pinworm slides
  • Fern test
  • urine sediment
  • vaginal smears
  • nasal smear examinations for granulocytes
  • fecal leukocyte examinations
  • semen analysis: qualitative examination, presence or absence, motility

Moderately complex testing includes:

  • hematology
  • chemistry
  • serology
  • coagulation
  • therapeutic drug monitoring
  • blood gases
  • urine colony counts
  • blood type and Rh factor
  • limited immunology
  • manual differential: limited identification; normal cells only
  • Gram's stain: urethral for gas chromatography (GC), cervical for GC
  • throat culture screen only: hemolysis, bacitracin, strep select agar
  • microscopic urinalysis (non-PPM)
  • rapid kits

Highly complex testing includes:

  • microbiology
  • immunohematology
  • bacteriology
  • cytology
  • histopathology
  • mycology
  • manual hematology counts
  • manual differential: identification of abnormal cells, complete identification, non-automated chemistry tests

The certificate fee and biennial inspection fee, which are based on the size of the laboratory and number of tests performed annually, must be paid. Accreditation by a private organization is an alternative option for certification.

The Health Care Financing Administration (HCFA) may deem a laboratory to meet all of the CLIA requirements if the laboratory is accredited by a private, nonprofit organization that is certified as having deemed status. This means that the certifying agency must:

  • provide reasonable assurance to HCFA that it requires the laboratories it accredits to meet all of the requirements equivalent to the CLIA requirements in the law
  • inspect its laboratories per condition level requirements of the law

Approved state laboratory programs are responsible for accreditation of laboratories in their state, if this is a requirement of the department of health of that state.

Personnel requirements must be met by laboratories performing moderately or highly complex testing. These positions can be held by the same person or by different persons who meet the qualifications.

Moderately complex laboratories must have the following personnel:

  • Laboratory director
  • Technical consultant
  • Clinical consultant
  • Testing personnel
    Each person performing moderately complex testing must follow laboratory procedures, maintain records, adhere to policies, identify problems, document corrective actions, testing personnel must have documented training in all areas of testing performed.

Highly complex laboratories are required to have the same personnel as a moderately complex laboratory, but must also have:

  • General supervisor
  • Technical supervisor

Quality Control

Must be implemented in all operations performing moderately and highly complex testing. General QC procedures are performed on all specialties and subspecialties as specified within the Federal Register. Procedures not found in the Federal Register must be conducted according to the manufacturer's protocol that has been approved by the Food and Drug Administration

The laboratory must establish and follow written QC procedures for monitoring and evaluating the test process to assure accuracy and reliability of results. There must be documentation of all calibration procedures.

  • A minimum of two levels of control materials are run on each day of testing.
  • All QC records must be retained for 2 years.
  • QC must be tested in a manner that provides results within the laboratory's stated performance requirements.
  • The laboratory must have instruments, reagents, and supplies sufficient for the type and volume of tests performed.
  • There must be defined criteria for proper storage of reagents and specimens.
  • A procedure for remedial actions must be established when QC fails.
  • All reagents, solutions, medias, QC materials, and standards must be labeled.
  • Different lot numbers are not to be interchanged unless specified by the manufacturer.

Proficiency testing (PT) is mandatory for all moderately and highly complex tests listed on the Compiled list of clinical laboratory systems, assays, and examinations categorized by complexity, published in the Federal Register. There are numerous CMS-approved PT programs available. Laboratories receive between three and five challenges for each analyte that requires PT. Challenges are received three times a year. The same personnel who routinely perform the test procedures must perform the PT. Laboratories are required to score 80% on all analytes to be considered in compliance. Blood bank specimens require a score of 100%.

A procedures manual must be written to include all testing methods used in the laboratory. It must be available and followed by all testing personnel. The manual must include:

  • the principle and methodology of each test
  • criteria for specimen collection, processing, and rejection (pre-analytical steps)
  • instructions for microscopic examination procedures
  • test procedures (analytical), including step-by-step instructions and result interpretation
  • preparation of materials, including of required solutions (e.g., reagents, controls, calibrators, stains, slides) and their location; step-by-step preparation of all materials needed; storage after preparation; labeling and dating instructions; safety precautions needed; solutions used in preparation (e.g., deionized water, diluent)
  • calibration procedures, including step-by-step instructions, verifications of calibration results, concentration and number of calibrators used. and calibration schedule
  • control procedures, including materials used, name, lot number, level, frequency used; reparation of materials; instructions for testing controls; control limits; description of how and where control results are recorded; corrective actions taken when controls are not within limits
  • actions taken when results deviate from expected values: recalibration, troubleshooting, repeats, dilutions
  • limitations or cautions of the procedure, including both positive and negative interfering substances (how they interfere) and (other) common sources of error
  • result reporting (post-analytical): reference ranges especially for range for different specimen types (e.g., serum, plasma, urine), for demographic variables (e.g., age, sex); panic values: what are life-threatening results or results needing special attention (critical results)
  • method or procedure references: manufacturer's product literature (e.g., inserts, manuals) and lit refs in textbooks, journals, etc.
  • specimen retention: period allowed for specimen to be consider valuid, and proper storage conditions.
  • system for reporting patient results (post-analytical): understanding unacceptable results (follow QC specifications); how reporting is done to requesting entity; protocol for panic and critical values
  • course of action for problems...solving problems...troubleshooting
  • specimen referral: testing personnel or section, and how specimen is handled, and how results are to be received

The manual or specific procedure in the manual is not approved until:

  • signed off by the laboratory director
  • reviewed by all personnel using the procedure
  • reviewed and updated by the supervisor or director

The manual must be rewritten when new procedures are implemented. The manual must be retained for future reference.

A quality assurance (QA) plan must be established and followed to monitor and evaluate the quality of tests.

  • The program must be an ongoing process.
  • It must evaluate all facets of the testing process.
  • The program must extend to the interactions of other health care professionals in the ordering of tests.
  • It must allow for actions to be taken to correct errors or problems.
  • The program must monitor and evaluate: specimen requirements, collection, handling and processing, corrective actions taken in QC and PT, result reporting, employee competence
  • All QA activities must be documented and evaluated monthly.

Inspections are conducted every 2 years by the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) or its designee. Laboratory inspectors have the right to:

  • interview all employees
  • access all areas of a facility
  • observe all testing
  • review all information and data records
  • determine that the laboratory is operated in a safe manner
  • determine that the laboratory is performing tests only within their complexity category
  • evaluate any complaints from the public

The inspectors may also:

  • conduct unannounced inspections at any time during hours of operation
  • ask personnel to perform testing on test samples that the inspector supplies
  • reinspect a laboratory at any time to evaluate accurate and reliable results

Sanctions may be imposed on laboratories that are found to be out of compliance with one or more of the conditions for Medicare coverage.

  • Suspension, limitations, or revocation of a certificate can be imposed.
  • A civil suit can be brought against a laboratory to forbid testing.
  • Imprisonment or fines can be levied against persons convicted of violations.
  • HHS publishes annually a list of laboratories that have been sanctioned.

Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA)

Regulations for Occupational Exposure to Bloodborne Pathogens require laboratories to establish workplace safety practices to prevent accidental exposure to etiologic agents in blood.

The regulations limit occupational exposure to blood and other potentially infectious materials that can transmit bloodborne pathogens and include but are not limited to: hepatitis B virus (HBV) human immunodeficiency virus (HIV)

The basis for all preventive measures is the practice of universal precautions. These are recommendations from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). Laboratory personnel must treat all human blood and other potentially infectious body fluids as if they were infected with HIV, HBV, and other bloodborne pathogens, and this includes materials that are contaminated with blood.

A written exposure control plan must be in place that describes the procedures established to minimize employee exposure to bloodborne pathogens. There must be identification of job classification, tasks, and procedures in which there is the potential of exposure. A determination must be made of the job types in which exposure could occur. All tasks must be included in the job description.

An explanation of personal protective equipment (PPE) available and in use must be included. Appropriate protective equipment must be supplied to employees at no cost to them. Equipment is considered appropriate only if it does not permit blood or other potentially infectious materials (PIMs) to pass through to an employee's clothes, skin, mouth, or other mucous membranes. Types of PPE are gloves, gowns, laboratory coats, face shields or masks, and eye protection. All employers are responsible for providing, maintaining, laundering, disposing of, replacing, and assuring the proper use of all PPE.

Engineering controls must be in place to minimize exposure. These are devices that isolate or remove the pathogen hazard from the workplace. These devices must be made available to all employees. Examples of engineering controls include:

  1. sharps containers and biosafety bags
  2. splash guards and self-sheathing needles
  3. biosafety cabinets and fume hoods
  4. mechanical pipetting devices
  5. hand washing facilities, eyewash stations, and showers

Work practices must be developed to reduce or eliminate employee exposure to blood or PIMs during the execution of their work tasks. Employees must fully understand these procedures. The procedures must be implemented, when appropriate. Work practice procedures include, but are not limited to:

  • hand washing
  • no recapping of contaminated needles: immediate discard?
  • no food or drink allowed in the laboratory
  • no mouth pipetting
  • warning labels affixed where needed, and biohazard symbols placed appropriately
  • a dress code that ensures safety

Housekeeping procedures are necessary for maintaining a clean and sanitary work site, which is a responsibility of employers. All equipment and work surfaces must be decontaminated. Sharps containers must be puncture resistant. Containers for biohazard materials must be constructed to contain all contents and prevent leakage during handling, storage, transport, and shipping. Procedure for disposal of biohazard materials will be based on federal, state, and local regulations. All housekeeping personnel will handle contaminated materials with the use of PPE.

An HBV vaccination program must be established. HBV vaccine and vaccination series must be made available to all employees who are identified as being exposed to PIMs. The vaccination series will be offered within 10 days of the employee being assigned to an area of possible exposure. The vaccination series must be offered at no cost to the employee. The program must be established using the protocol outlined in the Federal Register. The program must include procedures for evaluation of the circumstances of an exposure incident to prevent further incidents. Exemptions to the HBV vaccination program are:

  • employees who have already completed the HBV series
  • employees whose antibody tests reveal an immunity
  • employees for whom vaccination is contraindicated for medical reasons

Employees who decline the HBV series must sign the declination statement published in Addendum A of the Federal Register. All records are part of the employee's permanent file and are confidential information.

Postexposure and follow-up evaluations are available at no cost to any employee who sustains an exposure.

The communication of hazards to employees is required by OSHA regulations. All employees must receive free biosafety training during working hours. Training sessions must be presented in a way that can be clearly understood. These sessions must be designed for the particular situation and institution involved. Generic information and signing of a statement will not satisfy the requirements. Instructors must be knowledgeable and must demonstrate use and location of all safety devices, document all training, ensure that employees understand how to cope with hazards they may come in contact with during their employment.

A record-keeping policy must be in place. All documentation of training and exposure incidents must be a part of each employee's permanent record. All files are confidential information. Medical records are transferred when an employee leaves. All records are kept on file for the duration of employment plus 30 years.

OSHA regulations for Occupational Exposures to Hazardous Chemicals in Laboratories are published in the Federal Register. The laboratory must develop a systematic approach to significant risk determination. The standard defines employee as an individual employed in a laboratory workplace who may be exposed to hazardous chemicals in the course of his or her assignments. The standard defines toxic or hazardous chemicals as any substance that has the capacity to produce personal injury or illness to man through ingestion, inhalation, or absorption through any body surface.

The significant risk findings must be based on the following factors:

  • epidemiological information relating to disease and mortality rates
  • evidence that shows significant risk for specific substances that are used in the laboratory
  • general recognition that safe work practices are necessary to prevent adverse health effects
  • reported information about adverse health effects resulting from exposures to substances commonly used in the laboratory
  • relevant policy considerations

Exposure monitoring of employees is required only when there is reason to believe that permissible exposure limits (PEL) or action levels for a substance are routinely exceeded. The standard has a provision for a written chemical hygiene plan (CHP) to be formulated and implemented by the employer. Under the law, employees have the right to know any operations in their work area where hazardous chemicals are present. The location and availability of the written hazard communication program and material safety data sheet (MSDS) are required. A designated chemical hygiene officer (CHO) oversees the development, implementation, and administration of the CHP. The duties of the CHO include:

  • developing a standard operating procedure that is relevant to the safety and health of persons who work with hazardous chemicals (This need not be developed for each chemical. A generic approach that groups chemicals by their physical and chemical properties is acceptable.)
  • setting criteria that will be used by the laboratory to determine the need for and the implementation of measures to control and reduce employee exposures (The criteria should address engineering controls and PPE.)
  • describing the laboratory's employee information and training program
  • explaining the laboratory's program to provide OSHA-required medical examinations and consultations
  • designating specific operations that cannot be performed without prior approval
  • making provisions for providing additional protection for work with particularly hazardous materials, including selected carcinogens, reproductive toxins, substances with a high acute toxicity
  • labeling all chemicals to reflect the degree of hazard involved in their use

OSHA's final rule for Occupational Exposure to Formaldehyde was published on May 27, 1992. The risk of exposure in the laboratory is great without proper engineering controls and good work practices. A policy to maintain the permissible exposure limit (PEL) must be in place. PEL must be at or below 0.75 parts per million (ppm). The short-term exposure limit of 2.0 ppm per 15 minutes of exposure. The action level is 0.5 ppm. The formaldehyde exposure control plan must include

  • monitoring of employees prone to exposure
  • limitation of access to regulated areas to authorized persons who have been trained to recognize the hazards
  • establishment of identified and located engineering controls and work practices
  • provision of the necessary PPE and clothing at no cost to the employee
  • a quick-drench shower and eyewash facility located within the immediate work area
  • visual inspections for spills as a housekeeping procedure and cleaning up spills using formaldehyde-resistant clothing
  • performance by a licensed physician of all medical surveillance procedures without cost to the employee
  • training of employees at the time of employment, annually, and whenever a new exposure to formaldehyde is introduced into the work area
  • environmental monitoring record keeping for 30 years

Guidelines from OSHA for Preventing the Transmission of Mycobacterium tuberculosis in Health Care Facilities are published in the Federal Register. Each laboratory is responsible for having a policy on prevention of M. tuberculosis transmission, based on their own personnel's exposure to tuberculosis. This policy must include but is not limited to the following:

  • Control measures must include the use of administrative measures to reduce the risk of exposure to persons who have infectious tuberculosis by developing risk assessments.
  • Engineering controls include preventing the spread and reducing the concentrations of infectious droplets, as well as using personal respiratory tract protective equipment in areas where there is a risk of exposure. The most efficient of these is the high-efficiency particulate air (HEPA) filter.
  • Risk assessments must be performed to identify and manage personnel who have tuberculosis. Management includes early treatment and isolation.
  • A tuberculosis screening program for employees must be developed and must include the use of skin tests and follow-up treatment of all employees who have positive test results.
  • All employees must be trained and educated in the use of PPE, hygiene, and handling of patients who have tuberculosis.
  • The evaluation of tuberculosis infection control programs must include the effectiveness of the program and the outcome of exposures.

Three levels for preventing the transmission of tuberculosis include the following:

  1. First level is to develop and implement policies for rapid identification. If there is a suspicion of tuberculosis, there should be a way to alert all other health care workers of the possibility. Each facility must determine its own policy for rapid identification.
  2. Second level is the use of PPE and respiratory tract protection (e.g., HEPA filter) to protect health careworkers from the disease. This includes education and training programs for all employees, as well as the prompt evaluation of possible exposures to tuberculosis, including purified protein derivative skin tests and radiographs, if necessary.
  3. Third level is the coordination of activities with the local public health department. This includes the reporting of all cases, as well as continuation and completion of therapy.

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) was promulgated by the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, which addresses the use and disclosure of individuals' health information called protected health information. HIPPA created the first national standard to protect individuals' medical records and other personal health information. The major goal of the Privacy Rule is that the individual's health information is properly protected while allowing the flow of health information needed to provide and promote the public's health and well being. It applies to all health plans, health care clearinghouses, and any health care provider who transmits health information in electronic form. For patients, it means being able to make informed choices when seeking care and reimbursement for care based on how personal health information may be used. Each entity is responsible for HIPPA compliance via training and ensuring proper protocol is followed.

Financial Management

The budget process usually begins in advance of the implementation date and is prepared in phases dependent upon the fiscal year which consists of four phases.

  1. Development of goals. The goals must be in line with those of the organization and must be achievable and measurable.
  2. Budget assumptions. The organization provides a forecast of available money based on past earnings, cash flow, and changes in state or federal laws affecting reimbursement. Budget assumptions are based on annual test volume and revenue generated.
  3. Forecast of expenses. Capital expenditure is an investment and thus the rate of return must be evaluated and used as a criterion for budgeting decisions.a
  4. Monitoring. After operational budget is made and implemented, reports are reviewed monthly for variances.

Employee scheduling. One of the most challenging aspects of being a supervisor/manager, which has a huge impact on the departmental budget. Factors that impact scheduling:

  • test menu
  • education and experience Level of staff
  • number of employees needed for test volume
  • number of emergency areas served
  • turnaround time expectations (TAT)
  • level of test complexity

Health care reimbursement. Diagnostic related group (DRG) is a system to classify hospital cases into one of approximately 500 groups. The system was developed for Medicare as part of the prospective payment system. DRGs are assigned by a grouper program based on diagnoses, procedures, age, sex, and the presence of complications or comorbidities. DRGs have been used since 1983 to determine how much Medicare pays the hospital, since patients within each category are similar clinically and are expected to use the same level of hospital resources.

Current procedural terminology (CPT) is a listing of descriptive terms and identifying codes for reporting medical services and procedures. The purpose of CPT is to provide a uniform language that accurately describes medical, surgical, and diagnostic services, and thereby serves as an effective means for reliable nationwide communication among physicians, and other health care providers, patients, and third parties. This system of terminology is the most widely accepted medical nomenclature used to report medical procedures and services under public and private health insurance programs.

Medicare is a social insurance program administered by the United States government, providing health insurance coverage to people who are either age 65 and over, or who meet other special criteria.

Medicaid is the United States health program for individuals and families with low incomes and resources. It is an entitlement program that is jointly funded by the states and federal government, and is managed by the states. Among the groups of people served by Medicaid are eligible low-income parents, children, seniors, and people with disabilities.

Third-party payment is the practice of an insurer paying providers directly for services rendered to an insured, as opposed to an indemnity contract that pays the insured person for the services incurred.

Laboratory Information Systems (LIS)

The benefits of an LIS are:

  • Reduces time-consuming paper work
  • Stores patient data
  • Stores QC data
  • Provides immediate access to results
  • Reduces the chance of an assay being ran on the wrong patient when barcode is used
  • Managerial reports (tests per month, workload units, TATs)

The minimum requirements for LIS are:

  • Access codes or passwords per user for access to system
  • Assign a unique accession number
  • Provide a unique file for each assay (sample type, volume required, ranges)
  • Record time collected, received, and unique identifier of who received it, and who released results
  • Print bar-code labels (ISBT for immunohematology/transfusion services)
  • Interface with instruments and other information systems

  • amount of heat generated

  • linearity
  • sensitivity
  • specificity
  • accuracy

  • stability of hardware/software (bar coding and ID features)

Delivery Of Education Unit

A goal is a general statement which describes the instructor's purpose for a particular instructional activity. Objectives are statements that describe the specific learning outcomes of an instructional activity and are written in greater detail than goals.

  • cognitive: intellectual learning outcome such as the recall of information, the comprehension of that information, and the processes of analysis, synthesis, and evaluation
  • affective: emphasize values, attitudes, and interests
  • psychomotor: require neuromuscular coordination such as performing a task

Learning activities allow the student to master the objectives and are comprised of lectures, laboratory session, demonstration, computer assisted instruction, Internet resources, tutorials, and online courses. Evaluation determines the effectiveness of the learning activity and provides feedback regarding the learner's attainment of the objectives for the course.

Outreach Program

Physicians are treating patients more often on an outpatient basis; therefore, outpatient volumes can potentially reach upwards of 60% of a laboratory's total test volume. The following Added-value services must therefore be considered:

  • phlebotomy
  • turn around times (TATs): outpatient volumes can cause TATs to increase for inpatient testing
  • supplies
  • courier service
  • reports: electronic, hand delivered, or both
  • customer service staff memmbers: knowledge of the outreach program and its goals are critical

Know your competition. In particular, do SWOT analysis: strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats. One of the most important aspects of an outreach program is also to be aware of pricing.

Quality Assessment

Pre-Analytical

Pre-analytical quality assurance involves variables such as:
  • Proper patient identification
  • Procedures for proper labeling of specimen/sample containers
  • Checklist/procedure for proper sample/specimen collection (e.g., correct anticoagulant in tube)
  • Procedures for proper mixing of collected samples with reagents (stabilizing or analyte-preserving)
  • Timely delivery to analytical site
  • Specimens evaluated for abnormal collection (e.g., clots in blood tubes)
  • Interfering substances (medications) present in the sample/patient
  • Prior confounding medical procedures (e.g., blood transfusions, IV line contamination)

Quality Control

Statistically good quality control generally begins by reading at least 20 control samples and establishing precision. Normal or Reference Intervals must be established for each quantitative assay or method, and a sample size of 25 from clinically healthy male and female adults represents that principle, in addition to establishing a pediatric population.
