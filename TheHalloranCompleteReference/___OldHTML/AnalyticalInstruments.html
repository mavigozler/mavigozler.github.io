Operational Amplifiers

The diagram will illustrate the inputs and outputs. ε− and ε+ are input potentials and Vi represents the difference between these potentials. +PS and −PS refer to the power supply to the amplifier (usu. +15 and −15 V dc). The amplifier has a gain (β) in which the signal multiplicaiton factor is understood. The output voltage will therefore be Vo = −βVi. Zi and Zo represent the input and output impedance to the amplifier. Most opamps are self-contained units containing ~30 transistors and resistors put into an IC chip. Capacitors and diodes may also be part of the setup. The properties shared by most if not all opamps are:
  1. large open-loop gains (β = 104 to 106)
  2. high input impedances (Zi ≥ 106 Ω)
  3. low output impedances (Zo ≤ 1 to 10 Ω)
In addition, they should provide zero output for zero input (ideally < 0.1 mV output), but the zero output voltage can be obtained by use of an offset trimming system that reduces the voltage.

Measurement of voltages for both the input voltages and the output voltage are made with respect to a common ground in the circuit (the ground potential). This is essentially a conductor providing a common return of all currents to their sources. The ε− and ε+ do not represent how voltage potentials are to be connected but represent how potentials to the opamp are to be treated. Potentials connected to ε− will be inverted (a positive becomes negative, a negative a postive, and ac voltages are 180° out of phase), will those connected to ε+ are unchanged with respect to polarity.
