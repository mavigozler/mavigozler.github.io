CONDUCTING EFFECTIVE MEETINGS

Like most accounting professionals, you undoubtedly find yourself attending many meetings throughout your work week--from department get-togethers to discussions focused on specific company initiatives. While many of these meetings are productive, there have likely been times when you've questioned why you were asked to attend.

In a recent survey by our company, executives reported that they waste 7.8 hours per week, or 2.3 months per year, in unnecessary meetings. So what can you do to ensure you don't create this type of situation when it's your turn to lead a meeting? Here are some tips:

  • Determine whether the meeting is necessary. Is there a compelling reason to have one? Every meeting should have a purpose that everyone can relate to and support--even if it's simply to bring people together for brainstorming or team-building exercises. Consider whether the issue at hand can be addressed effectively through informal gatherings or e-mail communication instead.
  • Select the right location. While a conference room may be the appropriate venue for some meetings, it may not be the best option for others. For example, if your goal is to generate new ideas to improve your company's accounting procedures, an off-site location may provide a more creative environment. Or if you want to encourage a casual discussion, consider holding the meeting in a less formal place such as someone's office or an outside courtyard.
  • Focus on the primary objective when selecting participants. Determine whose job responsibilities will be impacted most by the topic of discussion. Can anyone else provide valuable input? Is attendance critical, or would a memo detailing the meeting's outcome suffice?

Keep in mind that some people may feel slighted if they're not asked to attend. When in doubt, explain the purpose of the meeting and let the individual decide whether to participate.

  • Create a written agenda. Provide attendees with an outline before the meeting so they can prepare to address key issues. An agenda will help ensure that your meeting about year-end financial reporting requirements doesn't stray into a discussion about what people plan to bring to the company picnic.
  • Be punctual. Unless there's a critical reason for a delay, always start your meetings on time. When people arrive late and see that the discussion hasn't even started yet, there's little incentive for them to show up on time for future meetings. Also be sure to end at the appointed time. If important issues still need to be reviewed by the end of the meeting, offer attendees the option of staying or rescheduling at another time.
  • Take notes. Make sure someone writes down key points brought up during the meeting. These notes will serve as a reminder of action items and a summary of what happened for those who couldn't attend.
  • Solicit input from everyone. As the facilitator, it's your responsibility to lead the discussion and encourage participation from those who may not otherwise express their views during meetings.
  • Keep the group focused. While you don't want to discourage creativity, make sure that attendees follow the agenda items. If topics come up that require significant time for debate, consider whether they can be addressed at another meeting. Remember, your goal is to achieve the objectives you've identified for this particular discussion.
  • Reassess how you conduct meetings. Recognize that what worked well for you in the past may not be the best method under current circumstances. For instance, now that a particular project is coming to an end, it may no longer be essential to hold weekly meetings with all participants. Ask others for feedback on how you can make your meetings more productive and effective.
  • Monitor how the group interacts. Try to pay attention to how the attendees work together. If someone seems to have concerns about any aspect of the meeting, talk with him or her individually afterward. Also, take note of any problems with the overall team dynamic. If the group will be meeting regularly, it may be useful to bring in an outside facilitator where practical to resolve any conflicts so future discussions can progress smoothly.

Companies today are encouraging interdepartmental communication through greater use of project teams while also demanding heightened employee productivity and efficiency. This makes it critical to know how to manage group discussions. By giving careful consideration to each discussion and taking the time to evaluate which techniques work best for you, you can help everyone get the most out of meetings.
