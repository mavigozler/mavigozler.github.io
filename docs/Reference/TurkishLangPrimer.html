Alphabet

The Turkish language used to be expressed in written form by literate society using the Arabic script. This influence is obvious from the time that the Turks adopted the religion of Islam, whose holy sayings are expressed in Arabic.

In 1923, along with other reforms that dictated costuming and the manner of dress, Mustafa Kemal Atatük, the founder of the modern Republic of Turkey, used his will to promulgate laws that Romanized the alphabet of the Turkish language. One reason given was that all of the consonant and vowel sounds of the language could be symbolized by the glyphs. Another reason was Atatük's desire that Turks do more to look as much to the Occident as to the Orient in the modernization of their culture.

Nouns

Cases

Six cases are used in Turkish: nominative (absolute), accusative (definite objective), genitive (possessive), dative, ablative, and locative. The case of a noun is distinguished as a suffix. In the absolute case, there is no ending to the noun. In the definite objective and genitive, four endings are possible, the ending reflecting vowel harmony. The dative (the "whither" sense), ablative (the "whence" sense), and locative (the "where" sense) take either one of two endings in each case, again dependent upon vowel harmony.

e or i ö or ü a or ı o or u

Definite objective (Accusative) -(y)i -(y)ü -(y)i -(y)u

Ablative from, out of -den/-ten -dan/-tan

In the definite object , genitive, and dative cases, which employ a vowel suffix, the suffixes employ some sort of "buffer" consonant in the event the noun ends in a vowel in order to distinguish the vowels. Note also that in the locative and ablative cases, the suffix is begins with a consonant-either voiced (d) if the last syllable of the noun ends in either a vowel or voiced consonant or voiceless (t) if the last syllable ends in a voiceless consonant.

Vowel length

Arabic has the greatest influence in Turkish, and its pecularities of speech are reflected in the spoken word. With some Arabic-derived words, the original long vowel is used in the last syllable, especially when a vowel is added. The vowel is shortened otherwise. The common words are cevap, lüzum, zaman, hayat, mahkum.

Word Order

The subject usually comes at the beginning, the verb virtually always at the end. For the rest, nouns with a definite nature precede nouns of an indefinite sense. Prepositions in English are postpositions in Turkish and reflect the noun case. Adjectives always precede the noun they modify. In some cases, a noun or word which comes just before the verb at the end may be out of its usual position as a matter of emphasis.

Articles

Definite article. There is no definite article per se in the form of a word. The definite nature of the noun can only be abstracted from its context.

Çay pahalı  Tea is expensive
Çay soğuk  The tea is cold

Indefinite article. The word bir serves as the indefinite article, meaning a, an, as well as one

Plural suffices (17)

The suffixes -ler and -lar, being used in accordance with vowel harmony, indicate the plural noun. It is used before any other suffix (case suffix, possessive suffix, pronomial suffix). Classes of objects are denoted in the singular, whereas in English the plural is generally used: to sell newspapers, to eat chocolates, to write letters is gazeti satmak, çikolata yemek, and mektup yazmak, resp.

Compound or Qualifying Nouns (33)

In English compound or qualifying nouns are common, combining one noun with the other as in: handbag, snakeskin, theater ticket. Turks put compound nouns in a qualifying relationship, saying "hand its-bag," "snake its-skin," "theater its-ticket":

el  hand çanta  bag el çantası  handbag
yılan  snake deri  skin yılan derisi  snakeskin
tiyatro  theater bilet  ticket tiyatro bileti  theater ticket

Other examples are:

mart ayı the month of March   1453 yılı the year 1453
Kıbrıs adası the island of Cyprus   evet cevabı the answer yes
gitmek niyeti the intention of going   çilek dondurması strawberry ice cream
kahve fincanı coffee cup   Ankara kenti the city of Ankara
yatak odası bedroom

The plurals of these compounds insert the -ler/-lar suffix before the possessive: kahve fincanları, yatak odaları. Possessors not in the third person singular receive their normal endings: kahve fincanım my coffee cup, kahve fincanınız your coffee cup. It should be noted that kahve fincanı can mean his/her coffee cup or just coffee cup, but onun kahve fincanı can be used to represent the former where the context does not remove ambiguity.

When case suffixes are used, the pronomial n must be inserted between the -i or -si suffix: kahve fincanında in the coffee cup, yatak odalarına to the bedrooms

Some expressions originated from these compound constructions but are now treated as simple nouns:

ayakkabı "foot its-cover" footwear, shoes
binbaşı "thousand its-head" major
yüzbaşı "hundred its-head" captain
onbaşı "ten its-head" corporal
denizaltı "sea its-underneath" submarine

Note:

çocuğun ayakkabısı the child's shoes
binbaşılar majors
yüzbaşıya to the captain
denizaltıda in the submarine

Place names and nationality qualifiers (34)

A noun with a definite possessor itself also has a genitive relationship. Bu yılan derisi is this snakeskin, but bu yılanın derisi is the skin of this snake. Place names do not take this genitive ending however since ambiguity really does not arise when a suffix is added or not:

Türkiye Başbakanı the Prime Minister of Turkey
Adana Müzesi the Adana Museum

However, where another modifying word is inserted as part of the noun phrase, the possessive suffix is added to the place name:

Türkiye'nin yeni Başbakanı Turkey's new Prime Minister
Adana'nın güzel müzesi the beautiful museum of Adana

Adjectives involving describing nationality usually use the form which denotes the person of that nationality:

Ingiliz Englishman
Ingiliz tarihi English history
Türk Turk
Türk ordusu Turkish army

For American the two words are the noun and adjective Amerikalı and the noun form Amerikan, where the latter is used for things, and the former for people: bir Amerikalı an American, bir Amerikalı turist kadın an American woman tourist, Amerikan doları the American dollar.

Place pronouns (here, there, where) (52)

The suffix -re/-ra is adding to demonstrative pronouns and the pronoun ne (what?), followed by the appropriate case ending. Note the accent is on the first syllable.

burada in this place, here nerede in what place, where?
buraya to this place, hither nereye to what place, whither?
buradan from this place, hence nereden from what place, whence?
şurada, orada in that place, there
şuraya, oraya to that place, thither
şuradan, oradan from that place, thence

As few people speaking English use whence or thither these days, it is important nonetheless to keep careful attention of it in Turkish:

Where are you living? Nerede oturuyorsunuz?
Where are you going? Nereye gidiyorsunuz?
Where are you coming from? Nereden geliyorsunuz?
He is standing there Şurada duruyor
He is going there Şuraya gidiyor
He is leaving there Şuradan ayrılıyor

As such words are nouns in Turkish, they can take the usual noun suffix (e.g., -li)
Sen nerelisin? Where are you from? Ben Bursalıyım. Sen de oralı mısın? I'm from Bursa. Are you from there too?

They can also take the plural: buralarda in these parts, oralarda thereabouts

They can also take the genitive:

Oranın havası güzel değil The climate of that place is not good
Buraların yabancısıyım I am a stranger in these parts

As subjects of a sentence, even though no case ending is predicted, they take the third person singular:

Burası güzel This place is beautiful
Burası neresi? What place is this?
Polis komiseri, "Buranın halkı kuzu gibi" dedi The police superintendent said, "The people of this place are like lambs"
Evleri kentin neresinde? Their house is in what part of the city?
Kentin orası çok pahalı That part of the city is very expensive
Şimdi şurası aklıma geldi The following point about it has just occurred to me (That-place-of-it has now come to my mind)

Uses of the ablative case (55)

The ablative case denotes a point of origin or departure, and is used with verbs which indicate separation, avoidance, or withdrawal (from). Consider:

Bu yüzden for this reason
Ondan korkuyorum I am afraid of him
Bıktım bu yagmurlu havadan I'm fed up with this rainy weather
Toplantıya gitmedim, vaktim yoktu da ondan I didn't go to the meeting; I had no time, that's why (and from that)

It shows the material from which something is made:

Oğlan için kağittan bir şapka yaptım   I made [for] the boy a hat out of paper

It also translates of, as in: çocuklardan biri one of the children (from-the-children one-of-them); ondan haberim yok I have no news of him; Bundan eminim I am sure of this

The ablative also translates as through, by way of:

Buradan geçmediler They did not pass through here
Pencereden bakıyor She is looking through the window
Köprüye başka bir yoldan gittim I went to the bridge by another road
Elimden tuttu He took me by the hand (elimi tuttu means he took my hand)
Selim'i telefondan arıyorlar Selim is wanted on the phone (They are seeking Selim through the phone)

Some adverbs are also formed using the ablative suffix:

sonradan afterwards önceden beforehand
eskiden in the old days çoktan long since
sahiden really and truly doğrudan doğruya directly

Other idiomatic uses should be noted:

Bu öğrenci matematikten tam numara aldı This student got full marks for math
Kavunları kaçtan aldın? At what price did you buy the melons?
Onu hırsızlıktan yakaladılar They have arrested him for theft

Uses of the dative case (69)

The dative shows the recipient or a destination, the point to which action is directed. It is therefore used with verbs of motion and with adjective conveying ideas as proximity and conformity. It usually maybe translated to, but the idiom used may often demand a different preposition (indicated by the boldened English words). The last five examples are direct objects in English, but which nonetheless take a dative in Turkish.

Bu çiçekleri size aldım I bought these flowers for you
Resimlere bakıyor She is looking at the pictures
Hastalara bakıyor She is looking after the patients
Kalemi cebime koydum I put the pen into my pocket
Ete tuz koydum I put salt onto the meat
Şarabı buza koydu He put the wine on ice
Sandalyeye/Yerime oturdum I sat down on the chair/in my place

Uses of the locative case (97)

The obvious senses are at, in,or on, but are also used with words pointing to qualities:

On bir metre uzunluğunda bir ip a rope eleven meters long
o kılıkta bir adam a man of that aspect
bal renginde kumaş cloth the color of honey
güvercin yumurtası büyüklüğünde bir taş a stone of the size of a pigeon's egg
Kaç yaşındasınız? How old are you? (You are in the age of how many?)
Otuz yaşındayım I'm thirty (years old)

The genitive (136)

The third person personal suffix -(s)i plays a large part in the Turkish language. It may connect a word to a preceding word placed in the genitive: Bu kitabın bir nüshası   one copy of this book; or it may connect to a preceding word in the absolute: Sovyet hükümeti the Soviet government. It can also indicate a word understood from the context but not expressed: Müdür, odasındadır the director is in his room. Other examples:

Bu söz yerindedir This remark is appropriate (in its place)
Şu kumaş çok pahalı. Daha ucuzu yok mu? That material is too expensive. Isn't there any cheaper (the cheaper of it)?
İyisi, hep beraber kalkıp oraya gidelim The best thing (the good of it) to do is, let's all get up and go there together
Hâsılı, ne yaptımsa fayda etmedi In short (the outcome of it) whatever I did achieved nothing
Fenası şudur ki... The worst part about it (the bad of it) is this, that...
Doğrusu... (the true of it) To tell you the truth...
Daha doğrusu... To put it more precisely...
Daha acısı... (the more painful of it) Still more painful is the fact that...
Eskisi gibi (like the old of it) As of old
Şurası da var (this place of it also exists) There's this point about it too
Orası öyle (that place of it is thus) That's so
Inadına (for the obstinacy of it) Out of sheer cussedness
Türkçesi (the Turkish of it) To put it bluntly

In the spoken language, this suffix is also added to the -ki suffix for no apparent reason.

Compound nouns (148)

The following compound nouns have already been discussed: kızkardeş 'girl-sibling' sister; anayasa 'mother-law' constitution; başkent 'head-city' capital. In some compounds, the second noun retains a vestigial third person suffix which is used to make qualifying nouns which are separate words, unlike the compounds where the nouns are forced together: denizaltı 'sea underneath-thereof' submarine; hanımeli 'lady hand-thereof' honeysuckle

Cuma Friday and pazar Sunday are combined with erte morrow to make cumartesi Saturday and pazartesi Monday.

Some nouns are compounded adjectives and nouns:

karabaş 'black head' Anatolian sheepdog
karayel 'black wind' northwester
büyükbaba grandfather
büyükelçi 'great envoy' ambassador

Some noun compounds incorporate a verb:

imambayıldı 'the Imam fainted' a food dish
hünkârbeğendi 'the Sovereign approved' also a food dish
unutmabeni forget-me-not (a flower)

Special cases

so-and-so, such-and-such (117)
The words falan, filan, and falanca all mean so and so, such and such.

Ben falan oğlu falanım diye anlatmaya başladı He began to explain [saying], 'I am so and so, son of so and so'
Filan tarihte falancaya gittik On such-and-such a date, we went to so-and-so

The words falan and filan also mean and so on, and all that:

Para falan istemiyorum   I'm not asking for money or anything like that
Martta falan gelecekmiş   He's supposed to be coming in March or thereabouts
Toplantıda çocuk falan bulunması   At the meeting let there be no children and so on (i.e., babies or other potential sources of disturbance)

The phrase and all that is also constructed by adding a form of the word following the noun used, but with the first letter m: çocuk falan can become çocuk mocuk.

Burada kutu yok  There's no box here
Burada kutu mutu yok   There's no box or chest or case or carton or anything of the kind here
Arkadaş markadaş, herkes borcunu vermelidir   Friend, mate, chum; no matter who it is, everyone should pay his debt

Of course if the original word starts with the letter m, then falan or filan must be used:

Mektup falan geldi mi?  Have any letters or postcards or parcels or any other mail come?

Falanıncı means the so-manyeth:

Falanıncı evin falanıncı katında oturuyorum diye yazınız Write [saying], 'I am living on floor number so-and-so of house number so-and-so'

It can be noted that the word evleri can have four possible meanings. Evleri sattık we sold the houses is the direct plural object form. It can also be we sold his/her/its houses as the possessive third person suffix is attached to the indefinite plural. It can also be we sold their house, as it is the singular third person plural possessive suffix. In another meaning the plural or the third person plural possessive suffix is not repeated as such: evlerleri sattık but only evleri sattık, meaning we sold their houses. Hence a fourth possibility.

Evin can also mean your house or ... of the house (the house's ...), the last being part of a phrase in the genitive. Hence there is no distinction between nouns ending in a consonant: gözüne to your eye or to his/her/its eye; evinden from your house or from her house; kolunda on your arm or on his arm. When the noun ends in a vowel, however, this particular ambiguity is eliminated: annenden from your mother annesinden from her mother.

To resolve the ambiguities show above, the use of personal pronouns preceding the nouns is recommended:

onun evleri his houses
onların evleri their houses
onların evi their house
onun gözüne to his eye
senin gözüne to your eye

Uses (29)

For pronouns showing possession, one form is clear. But what about nouns which show a possessive relationship to one another. Consider:

the doctor's room doktorun odası
(a) doctor's room doktor odası
the door of the house evin kapısı
(a) house door ev kapısı
the girls' room kızların odası
the girls' rooms kızların odaları

The difference between the comparable phrases is the definite character of the first noun, marked by the definite article the. Possessors which are not definite remain in the absolute form. It should be noted that plurality in the object of possession is not necessarily expressed as kızların odaları meaning 'of-the-girls their-room,' since the idea of plurality is indicated in the first noun form. The -ları/leri suffix is used therefore to pluralize the second noun rather than indicating the third person plural possessive form.

It is also the case where genitive forms of personal pronouns in the 1st and 2nd persons are used: benim oda 'of-me the room' my room, senin ev your house, bizim köy our village. In writing and formal speech however, odam, evin, and köyümüz are used. Benim odam, senin evin, and bizim köyümüz are intended as emphasized the identities of the possessor.

Note the use of kendi own:

kendi odam my own room
kendi evin your own house
kendi köyümüz our own village

Interrogative pronouns (who? what? why? which?) (76)

Some forms of Ne? What?:

Niçin? (for ne için?) Why? Nereye? (to) where?
Nereden? (from) where? Nereli? belonging to where?
Neci? of what profession?

Compounds formed with ne include kadar amount, zaman or vakit time, gibi like, cins genus, türlü sort, biçim shape, asıl origin:

Ne kadar? How much?
Ne zaman/vakit? When? Kim? Who?
Ne gibi/cins/türlü/biçim? What sort of? Hangi? Which?
Nasıl? How? What kind of? Kaç (+ sing. noun) How many (+ plur. noun)

Kim as a (pro)noun can also take noun suffixes (plurals, case endings):

Kimi gördünüz? Whom did you see?
Kimleri gördünüz? What people did you see?
Arabayı kimden aldınız? From whom did you buy the car?
Parayı kime verdiniz? To whom did you give the money?
Bu gözlük kimin? Whose spectacles are these?
Kiminle birlikte geldi? With whom did she come?

Kim, hangi, and kaç can also take possessive suffixes:

Kiminiz? Who among you?
Hangimiz? Which of us?
Bu bavulların kaçı sizin? How many of these trunks are yours?
Ayın kaçında? On which day of the month?

Hangisi? instead of hangileri? is used for which of them? since the first form presupposes the plurality.

Ne takes the place of plural suffices but remains in absolute form when the object of the verb (unlike with kim): Kimi gördünüz? Whom did you see? Ne gördünüz? What did you see? Generally the definite object neyi is used under the following conditions:

  1. when a compound of ne follows in the sentence: Neyi ve nerede satın aldın? What did you buy, and where?
  2. when seeking a specific answer to a question: Ne okuyorsun? will bring the answer Bir kitap, but Neyi okuyorsun? should give more specific responses

Other items to note:

Nen var? (What-of-yours exists? What have you?) What's the matter with you?
Neme lazım? ('to-what-of-mine necessary?') or Bana ne? What's it got to do with me?
Neler gördük! What things we've seen!
Niye? (for neye) and Neden? Why?

Note that neden also refers to the noun cause, to be used in preference to its Arabic-derived Turkish synonym sebep.

Ne güzel? How beautiful?

The particle mi makes a question of the word that precedes it. It is usually not used in sentences (e.g., sentences with interrogative pronouns) which are questions by nature. Ne istiyorsunuz? What do you want? (Not Ne istiyor musunuz?) In rare cases, it occurs with a interrogative pronoun, such as in an indignant reply to the question of what do you want?: Ne mi? You ask 'what?'?

Reflexive pronouns (86)

Kendi is not only an adjective used to indicate own, but can take a possessive suffix to indicate myself, yourself, etc.

  1. the emphatic sense:
    Ben kendim böyle düşünüyorum I myself think so
    Bunu kim kırdı?-Siz kendiniz Who broke this?-You yourself
  2. the reflexive sense, where kendi is usually repeated:
    Kendi kendime Olmaz dedim I said to myself, 'It won't happen'
    Kendi kendinizi yaraladınız You hurt yourself

The third person has two forms: kendi and kendisi, with both taking a pronomial n before suffixes. Kendi is used correctly only in the reflexive sense: kendi kendini yaraladi he hurt himself while kendisi is used in the emphatic, although Turks use kendisi for both. The plural for kendi and kendisi is kendileri:

Kendi kendilerini yaraladılar They hurt themselves

Kendisi is also a more polite alternative for o, for he and she. Kendiliğinden 'from its selfness' means spontaneously, of his/her/its own ccord.

Participles and Verbal Nouns (Gerunds) (73)

Participles are essentially adjectives formed from verbs. English has a present and past participle, as in The ship is departing and The ship has departed, respectively. Unfortunately, English uses the -ing ending in verbal nouns (gerunds) as well as present participles: His brother is swimming (present participle) and His sport is swimming (gerund). In the first case, swimming is essentially an adjective substitutable with such other adjectives as fat, energetic, late. In the second case, swimming can substituted with sports such as tennis, music, carpentry, all nouns.

In Turkish, use of the infinitive form -mek/-mak can be used as one form of a verbal noun: düşmek kolay can be translated as to fall is easy and falling is easy.

Participles are also used in Turkish where a relative clause is typically used in English. For example, The man who is walking in the street in Turkish becomes sokakta yüruyen adam (in-street walking man)

Present participle (73)

To form the present participle, verb stems ending in a consonant add -en/-an, and for those ending in a vowel add -yen/-yan.

olmak to become, to be, to happen
olan becoming, being, happening; who or which becomes/is/happens
olmamak not to become, not to be, not to happen
olmayan (preferred to olmıyan) not becoming, being, or happening; who or which does not become/is not/does not happen
yürümek to walk
yürüyen walking; who or which walks
yürümemek not to walk
yürümeyen (not yürümüyen)  not walking; who or which does not walk

The present participle must sometimes be translated in a past sense as it represents action contemporaneous with the main verb:

Konuşan adam susacak The man who is speaking will fall silent
Konuşan adam sustu The man who was speaking (the speaking man) fell silent

As with adjectives in Turkish, present participles can be used to form nouns. Bunu bilen insanlar people who know this may be recast as Bunu bilinler those who know this.

Kahve isteyen var mı? Is there anyone who wants coffee?
çalışmayan öğrenciler students who do not work
çalışmayanlar those who do not work
meşgul olan görevli the official who is busy

The meaning becomes different if a comma (or pause when speaking) is inserted:

Meşgul olan, görevli The one who is busy is the official

Perfect participle (74)

The suffix -miş is added to the verb stem: görmüş having seen, who/which has seen; görmemiş not having seen, who/which has not seen.

The future perfect tense (I shall have gone) is the perfect participle given in context with the future of olmak: gitmiş olacağım having-gone I-shall-be

Future and Aorist participles (75)

The future participle is namely the future tense. Gelecek means about to come, although there are other meanings. Hence geleceksiniz means you are about to come.

The aorist sense reflects a feeling in general (see elsewhere). Gelir means coming, and gelmez means not coming, so gelirsiniz means you come and gelmezsiniz means you don't come.

The sense of to be and the participle never lose their identities. Gelmiyeceğim is I shall not come, but gelecek değilim means I'm not about to come or I don't intend to come. Similarly the negative has special meaning: bilmez değilim not-knowing I-am-not which means I am not unaware.

Uses of participles (75)

Subjects usually precede their predicates, but adjectives always precede the noun they qualify. For instance, Kız güzel The girl is pretty, but güzel kız the pretty girl. This is as true of participles as of adjectives:

bu saat çalar this clock strikes
bu çalar saat this striking clock
kebap döner the roast turns
döner kebap revolving roast
o sokak cıkmaz that street does not come out
o çıkmaz sokak
  or simply o çıkmaz 		that cul-de-sac
zaman gelecek the time will come
gelecek zaman future time

The constructions who has been, which has been, who will be, or which will be are formed using the perfect or future tense usually followed by olan (who is, which is):

konuşmuş olan adam the man who has spoken (the man who-is having-spoken)
konuşacak olan adam the man who is going to speak
Almanya'dan dönmüş olanlar those who have returned from Germany
bizi Türkiye'ye götürecek olan uçak the airplane which is going to take us to Turkey

Some related uses: gelecek hafta next week, geçen hafta last week, geçmiş zaman past time, geçmişte in the past, gelecekte in the future.

adı Zeki olan kardeşim my brother, whose name is Zeki
parası olmayan bir genç a young person who has no money
babası ölmüş olan bir çocuk a child whose father has died

This is best understood by taking the statement Kardeşimin adı Zeki'dir My brother's name is Zeki, and taking the -dir in adı Zeki'dir and replacing it with olan.

Verbal nouns and uses (82, 83)

In addition to the infinitive -mek/-mak, verbal nouns can be suffixed with three other endings: ‑me/-ma, -meklik/-maklık, and -(y)iş/üş/ış/uş . The latter may take both case and possessive suffixes whereas -mek/-mak never takes possessive or genitive suffixes; -mek/-mak can take locative (bilmekte, olmakta) or ablative (bilmekten, olmaktan) cases. The accusative and dative cases are obsolescent and have effectively been replaced by the cases used in -me/-ma. This evolution in the language started with front-vowel verbs (bilmeği becoming bilmeyi, bilmeğe becoming bilmeye) and is progressing to back-vowel verbs (olmağı becoming olmayı and olmağa becoming olmaya), there being no differences in any pronunciation.

As bilmem can be my knowing and the negative aorist I don't know, the ambiguity can be resolved by using bilmekliğim to refer to the former.

The accent on -me/-ma in the verbal noun is on the -me/-ma, in contrast with negatives used, for example in imperatives:

Gelme! don't come!
Bakma! don't look!
gelme the act of coming
bakma the act of looking

The following are more complex constructions:

gelmeme is both the nouns the act of not coming and to my coming
bakmama is both the act of not looking and to my looking
gelmememe is the noun to my not coming
bakamama is to my not looking

Again the distinction in the first two cases is accent on syllables: the first syllable is accented to signify the first meaning, the last syllable to signify the second meaning.

As for negative verbal nouns, endings include: -memek, -meme, and -meyiş. Two more based on the negative aorist are present: -mezlik and -memezlik. Abstract nouns are generally formed with these aorist nouns: anlaşmak to understand each other, anlaşmazlık misunderstanding; saldırmak to attack, saldırmazlık non-aggression.

In general, the infinitive verb form remains in the infinitive form when the object of istemek to want or bilmek to know:

Yüzmek istiyorum I want to swim
Yüzmek biliyorum I know [how] to swim

There is a growing tendency to use the object form of the verbal noun (-me/-ma) with istemek: Yüzmeyi istiyorum.

For other verbs, the appropriate case of -me/-ma should be used:

Yüzmeyi severim I like swimming
Büroya uğramayı unuttum I forgot to call at the office
Şarabı buza koymayı unutma Don't forget to put the wine on ice
Van gölüne gitmeyi düşünüyoruz We are thinking of going to Lake Van
Türkçe öğrenmeye çalışyorsun You are trying to learn Turkish

As for -mek için, the verbal noun dative also expresses purpose: Sizi görmeye geldik We came to see you. Ayakkabı almaya gidiyorum I am going to buy shoes.

The locative of -mek combined with the verb to be expresses is/was in the act of or a simple present or past:

Millî futbol takımımız Roma'da şimdi oynamaktadır  Our national football team is now playing in Rome
or: ...dün oynamaktaydı ...was playing yesterday

This use is largely literary but finding its way into general speech. Verbal nouns are frequently used in requests:

Beklemenizi rica ederim I should like you to wait
Eşim, annesine telefon etmemi istiyor My wife wants me to phone her mother
Orada durmamamızı söylediler They told us not to stand there

Verbal nouns are also used as:

  • qualifiers: okuma kitabı reading book, bekleme salonu waiting room
  • adjectives: yazma kitaplar manuscript books, dolma kalem fountain pen, doğma büyüme adalı an islander born and bred. (büyümek to grow up)
  • nouns: the results of action, that is. Dönme a convert is formed from dönmek to turn.

Verbal nouns ending in -iş refer to a way of doing: hence görüş is a way of seeing or outlook, yürüyüş is way of walking or gait. In other cases, this sense is not so obvious: alışveriş (taking-giving) commerce, shopping; gösteriş (way of showing) ostentation, and its adjectives gösterişli ostentatious and gösterişsiz unostentatious. From kullanmak to use comes kullanış use and kullanışlı useful.

Nouns formed with -memezlik referred to earlier have two senses, with one shown here (for other, see Notes on Certain Verbs under gelmek):

Sakın gelmemezlik etme, pişman olursun  Mind you don't fail to come or you'll be sorry

Personal participles (101-105)

Participles referred to above use a relative pronoun to introduce a relative clause describing a noun or subject: the man who visited us, the shop which stands on that corner, the horse that neighed. For other relative clause constructions, Turkish uses a relative clause of the second kind. Two participles, one denoting both past and present, the other denoting the future are used. The participle is constructed by adding to the verb stem the suffix ‑dik/‑dük/‑dık/‑duk, followed by a possessive suffix. Using oturmak:

oturduğum ev the house in which I live/lived
oturduğun ev the house in which you live/lived
oturduğu ev the house in which he/she lives/lived
oturduğumuz ev the house in which we live/lived
oturduğunuz ev the house in which you live/lived
oturdukları ev the house in which they live/lived

It is best not to think of these constructions as relative clauses but rather as literal translations of what they represent: think of shop of my buying the coat paltoyu aldığım dükkân rather than the shop where I bought the coat. It is better to express a mistake of my making yaptığım bir hata rather than a mistake which I make/made.

For the future tense, the future participle -ecek/-acak is used with the personal suffix and essentially refers to 'pertaining to future doing' or 'of future doing':

oturacağım ev the house in which I shall live
oturacağın ev the house in which you shall live
oturacağı ev the house in which he/she shall live
oturacağımız ev the house in which we shall live
oturacağınız ev the house in which you shall live
oturacakları ev the house in which they shall live

More examples of the personal participle:

Büroma gittiğim otobüs her zaman kalabalık   The bus on which I go to my office is always crowded
Dün gittiğimiz lokanta, Ulus meydanındaydı   The restaurant to which we went yesterday is in Ulus Square
Yarın Ankara'ya gideceğiniz uçak kalabalık olmıyacak  The aircraft on which you will be going to Ankara tomorrow will not be crowded
Herkes olduğu yerde kalsın  Let everyone remain where he is
Oturduğunuz apartman pahalı mı?  Isn't the apartment where you live expensive?
Sana verdiği kitabı okudun mu?  Did you read the book that I gave you?
İstediğin kadar al  Take as much as you want

How to express when clauses is shown here:

Londra'ya gittiğimiz zaman hava berbattı  When we went to London, the weather was terrible
Londra'ya gittiğimiz zaman hava berbat olacak  When we go to London, the weather will be terrible
Londra'ya gideceğimiz zaman hava hep berbattır  When we are about to go to London, the weather is always terrible

Note the different future sense in examples two and three.

As for any word with a possessive suffix, the personal participle may have the possessor expressed, in the genitive if definite, the absolute form if indefinite:

oynadıkları park  the park where they play
çocukların oynadıkları park  the park where the children play
çocuklar oynadıkları park  the park where the children play
Kedi bulunmadığı yerde fare başkaldırır  In the place where there is no cat, the mice get above themselves (When the cat's away, the mice will play)

In expressions of time however, the subject of the participle (the possessor) remains in the absolute form even when definite:

Çocuklar oynadıları zaman elbiselerini kirletirler  When children (the children) play, they get their clothes dirty

With use of the locative of hal, halde, the personal participle means either although or in a state of:

Bunu bildiğim halde bir şey söylemiyorum   Although I know this, I'm not saying anything
Elinde bir bıçak olduğu halde kapıdan fıraldı  He dashed out of the door with a knife in his hand

The use of üzere with personal participles is to mean as:

yukarda yazıldığı üzere  as it is written above
şimdi anlatılacağı üzere  as will now be explained

To indicate the meaning of instead of, the word yerde in place is used with the future personal participle:

Orada bekliyeceğimiz yerde tiyatroya doğru yürümeye karar verdik  Instead of waiting there, we decided to walk towards the theater

The Personal Participle as a Noun

The personal participles described above are essentially adjectives: yaptığım iş 'the job of my doing' the job which I do/did.

a. Personal participles become nouns meaning the fact of doing

Memnun kalacağınızda şüphem yok   I have no doubt that you will be pleased
İşimi yaptığım bilinir  It is [well] known that I do my job
İşimi yaptığımı herkes bilir   Everyone knows that I do my job

For expressing indirect speech:

İstanbul'dan yarın ayrılacağız We shall leave Istanbul tomorrow
Kendisine İstanbul'dan yarın ayrılacağımızı daha söylemedim I have not yet told her that we will leave Istanbul tomorrow
Üç gün kalmak istediklerini söylediler They said they wanted to stay three days

The verbal noun use is very much different in meaning than the personal participle:

Beklememizi söyledi He told us to wait
Beklediğimizi söyledi He said we were waiting

The personal participle is often used with için to mean for, because of. The ablative case of the participle also provides similar meaning, because of the fact of:

Cevabı bilmediğim için sustum
Cevabı bilmediğimden sustum   I was silent because I didn't know the answer

In the locative, the participle means when/on doing:

Haberi aldığımda çok kızdım I became very angry when I received the news

In the dative, it means instead of, just as with yerde:

Okula gideceğine futbol maçina gitti Instead of going to school, he went to the football match (think of it as: '[in exchange] for his future going')

In the dative used with göre:

Bu sorunun cevabını bildiğinize gore, nicin soruyorsunuz? In view of the fact that you know the answer to this question, why do you ask?

b. Personal participles become nouns meaning 'that of my doing' or that which I do/did

Gazetede okuduğum çok ilginç What I read in the paper is very interesting
Bize Rusya gezintisinde gördüklerini anlattı She told us about the things she had seen on the trip to Russia
Yaramaz çocuk kacti, adamin kendisini yakalayamayacağina inandigi bir uzaklıkta durdu The naughty child ran off and stopped far enough away which the child believed was beyond the man's ability to catch him 'The naughty child escaped [and] stopped at a distance pertaining to his believing in the man's future inability to catch him'

Verbs

The infinitive form of the verb is recognized by its -mek/-mak ending, the form entered in dictionaries. The suffix ending is in accordance with vowel harmony, From this form, it is relatively easy to construct the proper declension by following the rules regarding tenses and being careful to preserve the root/stem of the verb, i.e., those letters preceding -mek/-mak.

The verb to be

As in English, Turkish uses it extensively. The infinitive olmak is used as an infinitive of to be, but a more accurate translation is to become or to happen (which reflect the essence of existence intrinsic in the meaning of to be).

• Present tense (24)

All persons of the present test form of the verb to be are represented as unaccented suffices. In the table below are the suffices which are affixed according to vowel harmony. The use of d or t depends on a preceding voiced or voiceless consonant, respectively.

After e or i After ö or ü After a or ı After o or u
I am -im -üm -ım -um
you are -sin -sün -sın -sun
he/she/it -dir/-tir -dür/-tür -dır/-tır -dur/-tur
we are -iz -üz -ız -uz
you are -siniz -sünüz -sınız -sunuz
they are -dirler/-tirler -dürler/-türler -dırlar/-tırlar -durlar/-turlar

The second person singular form is only used to address children or intimates, where the second person plural form is used to address persons in a formal manner, such as persons on the street or people of only passing acquaintance. The plurality of the second person comes into play in all cases where addressing is done to more than one person or a group. Examples of use of the verb to be:

Türküm I am Turkish
Türktür She is Turkish
Fransızım I am French
Fransızdır He is French
Amerikalıyım I am American
Amerikalıdır She is American

Where the to be suffix is added to words ending in vowels (Amerikalı), a 'y' is added to observe the natural continuation between two vowels which are the suffixes of the first person I and we, and is sounded.

The ending of the third person (-dir/-tir) is used rarely in speech or conversation: Ahmet Ankara'da. However, it should always be used in written communications or a formal letter: Ahmet Ankara'dadır.

Plural subjects which are inanimate generally take a singular verb, meaning people are and things is. Animate plural subjects can also take a singular verb if there are a number of people represented as acting in unison. And nonhuman living subjects can take a plural verb if they are considered to act as individuals.

• Negative değil (25)

The negative of to be is expressed by a form of değil.

değilim I am not değiliz we are not
değilsin you are not değilsiniz you are not
değil(dir) he/she/it is not değil(dirler) they are not

It can make negative a part of the sentence where the verb is understood in context: Ahmet değil, Orhan gidiyor It is not Ahmet, it is Orhan who is going. The indefinite article bir is not used in negative statements, the reason being that, if one is not a member of a class, it is unnecessary to state that one is a single member of the class: çocuk değilim I am not a child. Note in general that bir is not used in sentences expressing nationality or occupation: Türküm I am a Turk; öğrencisiniz you are a student.

• Past tense, its negative, and negative-present-conditional (36, 37)

The past tense of the to be form is expressed as a suffix added to the stem -i.

idim I was idik we were
idin you were idiniz you were
idi he was idiler they were

İzmir'de idik/İzmir'deydik we were in Izmir
hazır idin/hazırdın you were ready
sorumlu idim/sorumluydum I was responsible
müdür idi/müdürdü he was the director

The particle mi combines with the past tense suffix in interrogatives:
İzmir'de miydik? Were we in Izmir?
Hazır mıydın? Were you ready?
Sorumlu muydum? Was I responsible?
Müdür müydü? Was he the director?

The past tense of the verb for to be, to become, to happen, olmak, is regular: oldum, oldun, oldu, olduk, oldunuz, oldular.

• Conditional (36)
The conditional is also based on the stem -i with the suffix -se added.

İzmir'de isek/İzmir'deysek if we are in Izmir
hazır isen/hazırısan if you are ready
sorumlu isem/sorumluysam if I am responsible
müdür ise/müdürse if he is the director

Formation of the negative of the past tense and present-conditional essentially attaches suffixes (according to vowel harmony) to değil:

değildim I was not değilsem if I am not
değildin you were not değilsen if you are not
değildi he was not değilse if he is not
değildik we were not değilsek if we are not
değildiniz you were not değilseniz if you are not
değildiler they were not değilseler if they are not

• The Perfect it-is-said tense (imiş) (87)

The ending -miş when suffixed to any verb stem other than to be forms the perfect participle. Attached to the stem -i however gives the sentence another meaning, namely reportedly is/was:

Babası fakir bir köylü imiş her father is/was said to be a poor peasant

Alternative translations: It seems/seemed he is/was; I gather that he is/was. If someone says Enayisin (You are an idiot), the proper response is Niçin enayi imişim? (Why am I allegedly/supposedly an idiot?), not Niçin enayiyim?, the latter more likely getting the answer Because you were born an idiot.

Verbs in the present tense (42)

The present tense of verbs is expressed by adding the suffix -yor. Verb stems ending in a consonant before adding -yor will add a high (front) vowel (i.e., i, ü, ı, or u) which depends on the vowel of the last syllable in the stem:

geliyor he is coming görüyor he is seeing
alıyor he is taking buluyor he is finding

The -yor is added directly to verbs already ending in a high vowel:

taşımak to carry taşıyor he is carrying
korumak to protect koruyor he is protecting

Verb stems ending in a low vowel is such that the -y in the suffix forces the last vowel of the stem to become high:

demek to say diyor he is saying
söylemek to tell söylüyor he is telling
anlamak to understand anlıyor he is understanding
kollamak to observe kolluyor he is observing

gördün you saw gördünüz you saw
gördü he/she/it saw gördüler they saw

baktın you looked baktınız you looked

buldun you found buldunuz you found

Although the simple past is shown, this form also implies the form has given or have given, as Turkish does not use progressive forms.

Future tense (57)

Added to the verb stem is -ecek/-acak according to vowel harmony. Personal suffixes are then added to the suffix. The k is converted to ğ between vowels.

gideceğim I shall go, I will go
gideceksin you shall go, you will go
gidecek he shall go, he will go
gideceğiz we shall go, we will go
gideceksiniz you shall go, you will go
gidecekeler they shall go, they will go

Vowel changes occur in voice and in writing for words with a stem vowel at the end: beklemek and anlamak are more often bekliyecek and anlıyacak more than they become beklecek he will wait and anlayacak he will understand.

In forming the negative of the future tense, such spelling changes occur in the negative syllable and in the interrogative form: gitmiyecek instead of gitmeyecek he will not go.

gidecek miyim? am I going to go? gitmiyecek miydim? was I going to go?
gitmiyeceğim? I will not go? gitmiyecek miyim? am I not going to go?
gidecek miydik? were we going to go? gitmiyecek miydik? were we not going to go?

In other constructions, Turks use the phrase gelecek, göreceksiniz, which means you will come and see. Turks do not generally use geleceksiniz, göreceksiniz any more than an English speaker would say you will come and you will see. This is true for any verb tense but the past containing -di, since that suffix is an integral part of the verb. In a special case, gidecekseniz if you are going to go is also expressed as gidecek olursanız ('if-you-are about-to-go').

Aorist tense (64)

Aorist tenses expresses unbounded actions, habitual doing, or a readiness and willingness to do. The suffix r is added directly to stems ending in a vowel:

anlamak to understand anlar he understands
taşımak to carry taşır he carries
demek to say der he says

When stems end in a consonant, the insertion of r is subject to the following rules.

  1. When the consonant-ending stems are one syllable, -er/-ar is added:
    etmek to do eder he does
    gitmek to go gider he goes
    çıkmak to go out/up çıkar the goes out/up
    sormak to ask sorar he asks
  2. Some common verbs of the language show fourfold harmony
    -ir: bilmek to know bilir she knows
    gelmek to come geliriz we come
    vermek to give veririm I give
     
    -ür: görmek to see görürsünüz you see
    ölmek to die ölür he dies
     
    -ır: almak to take/buy alır mısın? are you taking/buying?
    kalmak to remain kalırlar they remain/stay
    sanmak to suppose/think sanır she suppose/thinks
     
    -ur: bulmak to find bulur he finds
    durmak to stand dururuz we stand
    olmak to be, become olur it happens, it becomes
    vurmak to strike, hit vurur it hits
  3. Some other multi-syllable verbs also show fourfold harmony:
    getirmek to bring getirir she brings
    kullanmak to use kullanırız we use
    öksürmek to cough öksürür he coughs
    dokunmak to touch dokunurum I touch

görürsün you see görürdün you used to see

görürdüler 		they used to see
görürsem if I see, etc.

• Uses of aorist (66)
The basic sense is in principle I do or in general I do. Consider okumak to read. To say okurum implies that I am capable of reading, that I am prepared to read. It does not say that I ever do read and not that I am reading now: okuyorum is used for that sense.

The aorist is most often used in making polite requests:

Bana bir kibrit verir misiniz?  Will you give me a match?
Evet, veririm  Yes, I will (I give)

It would also be used in making promises:

Gelirim dedim mi, gelirim ben  I'll come; did I say? I'll come  If I say I'll come, then I'll come

It is found particularly used in proverbs:

İt ürür kervan yürür  The dog howls, the caravan goes on

There is an interchangeable use of the present and aorist tenses however, such as biliyorum and bilirim. Many useful expressions are aorist: teşekkür ederim thank you; affedersiniz 'you are ready to forgive' I beg your pardon; olur 'it happens' it is possible, all right, okay; olmaz 'it does not happen' it won't do, no way!

Interrogative form (particle mi) (29)

Words are turned into questions by putting mi after them. The particle follows vowel harmony in accordance with the vowel of the last syllable of the preceding word: Kız mı? The girl or Is it a girl? Türk mü? Turkish? There are some exceptions however: Gol mü? A goal? Saat mi? Is it a clock? The accent is always on the preceding word or syllable, never on the particle.

Again the particle emphasizes the word preceding, putting it into question:

Başbakan Çin'e gidiyor The prime minister is going to China
Başbakan Çin'e gidiyor mu? Is the prime minister is going to China?
Başbakan Çin'e mi gidiyor? Is it to China that the prime minister is going?
Çin'e başbakan mı gidiyor? Is it the prime minister who is going to China?

The negative of to be değil is combined with the particle mi to mean not true? or isn't it (so)? in the same sense as the French n'est ce pas?:

Hava güzel, değil mi?  The weather is beautiful, isn't it?

• Present and Present-past tense (43)
The particle mi is placed after the -yor except in the case of the third person plural, where it follows the -lar:

geliyor muyuz? are we coming? gelmiyor muyuz? are we not coming?
görüyorlar mı? are they seeing? görmüyorlar mıydı? were they not seeing?
görmüyordular mı? alıyor mu? is she taking? almıyor muydu? was she not taking?

Transformed (compound) verbs (58)

A number of verbs have been formed merely by compounding to produce verbal nouns. A noun form is combined typically with the verb etmek to do, a pattern exemplified by yardım etmek 'to do help' to help. It is often the case these words are of foreign origin, although Turkish equivalents exist. They are still very much used: teşekkür etmek to thank, kabul etmek to accept, telefon etmek to telephone.

In speech, the accent is on the last syllable of the noun except when the verb is interrogative or negative or both, in which case the rules for accenting have been noted:

Telefon ediyorsunuz You are telephoning
Telefon etmiyorsunuz You are not telephoning
Telefon etmiyor musunuz? You are telephoning?

With nouns of Arabic origin and monosyllablic, the word is combined with etmek:

af(ffı) pardon affetmek to pardon
sabır(brı) patience sabretmek to show patience

Some older words, eylemek and kılmak, also mean the same as etmek, and these words survive in some phrases. Eylemek is hardly seen except in Allah rahmet eylesin May God have mercy on him/her (when speaking of the dead). Kılmak is also used in a limited manner: namaz kılmak is to perform the rites of Muslim prayer. Kanunları etkili kılacağız is we shall make the laws effective.

The Perfect-Past (-miş) (89)

Formed by adding the form of to be to the perfect participle -miş. Note that this differs from imiş since (a) it is used exclusively to express the past tense and (b) it does not invariably imply a lack of first-hand knowledge. It should be kept in mind that the perfect tense describes the present state arising out of past action. Orhan has gone can be translated as Orhan gitti or Orhan gitmiş. The first tells us what Orhan did, the second the way he is consequently now.

If you have been standing over the pot while it was on the stove, you can say Yemek iyi pişti The food cooked well. However if you notice it only when it reaches the table, you would say Yemek iyi pişmiş the food is well-cooked. If snow has fallen and you are reporting the occurrence, whether or not you saw it fall, you would say Kar yağmış. But because you have not said Kar yağdı, room for doubt remains whether you have first-hand knowledge, and so the tense has acquired the inferential sense of imiş from to be.

By saying Orhan gitmiş, you come to that statement because you believe he has gone, either because it is hearsay or you see his hanger missing. To make it clear his being gone and your having seen him go, say either Orhan gitti or Orhan gitmiştir. For the 3rd person plural, gitmişlerdir asserts the most positive, whereas gitmişler expresses the ambiguity. With other forms (gitmişim, gitmişiz, gitmişsin, etc.) the sense is as with gitmiş. If the endings past or conditional are added however, there is no inferential sense (see ahead).

Note that -dir can be added to first and second person in the perfect to asset as fact something not positively known: Konuk yıldızmızın otobiyografisini hepiniz okumuşsunuzdur Of course you have all read our guest star's autobiography.

The Perfect

• Pluperfect (Past-Perfect) (92)
This tense is formed by adding a simple past form of to be (I was) to the perfect participle. Hence olmuştum (olmuş idim 'I was having-become') I had become, almıştın you had bought, bilmiştik we had known. Görmek is declined as follows:

görmüştüm I had seen görmüştük we had seen
görmüştün you had seen görmüştünüz you had seen
görmüştü he/she/it had seen görmüştüler,
görmüşlerdi 		they had seen

This tense has no relationship to the inferential quality of the perfect expressed in -miş. The pluperfect is used more frequently in Turkish than in English, showing that one past action preceded another. We would say: They bought the house twenty years ago and sold it yesterday. But Turks use the form They had bought the house... Evi yirmi yil önce almişlardı, dün sattılar. The pluperfect also appears in colloquial (not literary) forms, one made by suffixing idim to the past base, the other by suffixing idi to the past tense.

gördüydüm gördümdü I had seen
gördüydün gördündü you had seen
gördüydü he had seen
gördüydük gördüktü we had seen
gördüydünüz gördünüzdü you had seen
gördüydüler gördülerdi you had seen

• Perfect conditional (93)
The suffixed forms of isem are added to the perfect base.

Van gölünü görmüşseniz bahtiyarsınız If you have seen Lake Van, then you are lucky ('If-you-are having-seen Van its-lake...')

There is nothing inferential about this construction. To make it inferential, imiş must be used:

Van gölünü görmüş imişseniz bahtiyar imişsiniz They say that if you have seen Lake Van, then you are lucky

Passive voice (93)

Passive voice essentially states that the subject undergoes the action rather than is the agent of the action. Passive verbs are formed by adding to the stem of the active voice verb form, subject to the following rules:

  1. to stems ending in any consonant except l, the syllable -il/ül/ıl/ul is added:
    sezmek to perceive sezilmek to be perceived
    görmek to see görülmek to be seen
    yazmak to write yazılmak to be written
    tutmak to hold tutulmak to be held
  2. to stems ending in l, the syllable -in, etc., is added:
    bilmek to know bilinmek to be known
    bulmak to find bulunmak to be found
  3. to stems ending in a vowel, -n is added:
    yıkamak to wash yıkanmak to be washed
    söylemek to tell söylenmek to be spoken
    okumak to read okunmak to be read

Three irregularities are found: anlaşılmak to be understood (from anlamak), ayrılmak to be separated (from ayırmak), çevrilmek to be turned (from çevirmek). Note that the reflexive verbs also have the same forms as the passive; see the section on reflexive verbs to see how to distinguish meaning with less ambiguity.

• Uses
Turkish allows intransitive verbs to be expressed passively. Since there is no object of the active verb, there is no subject in the passive. Rather the passives are said to be used impersonally.

İstasyona bu yoldan giderler They go by this road to the station
İstasyona bu yoldan gidilir This is the road by which one goes to the station
 
Türk genci kaç yaşında asker olur? At what age does the Turkish youth become a soldier?
Türkiye'de kaç yaşinda asker olunur? In Turkey, at what age does one become a soldier?

The Turkish for elective offices is seçimle gelinen yerler ('election-by-coming-being-done places'). Compound verbs (verbs formed by etmek) are made passive with edilmek or with olunmak: tatbik etmek to apply is either tatbik ediliyor or tatbik olunuyor it is being applied.

Irregularities include kaybetmek to lose and tiraş etmek to shave (someone); the passives are kaybolmak to be lost and tiraş olmak to be shaved or to have a shave.

Reflexive verbs (93)

A reflexive verb is one whose action affects no external object but reflects back to the subject. I washed myself. He bestirred himself. The reflexive stem is formed by adding -n to stems ending in a vowel, and -in/ün/ın/un to stems ending in a consonant:

yıkanmak to wash oneself
söylenmek to talk to oneself, mumble
yüklenmek to take up, to load upon oneself
 
çekinmek to withdraw oneself, abstain
bulunmak to find oneself, to be

Since passive and reflexive verbs share common forms, e.g., çocuk yıkandı the child washed himself or the child has been washed, it is possible to make this the reflexive form and require that -il be added to make it passive: çocuk yıkanıldı. In addition, reflexiveness can be conveyed by using the active form of the verb in combination with the reflexive pronoun: çocuk kendi kendini yıkadı.

For some reflexive constructions, one does not do an action to oneself, but for oneself:

etmek to do edinmek to acquire
geçmek to pass geçinmek to get along, to make a living

Görünmek is the reflexive (not passive!) of görmek, and means to seem, to appear.

Reflexives may be used impersonally (as for passives):

Burada yıkanılmaz One may not wash oneself here 'here washing-oneself-is-not-done'

Verbs meaning to begin (başlamak) are made passive when they govern a passive verb:

Raporu basmaya başladılar They have begun to print the report
Rapor basılmaya başlandı The report has begun to be printed

Agents of passive verbs are denoted by use of the postposition tarafından:

Pencere, çocuk tarafından kırıldı The window was broken by the child

This use of the passive is not common however, as Turks prefer the more direct: Pencereyi çocuk kırdı It was the child who broke the window (note subject not placed at the beginning of the sentence but next to the verb indicates emphasis).

The adverbial suffix -ce is also used regularly in the passive voice:

Bu işe hükümetçe karar verildi This work has been decided on by the government 'To this work a decision has been governmentally given'

Transitive and intransitive verb use (70)

It is generally the case English uses a verb which can be transitive (an object is supplied) or intransitive (no object). Turkish seems to have an aversion to intransitive forms, supplying an object typically formed from the root of the verb where no noun is obvious:

Babam mektuplarını yazıyor My father is writing his letters
Babam yazı yazıyor My father is writing
Bugün et yemedim I have not eaten meat today
Bugün yemek yemedim I have not eaten today
Eşim robunu dikiyor My wife is sewing her dress
Eşim dikiş dikiyor My wife is sewing
Annem bana bir kazak örüyor My mother is knitting a pullover for me
Annem örgü örüyor My mother is knitting
Bira içer He drink beer
İçki içer He drinks

Imperatives (79)

The imperative in Turkish for the 2nd person singular is the verb stem itself: Koş! Run! The negative is the addition of the appropriate -me/ma: Koşma! Don't run! To the verb stem may be added -in or -iniz, with y being added for verb stems ending in vowels:

De/deyin/deyiniz! Say (it)!    Deme/demeyin/demeyiniz! Don't say!

The longer the suffix, the more polite. Note that there is no change from low to high vowels in the imperative when a vowel is at the end of the stem: başla, başlayın, başlayınız. The second a never becomes ı.

Another imperative form is a third-person usage. The suffix -sin/sün/sın/sun is added for the singular, and -ler/lar is added to make it apply to the plural:

gitsin let him/her/it go gitsinler let them go
görsün let him/her/it see alsınlar let them take

• Buyurun! (90)
Buyurmak to command is defined in the dictionary also as 'speak, come, go, pass, enter, take, ...' This derives historically from a time when there were people who never did anything for themselves, but ordered that it be done. If someone makes a telephone call today, one says telefon etti 'he has done telephone.' If the Sultan had done such, the activity was reported as telefon buyurdular they have commanded telephone. The usage Buyurun! 'Command!' is very common in courteous speech to ask someone how they might be helped.

Bize buyurmaz mısınız? Won't you deign to call on us?
Ne buyurdunuz? What did you say?
Buyurun çayınızı Please drink your tea
Buyurun asansöre Be so good as to enter the elevator

Auxiliary verbs

• can (106)
-ebil/abil is added to consonant stems, and -yebil/yabil to vowel stems to convey the sense of can or to be able. Vowel harmony only affects choice of e or a, but the bil remains fixed, derived from bilmek.

gelmek to come gelebilmek to be able to come
geliyoruz we are coming gelebiliyoruz we can come
geldiniz you came gelebildiniz you were able to come
söylenir it is said söylenebilir it can be said
okuyacak mısın? will you read? okuyabilecek misin? will you be able to read?

okuyanlar those who read, okuyabilenler those who can read; yaptığım iş the job I do/did, yapabildiğim iş the job I can/could do.

Negatives may be attached to the verb stem before the expression of ability. This is not exactly the same as 'can not' (see below). gelmeyebilirim 'I am able not to come' I may not come (e.g., if the weather is bad) or I don't have to come if I don't want to. The alternative spelling is gelmiyebilirim.

• cannot (107)
The expression of not to be able to or can not is by insertion of an unaccented -e/a before a negative -me/ma, all suffixed to the stem. There is no -ebil/abil suffix on the stem.

gelmemek not to come gelememek to be unable to come
gelmiyoruz we are not coming gelemiyoruz we can not come
gelmediniz you did not come gelemediniz you could not come
söylenmez it is not said söylenemez it can not be said
okumayacak mısın? are you not going to read? okuyamayacak mısın? will you not be able to read?
gelemeyebilirim I may not be able to come
okumayanlar those who do not read okuyamayanlar those who can not read
yapmadığım iş the job I don't/didn't do yapamadığım iş the job that I can not/could not do

yakalayacağı the fact that he will catch yakalamayacağı the fact that he will not catch; yakalayamayacağı the fact that he will not be able to catch (note the following alternative spellings: yakalıyacağı, yakalamıyacağı, yakalayamıyacağı)

• must, ought, should (116)
Turks say your waiting here is necessary rather than you must wait here. The word for necessary is expressed by the old Arabic term lazım and by the more Turkish gerek. Both mean the adjective necessary and the noun necessity. There is also an adjective gerekli. The verb gerekmek means to be necessary.

Burada beklemeniz lazım/gerek/gerekli/gerekiyor. Otobüs durağından beş kilometre yürümeniz gerekecek. We shall have to walk five kilometers from the bus stop.

The expression of should or ought, being less pressing than necessity, is done by adding -meli to the verb stem.

Fakirlere yardım etmeli One should help the poor
Soğuk su ile tıraş olmamalı One ought not to shave with cold water
Bir şeyi iyice düsünmeli, ondan sonra söylemeli One should think well about something, and then speak

This form of expression is combined with the present or past using any person:

İstasyona erken varmaya çalışmalıyız We ought to try to get to the station early
Böyle davranmamalıydınız You should not have behaved in this way

Both the personal and impersonal sense of the third person are seen in these constructions:

Günde bir buçuk litre su içmeli One should drink 1.5 liters of water a day
Herkes günde bir buçuk litre su içmeli Everyone should drink...

The word zor (force, obligation) is also used to express necessity.

Mahkeme, kanunları uygulamak zorundadır The court is obliged ('in the obligation of') to apply the laws

This use of the word zor has replaced the old Ottoman usage mecubiyet. The adjective mecburi required is of course still used frequently.

Adverbial verbs

To verb stems may be added several types of suffixes to indicate adverbial states:
• -erek/-arak (107)
This is supposed to show action accompanying or slightly preceding that of the main verb. It should be thought of as doing or by doing:

Açlık grevi yaparak öldü 'by-doing hunger its-strike he died' He died by going on a hunger strike

Olarak is formed from olmak, and it means as, as being, for or by way of:

Türkiye'ye ilk defa olarak geçen yaz geldik We came last summer to Turkey for ('it being') the first time
Bunu sana dost olarak söylüyorum I am telling you this as a friend
Bana yol parası olarak beş bin lira verdiler They gave me five thousand lira for fare

Verb stems ending in vowels have y to buffer the verb stem with the suffix:

Her ikimizce de bilinen bazı noktaları tekrarlayarak söze başlıyacağım I will begin what I have to say by repeating some points which are known to the both of us
Gülümseyerek cevap verdi He replied with a smile

• -ince/-ünce/-ınca/unca (108)
This sense expresses action just preceding that of the main verb: on doing, when doing.

Pencereden bakınca karşımda Adaları gördüm On looking out of the window I saw the islands facing towards me
Sizi görünce tanıdım I recognized you on sight (the moment I saw you)
Bahar gelince çiçekler açar Flowers bloom when spring comes
Yüzbaşı nöbetçi nerede? diye sorunca şaşırdım, dısarda olduğunu söyledim I was startled when the captain asked where's the sentry? and I said he was outside.

Note that all of these forms in which -ince is given can be replaced by the locative of the personal participle: baktığımda, gördüğümde, geldiğinde, sorduğunda.

• Other adverbial forms (129)
Verb stems also have many other suffixes, some which are similar to the -erek or -ince forms. Note for stems ending in a vowel, a y is inserted.

  1. -e -e (or -a -a) Verb stems with these endings occurring in pairs denote a repeated action which modifies the main verb. The accent is on the first -e of the pair:
    Bile bile yalan söyler He tells lies deliberately (knowingly)
    Koşa koşa geldi He came running
    Sora sora aradığı yeri buldu With constant asking he found the place he sought

    The term for farewell, Güle güle, essentially means [Go] smilingly. The -e -e construction is usually interchangeable with -erek. İstemiyerek gitti is replaced by İstemiye istemiye gitti he left unwillingly. It should be noted that -erek endings do not indicate repetitious action. Sometimes different but related stems are used in expressions: Öksüre aksıra odama girdi Coughing and sneezing he entered my room.

  2. -inceye kadar, -inceye dek, -ene kadar, -ene dek until
    Postacı gelinceye kadar bekledik We waited until the postman came
    Hava açılıncaya dek yola çıkmıyalım Let's not go out on the road until the weather clears up
    Çocuklar okuldan eve gelene kadar hep pencereden bakar She keeps looking out the window until the children come home from school
  3. -meden without, before
    The accent is on the syllable before the suffix.
    Bana sormadan tornavidamı almış He has taken my screwdriver without asking me
    Siz olmadan yapamayız We cannot do without you ('you not-being')
    Görmeden inanılmıyacak It has to be seen to be believed ('without seeing, it will not be believed')

    The sense of before can be made unambiguous by adding önce or evvel: Ben gelmeden üç gün önce Konya'ya gitmişti He had gone to Konya three days before I came.

  4. -dikten sonra after

    Mektubu okuduktan sonra geri verdim After reading the letter I gave [it] back

  5. -eli, -eli beri, -eliden beri since

    Buraya geleli hiç mektup yazmadım Since coming here I have written no letters at all

    The subject of the since clause may be shown by inserting a noun or pronoun: Biz/Oğlum buraya geleliden beri ben hiç mektup yazmadım Since we/my son came here I have written no letters at all. It can also be clear from the context: Doğu uygarlığından umudumuzu keseli çok oldu It has been a long time since we gave up hope ('since cutting our hope') of Eastern civilization.

    The subject is also revealed by using the past tense form preceding the -eli form of the verb:

    Buraya geldim geleli Since I came here
    Buraya geldiniz geleli Since you came here
    Şu kapıdan çıktı çıkalı Since he went out of that door

    The negative of -eli is best translated in the positive:

    Oğlunuz maşallah biz görmiyeli çok büyümüş Your son has grown very much since we last saw him ('since we have not seen')

  6. -dikçe the more...
    İnsan yaşadıkça bilgisi artar The more one lives, the more one's knowledge increases
    Kız güldükçe yüzünde güller açılır, ağladıkça gözlerinden inciler saçılırmış It is said that every time the girl laughs, roses bloom on her face; every time she weeps, pearls are scattered from her eyes
    Fil gibi, yedikçe yer Like the elephant, the more he eats the more he eats

    Her every is sometimes found in these constructions. Bursa'yı her gördükçe daha çok seviyorum The more I see Bursa, the more I love it. Gittikçe 'the more it goes' means gradually, and oldukça 'the more it happens' means rather, quite, quite a lot.

  7. -meksizin without

    Bir dakika bile kaybetmeksizin geldiler They came without even losing a minute

  8. -mektense rather than

    Televizyon seyretmektense, bahçede oturup bira içelim Rather than watch TV, let's sit in the garden and drink beer

Reciprocal and cooperative verbs (109)

For verbs ending in a vowel, the addition of -ş, or for verbs ending in a consonant, the addition of -iş/üş/ış/uş generally alters the verb to indicate action performed by more than one agent, either in cooperation or in opposition.

oynamak to play oynaşmak to play together
-e çarpmak to hit çarpışmak to collide
dövmek to beat dövüşmek to fight one another
görmek to see görüşmek to see one another, converse

It is not always the case that verbs with the -ş suffix indicate this property. Gelişmek does not mean to come together but to develop; yetişmek is not 'to suffice together' but to grow up.

Causative form of verb and its uses (111)

Causative verbs are verbs such as we laid him to the bed (cf. he lay on the bed) or you felled the tree (cf. the tree fell). Verbs of the causative nature are formed by adding -dir to the stem.

yemek to eat yedirmek to feed
ölmek to die öldürmek to kill
kanmak to be satisfied kandırmak to convince
unutmak to forget unutturmak to cause to forget

There are some exceptions depending on whether the verb ends in a vowel or has more than one syllable. For verbs ending in a vowel and having more than one syllable:

okumak to read okutmak to educate
anlamak to understand anlatmak to explain, narrate, tell
beklemek to wait bekletmek to keep waiting

Stems of more than one syllable ending in r or l often add -t:

oturmak to sit oturtmak to seat
darılmak to be cross darıltmak to irritate

Some monosyllabic verbs, usually those ending with a k, add -it/üt/ıt/ut:

korkmak to fear korkutmak to frighten
akmak to flow akıtmak to let flow

Another some twenty monosyllabic verbs add -ir/ür/ır/ur:

bitmek to end bitirmek to bring to an end
geçmek to pass geçirmek to spend (time)

Other common verbs in this class are batmak to sink, doğmak to be born, doymak to be satiated, duymak to feel or hear, düşmek to fall, içmek to drink, kaçmak to escape, pişmek to cook, uçmak to fly, yatmak to lie down or go to bed.

Three verbs add -er/ar:

çıkmak to go up, out çıkarmak to extract, expel
gitmek to go gidermek to remove
kopmak to break off, out koparmak to break off, out
(intrans.) (trans.)

The following forms are irregular:

kalkmak to rise, depart kaldırmak to raise, remove
görmek to see göstermek to show

The verb görmek also has a secondary sense which is causative and its form is regular:

Bu vazifeyi göreceğim I shall perform this duty
Bu vazifeyi sana gördüreceğim I shall make you perform this duty

In the preceding sentence we see that the subject of a simple verb goes into the dative whereas the direct object of a simple verb remains the object of the causative. More examples:

Müzeyi gezeceğiz We shall go round the museum
Kenan bize müzeyi gezdirecek Kenan will take us round the museum
 
Mektubu yazdım I wrote the letter
Mektubu ona yazdırdım I got him to write the letter

The indirect object identified as the agent need not be used if it is unnecessary:

Mektubu yazdırdım I got the letter written

Where the simple verb has no direct object (e.g., a verb such as başlamak to begin or inanmak to believe, which usually take a dative), the subject becomes the object of the causative:

Fatma piyanoya başladı Fatma has started the piano
Annesi, Fatma'yi piyanoya başlattı Her mother has started Fatma on the piano
 
Ben buna inanıyorum I believe this
Buna sizi de inandırmak istiyorum I want to make you believe this too
 
Çocuklar parkta geziyorlar The children are strolling in the park
Çocukları parkta gezdiriyorlar They are taking the children for a walk in the park

When negative, the causative may not mean to cause to but to let:

Öğrencilerini söyletmez He won't let his pupils speak
Treni kaçırdım I missed the train (I let the train get away)

A verb can take more than one causative suffix:

Otel memuru rezervasyonu yapar The hotel official makes the reservation
Müşteri rezervasyonu yaptırır The client gets the reservation made
Müşteri, otel memuruna rezervasyonu yaptırtır The client gets the hotel official to make the reservation

One sees that yaptırtmak is the causative of yaptırmak, which is the causative of yapmak.

Yemek pişiyor The food is cooking
Aşçı yemeği pişiriyor The cook is cooking the food
Eşim aşçıya yemeği pişirtmeye çalışıyor My wife is trying to get the cook to cook the dinner
 
Fare ölmedi The mouse did not die
Kedi, fareyi öldürmedi The cat did not kill the mouse
Kediye fareyi öldürtmedim I did not let the cat kill the mouse

Reflexive verbs and reciprocal verbs also have causative forms:

bulunmak to be, to find oneself
bulundurmak to cause to be found, to keep
Bakanla görüşmek istiyoruz We want an interview with the minister
Bizi Bakanla görüştürebilir misiniz? Can you schedule us an interview with the minister? (Can you make us, with the minister, see one another?)

Passive verbs cannot be made causative, but causative verbs can be made passive:

Bakanla görüştürülemedik We were not able to get an interview with the minister
Bekletilmekten hoşlanmaz She does not like to be kept waiting

Verbs containing a -ştir form are not necessarily reciprocal + causative, but rather expression intensification: sormak to ask, soruşturmak to make inquiries, to investigate; aramak to seek, arastırmak to research, to investigate.

Correspondence (the suffix -ip) (118)

Formerly a past participle ending, -ip is now used to indicate repetition of the suffices of verb combinations or pairs in which the verb tense is the same and applies to the same subject. Oturabilieceğiz ve konuşabileceğiz becomes Oturup konuşabileceğiz We will be able to sit and talk. Cesaretini topladı ve sordu becomes Cesaretini toplayıp sordu He gathered his courage and asked. Gitmelisiniz ve görmelisiniz becomes Gidip görmelisiniz You ought to go and see (although some also say Gitmeli, görmelisiniz too).

In some cases the action in the first verb is positive, in the second negative. In such a case the first verb ends in -ip but is also followed with de so that the distinction is made:

Okuyup anlamaz He does not read and understand
Okuyup da anlamaz He reads but does not understand

Where two verbs have the same stem but the second is in the negative form, the de construction is not used. This is seen also in English: I don't know whether he'll come or not Gelip gelmiyeceğini bilmiyorum ('I don't know his future coming and his future not coming').

Davetimizi kabul edip etmiyeceği, işini yarın akşama kadar bitirmiş olup olmadığına bağlıymış Whether or not he will accept our invitation depends, it seems, on whether or not he has finished his work by tomorrow evening 'His future accepting and not accepting are, it seems (-ymış), tied to his being and not-being having-finished'

Conditional sentences--open, remote, unfulfilled (118, 120)

The conditional form of to be (isem if I am) has been covered along with other tense bases: gidiyorsam if I am going, verirsen if you give....

To form the past conditional, the past base is added to the root followed by a suffixed form of isem (gördüysem if I saw) or the third person -(y)se/sa to the past tense (gördümse if I saw). These conditionals are said to be open, meaning they do not imply that fulfilment is unlikely or impossible:

Kendisini görürsem söylerim If I see him I'll tell [him]
Siz kalmazsanız biz kalmayız If you don't stay, we won't stay
Yemek yiyorsa dışarda bekleyin If he is eating, then wait outside
Anlamadınsa daha açık konuşamam If you did not understand, I cannot speak more openly
Gideceksen git If you will go, then go

Although conditionals may start with eğer, the sense is not affected.

A second type of conditional is the remote conditional, remote meaning If I were to see him, I would tell him. The implication here means the likelihood of fulfillment is remote or not likely. Note that these suffixes: -sem/sam, -sen/san, -se/sa, -sek/sak, -seniz/sanız, -seler/salar when added to the stem i- may the conditional of to be, and are added directly to the stem of other verbs: gitsem if I were to go, versen if you were to give, alsak if we were to stay.

The remote conditional of to be uses the conditional forms of olmak. While isem is if I am, olsam is if I were:

Ben sizin yerinizde olsam onlarla iyi geçinmeye çalışırdım If I were in your place, I would try to get on well with them
Efendim, saatinizi çalışmış olsam cebimde bırakacak kadar ahmak değilim Sir, if I had stolen your watch, I would not be so stupid as to leave it in my pocket
Yarım saat sonra gelsem olmaz mı? If I were to come half an hour later, would that not be possible?

Bunu yapsam mı? (an irresolute If I were to do this?) really means Should I do this?

Pairs of conditionals which represent alternatives are often expressed with a de/da in both clauses:

Yağmur yağsa da yağmasa da yola çıkmalıyız Whether it rains or not we ought to get on the road
Biletçi, 'Ayakta da olsan vereceksin. Otursan da vereceksin' dedi The ticket collector said If your on foot you will pay, and if you are seated you will pay too

A third type of conditional is one with no possibility of being fulfilled. Here the past tense of to be is added to the conditional of the verb:

Elimde olmuş olsaydı buraya gelmezdim Had it been in my hands, I would not have come here ('if it were=olsaydı having been=olmuş in my hand...')
Herkes bunlar gibi düşünseydi, şimdi hangimiz dünyada olurduk? If everyone thought like these people, now which of us would be in the world?

Note the construction bildiysem is if I knew, but bilseydim is if I were to have known or had I known or if only I had known! The first construction in the past tense is an open conditional however:

Cevabı o zaman bildiysem şüphesiz ki size söyledim If I knew the answer at that moment I surely told you

The other (second) sense is unfulfilled:

Cevabı o zaman bilseydim size söylerdim Had I known the answer at the time I would have told you

Conditional verbs followed by de mean although:

Bürosuna üç defa gittimse de kendisini göremedim Although I went to his office three times, I was not able to see him
Koşuyorsa da geç kalacak Although she is running, she is going to be late

Conditionals in general (123)

The following examples show how to express conditional situations without use of a conditional verb:

Ahmet yapmadı da kim yaptı? If Ahmet didn't do it then who did?
Orada bulmadılar mı buraya gelirler If they don't find it there they'll come here

In addition to these forms implying a conditional, Turkish has a form similar to the 'if' used in requests or commands, such as If you'll be good enough to wait a moment... Otursanıza! Do sit down! If you will sit down... Dinlesenize! Do listen! Baksana! Do look! If you will please look... The speaker's tone will indicate whether the usage is one of impatience or is courteous.

Other common expressions have conditional verbs: Allahını seversen (pronounced Allaysen) is For Heaven's sake! ('If you love your God').

The form ise if it is is used a great deal:

Hikmet ise/Hikmet'se pek tembeldir As for Hikmet, he's very lazy ('If it is Hikmet [that you're asking about]...')
Bense pek çalışkanım As for me, I'm very hardworking

Neredeyse soon
Öyleyse In that case
Hiç olmazsa At least
Olsa gerek It (logically) must be
Bilse bilse o bilir If anyone knows, he does

Subjunctive (124)

A form of the verb not used to express happenings but rather concepts whose realization is envisaged, feared, or desired. The base is -e/a after consonants, -ye/ya after vowels. Note the personal endings. The form of to be will serve as a starting point:

olayım may I be, let me be, that I may be
olasın may you be, etc.
ola may he/she/it be, etc.
olalım may we be, etc.
olasınız may you be, etc.
olalar may they be, etc.
 
söyleyeyim may I tell, let me tell, that I may tell
söyleyesin may you tell, etc.
söyleye may he/she/it tell, etc.
söyleyelim may we tell, etc.
söyleyesiniz may you tell, etc.
söyleyeler may they tell, etc.

Only the first person forms find use in the subjunctive. If some one has been considering whether to take a particular action such as buying something, they may exclaim Alayım Let me buy [it], meaning I'll take it. Bakıyım is the often uttered Let me see [it]! The first person plural is used very often: Gidelim! Let's go! Bakalım! Let's see! Biletsiz kalmıyalım! Let us not remain without tickets! (A cry which city bus conductors may utter)

The second persons are rarely heard and third person forms are confined to a few stereotyped pious phrases. The third person subjunctive is often expressed in the imperative form (i.e., olsun instead of ola may he be, and versin rather than vere may he give).

In conversation, the -ye/ya of vowel-ending verb stems is often dropped: söyleyeyim becomes söyleyim let me say, başlayayım becomes başlayım let me begin. Also Yapim is the impatient way of saying yapayım let me do, while Napalım? is the short form for Ne yapalım? What are we to do?

Absolute and genitive case postpositions (46)

Special notes on usage---

The noun üzer- occurs only in possessive suffixes, such as Anahtar, rafın üzerinde The key is on the shelf; Bu ödevi üzerime aldım I have taken this duty on myself ('to the top of me'). Not only are the words on the left side of the list nouns, they can also be made into adjectives. For example, dış can be used for dış ticaret external trade. Indeed the term Dışisleri Bakanlığı Ministry of External (Foreign) Affairs shows dış being used as a noun.

In the event no case ending are placed on üstü when it is used after nouns, the meaning is on the point of or in the act of:

akşam üstü at the coming of evening

The compound suçüstü (suç guilt) means red-handed, in the act of:

Hırsızları suçüstü yakaladık We caught the thieves in the act

üzere (98)

The postposition originally means on, but is used with infinitives (verbs with -mek ending) to express other senses:

  1. so as to, in order to:
    Çocuklar okula gitmek üzere hazırlanıyorlar They are getting the children ready to go to school
  2. on condition that:
    Yarın geri vermek üzere bu kitabı alıyorum I am taking this book with the understanding that I give it back tomorrow
  3. on the point of:
    Yola çıkmak üzereyiz We are on the point of starting out

olmak üzere 'on the basis of being' is used in expressions involving numbers or counting and means being or as being. It can be omitted from the translation:

İki tanesi eski, bir tanesi yeni olmak üzere üç tane elbisem var I have three suits, two of them [being] old, one [being] new

Note that when parting company with someone whom you might expect to run into again, instead of good bye (Güle güle or Hoşça kal) or see you later (Görüşürüz), you might hear Görüşmek üzere! which would translate literally as 'so as to converse...'

Adjectives (30)

Adjectives are sometimes used as nouns in English, e.g., meek as a noun in Blessed are the meek. The use of adjectives as nouns is typical in Turkish:

genç çocuklar young children
bir genç a youth, young person
gençler the young, the youths

Conversely, adjectives in Turkish are also made into nouns. Here the adjectives uzak far and yakın near, close are used in unchanged form as nouns, as seen with the case endings used for nouns: uzaklarda far off, in the distance; yakında or yakınlarda near by, soon, recently. Note the adjective and noun use of gold:

altından bir saat a watch [made] of gold
bir altın saat a gold watch

A confusing aspect of the use of Turkish is the indefinite article bir combined with other adjectives. In English a big house or a rainy day is the rule, but in Turkish, 'big a house' and 'rainy a day' is the rule. There is one exception (the explanation follows):

Ablası, akıllı bir kız Her older sister is a clever girl
Kardeşi, bir akıllı kız His sibling is an intelligent girl
Fare deliğinde küçük bir fare gördüm In the mousehole I saw a small mouse
Fare deliğinde bir küçük kertenkele gördüm In the mousehole I saw a small lizard

Some notes on the previous examples. First, the use of bir before another adjective emphasizes the noun as much as the adjective. In the first example, the word abla (elder sister) indicates the subject is female, so there is no need to emphasize kız girl. In the example following, the emphasis on kız is desired since the sibling may be either boy or girl. In the next pair of examples, it is desired that the attention be focused on lizard since it is quite astonishing to find such.

In Turkish, adjectives always precede the nouns they describe, as in kara kedi black cat. This is true in English as well, but the point is emphasized because in some languages, the adjective can follow the noun, as in the French chat noir black cat. Kedi kara in Turkish is actually a sentence meaning the cat is black.

Comparatives and Superlatives (54)

The words daha more and en most are added to express the comparative and superlative, respectively, in Turkish.

iyi good daha iyi better en iyi best
kötü bad daha kötü worse en kötü worst
uzun long daha uzun longer en uzun longest
çok much daha çok more en çok most
az little daha az less en az least

Comparative sentences such as A is better than B are expressed in the following form in Turkish 'A from B is good.' This becomes A, B'den iyi. Note that A, B'den daha iyi is not used typically except when particular emphasis is desired. Note the ablative sense is used in these comparisons, which essentially expresses the sense: A is good if we take B as a point of departure.

The comparison daha ... is used rarely in these constructions involving than, as noted. It is better use is to express more: Şunu beğenmiyorum, bu daha iyi I don't like that; this is better.

Ankara, İstanbul'dan küçüktür Ankara is smaller than Istanbul
Otelimiz, sizinkinden az pahalı Our hotel is less expensive than yours
Bu kilim, öbür dükkândakinden daha güzel ve ucuz This rug is more beautiful and less expensive (cheaper) than the one in the other shop
Bu kitap, şu kitaptan az yararlı This book is less useful than that book
Daha ağır bir çekiç gerek A heavier hammer is necessary
En iyisi, burada beklemek The best thing to do is wait here ('the most good of it is to wait here')
Tarsus'taki kiliseler, Mardin'dekilerden sayıca azdır The churches in Tarsus are fewer ('numerically less') than those in Mardin
Kenan, onların çocuklarından hem en büyüğü hem de en akıllısıdır. --Kendi kızkardeşinden akıllı mıdır acaba? Kenan is both the biggest and the most intelligent of their children.--Is he more intelligent than his own sister, I wonder
Bence en güzel kokulu çiçek, güldür I think (or to my mind) the flower with the most beautiful scent is the rose

Intensive adjectives (55)

In addition to making superlatives with en, two other ways exist:

• repetition of the adjectives

derin düşünceler deep thoughts
derin derin düşünceler profound thoughts

Consider the manner of intensification however: yeni yeni fikirler is not very new ideas, but ever new ideas. Güzel güzel çiçekler is not just exceedingly beautiful flowers, but flowers each more beautiful than the last.

• prefixing of a syllable to the adjective which resembles its first syllable, with the first syllable ending in m, p, s, or r.

başka other bambaşka totally different
siyah black simsiyah jet black
kırmızı red kıpkırmızı bright red
yeni new yepyeni brand new
bütün whole büsbütün entirely
doğru straight dosdoğru dead straight
temiz clean tertemiz spotless

Bir (135)

Nouns preceded by bir are not necessarily indefinite.

Gözlerimle oturacak bir yer ararken arkadaşım bana boş kalan bir koltuğu gösterdi While looking for a place to sit, my friend pointed out to me an empty chair which was empty

Note here that bir koltuk is an (indefinite) chair, but bir koltuğu is a particular chair.

Also bir may mean some or any in a particular context: Bir şey istiyor musunuz? Do you want anything? This explains its use with plural nouns:

Bir şey mırıldandı He muttered something Bir şeyler mırıldandı He muttered something or other
Gözlerini kısıp ilerde bir yerlere baktı He half-closed his eyes and gazed somewhere ahead  ...ilerde bir yere baktı... he gazed at a specific place

Bir can also be an adverb meaning only or once:

Bir Allah bilir, bir de ben Only God and I know
Bir gördüğümü bir daha unutmam What I have once seen Ido not forget again
Her haftada bir gelir He comes once a week
Her şey bitti, bir bu kaldı Everything is finished, only this is left

Şöyle bir is just a bit:

Şöyle bir gezip geldik We've just been for a bit of a stroll
Önce şöyle bir düşündü, sonra oturup elini telefona uzattı First he thought a bit, then sat down and reached for the telephone

Other expressions: bir varmış bir yokmuş once upon a time; birden all at once; birdenbire suddenly; ikide bir frequently.

Adverbs (49)

Turkish pretty much uses adjectives and adverbs interchangably: If you are not sick, then you are good. The adjective pek strong also is used as an adverb very.

Repetition of adjectives can also indicate an adverbial phrase:

Derin derin düşündüm I thought deeply
Yavaş yavaş gidiyorlar They are going slowly
Sık sık Ankara'ya geliyor She frequently comes to Ankara

The unaccented suffix -ce also turns adjectives and nouns into adverbs:

güzel beautiful güzelce beautifully
sayı number sayıca numerically
hükümet government hükümetçe on the part of government
ben I bence for my part, to my mind
sade simple sadece simply, merely, only
doğru direct doğruca directly

Words showing the pronomial n before cases also add it to this suffix: bu this, bunca in this way, this much.

Note that Türkçe means Turkishly, so Türkçe konuşmak means to speak in the Turkish way. However, formation of this adverb-like word does not make it an adverb, since they are used as adjectives and nouns:

Türkçe sözlük Turkish dictionary
Türkçeniz nasıl? How is your Turkish?
