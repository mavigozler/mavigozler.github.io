Significant Digits, Decimal Places, Conversions

Measured Quantities

In making quantitative observations in chemistry, you will be determining a (measured) quantity

The quantity will have a number (numerical value) and units associated with it, creating a dimension. Whenever you report data in your laboratory notebook, you are almost always 100% reporting a measured quantity that requires that number with its units. Failure to properly record that number with units creates problems at the professional level of laboratory science, and it's points off on scoring at the student level.

Significant Digits

Also called significant figures, the significant digits of a value are at least the number of digits that extend from the first nonzero number to the last nonzero number, with all the zero digits that are in between as well. "123.45" has five significant digits.

Digits which are zeroes between a non-zero digit to the left and a non-zero digit on the right are called sandwich zeroes and are always significant. For example, the number "1005" has four significant digits.

Digits having a zero to the left of the first nonzero digit are not significant, whether they come after the decimal point or not. "0.0075" has two significant digits.

Digits having a zero to the right of the last nonzero digits are significant if they come after the decimal point, or when there is a decimal point indicated in an integer number.
1200 has two significant digits.
1200. has four significant digits (note the decimal point).
1200.00 has six significant digits.

Most do not consider the zeroes to the right of the last nonzero digit of an integer as significant (see the use of scientific notation in helping decide what digits are significant).

Numbers in scientific notation have two components: the significand (formerly called the mantissa), which is a number between 1 and 10 with a digits after a decimal point, and the exponent, which is a power of 10 (it has format with a 10n value).

The significand part requires that the number be written exactly according to the significant digits of the number. Notice has the significand is written for the numbers that are all equal to 200,000: the zeroes that become signficant must be expressed to their accuracy in the significand. Thus converting a number to scientific notation forces one to think about just what the significant digits are.

Exact Numbers

These quantities, such as constants and conversion factors, are not measured quantities and are exact numbers. This means they have an infinite number of significant digits. Examples include:

  • Numbers obtained by counting discrete objects: 12 eggs, 5 students. There is no uncertainty in this determination which requires indicating a level of precision
  • Conversion factors: these are exact numbers that should not be treated as measured quantities for purposes of determining precision (significant digits, decimal places)

Rounding Numbers

Before getting into this important subject of showing results of operations, a reminder on rounding off numbers.

Rule: when the digit used to determine rounding off is between 0 and 4, one rounds down; when it is between 5 and 9, one rounds up. Rounding down is equivalent to just truncating (lopping off) the digts from the rounding digit to the right. Rounding up is the equivalent of truncating the numbers as well, but adding 1 to the digit to the left of the rounding digit.

Suppose the number 8.49918365107 is to be rounded off to a certain digit.

Suppose the number is to have the format x.xxxxxxx, that is 7 digits after the decimal point. This means that the 8th digit after the decimal point, the number "5," is to be used in rounding off. Since "5" indicates that rounding up occurs on the 7th digit, which is "6," then the value becomes 8.4991837 when rounding up and truncation occur.

Now suppose that number will have format x.xx, or 2 digits after the decimal point. This means that the 3rd digit after decimal point, or "9," will lead to a rounding up of the number to be formatted: 8.50. This was a tricky round up in fact, because 1 was added to the 9 in the 2nd digit after the decimal point, which then carries a one to the 9 in the 1st digit after the decimal point, and so there were multiple changes of the digits as a result of these digits valued at "9."

Significant Digits of the Results of Operations On Numbers

The first principle involving determining the number of significant digits of the result of a binary operation is that the result will have the same number of significant digits as the number involved in the operation that has the least significant digits.

Here's an example. Evaluate and give the result with the correct number of significant digits the operation: \[ 9.7 \times 12.35 = ? \]

If one merely enters the values into the calculator and writes down all the digits reported by the calculator, the result is: \[ 9.7 \times 12.35 = 119.795 \]

However, as a scientific result, this is not correct. For example, if the number 9.7 were a volume in milliliters (ml), and 12.35 were a concentration in micromole per milliter (which is millimolar, or mM), then the result is a number of micromoles, which is 119.795 micromoles (µmol). No scienist believes that the mass is that accurate. Since the number 9.7 has 2 significant digits, and 12.35 has 4 significant digits, the result will have signficant digits that are the least of the significant digits of one of the two numbers, namely 2 digits. Thus the result is rounded to 120 µmole, which is the answer: \[ 9.7 \times 12.35 = 120 \]

The same is true if we had divided numbers, for example: \[ \frac{35.155}{250} = 0.14062 = 0.14 \]

That result was rounded off to 2 significant digits because the denominator 250 only had 2 significant digits by the rules, even though the numerator had 5 significant digits.

Setting Up A Calculation: The Numerator-Denominator Principle in the Use of Conversion Factors

A calculation is really an equation, and an equation must have an equal sign in standard mathematics.

On one side of the equal sign is the answer, the result of the calculation (usually on the right side of the equation for people who think left-to-right).

On the other side of the equation are the factors: all the numeric values that will be used to make the calculation. By definition, factors are multiplied to one another, and that is the case for pretty much 99.9% of all calculations in this setup. In rare cases, you will use algebra where you set up numbers that are added or subtracted from one another, but they become quantities (values enclosed in parentheses) either in the numerator or denominator of a factor.

All the factors will be represented in a numerator or denominator fashion. That is, if a factor is used in a division operation, then it will appear as its inverted value (that is, if value x is used to divide the factors in the calculation, it will be represented as 1 / x and multiplied to all factors).

The equation will then have the form that looks much like this: \[ \frac{w \text{ dim1}}{\text{dim2}} \times \frac{x \text{ dim2}}{\text{dim3}} \times \frac{\text{dim3}}{y \text{ dim4}} \times \frac{z \text{ dim4}}{1} = a \text{ dim1} \text{ [1]} \]

The dim n, where n = 1, 2, 3, …, actually refers to the name of a dimension or unit, such mg (milligram) or µl (microliter). The values w, x, y, z are numbers expressed in any form (real or floating point or decimal; integers; scientific notation); they are the factors in this product. The product itself is the value a. Note that the value of y is in the denominator, that is the factor is 1/y.

But what is important here is not the values w, x, etc. It is the dimensions dim n. The values were merely arranged in this style in order to arrange the dimension to produce the desired final dimension with its value. Note how dim2, dim3, and dim4 all cross out, leaving dim1 in the final answer. One can remove the dimensions and then just be left with multiplying all numbers in the numerator, then multiplying all in the denominator, and finally dividing the product in the numerator by the product in the denominator to get the final answer.

In constructing the equation (calculation):

  • It is essential to know what one really wants as the answer, which is a value and possibly a dimension. For instance, if a volume is asked for, then the dimension must be in units that represent a volume (µl, ml, L). If the target answer is a mass, then it should be in some form of grams (mg, g) or possibly moles. For concentrations, this is really solution density, or mass units per volume units.
  • Write down all the factors available, even if they will not be used in the final calculation.
  • The missing factors in a calculation are typically conversion units. That is, the conversion of one expression of the volume into another. Thus there are 1000 µliters (µl) in 1 milliliter (ml), and 1000 milliliters in 1 liter (L). Know your conversion units by reflex. Note that these are not constants: a constant is value reflecting a fundamental physical or chemical property in nature or the universe. Planck’s constant is 6.62 × 10−34 J s, and not a conversion of one value into another form.
  • Constants. A typical constant in solution density determinations is the molecular or atomic weight of a substance, which is the amount of grams in one mole of the substance. This is typical missing factor whenever one factor is expressed in grams and the answer sought in some form of moles.

In setting up the final equation which is the product of these factors, the solution is arrived at by doing what is called a dimensional analysis, which was the process described above.
