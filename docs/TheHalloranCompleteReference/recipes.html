  • 1 tsp baking soda
  • 1 tsp salt

  • ¾ cup packed brown sugar

  • 2 large eggs

  1. Preheat oven to 375° F.

Pies

In this section you will find pie recipes.

Pie Plates

Best pie plates are:
  • anodized aluminum
  • tin- or enamel-coated steel
  • porcelain-coated aluminum with non-stick finish
  • glass (Corning tempered)
These materials absorb heat, which helps the pie crust to become brown.

Crusts that are baked in shiny, heat-reflecting metal pans are often pale. If all you have are these kinds of baking containers, or you do not have the above, then it helps to put the pie dish on a cookie sheet, which will assist browning of the pie crust.

Greasing

Never grease a pie plate unless the recipe calls for it. Pie crusts are made of grease themselves and usually do not cause the crust to stick to the plate.

If there is an unbaked crumb crust that needs to be loosened, do the following:

  1. Soak a cloth in hot water and wring it out well.
  2. Wrap the cloth around the plate for a minute or two.
  3. Cut the pie.

  2. Hold or lightly rest the rolling pin over the pastry circle but close to one edge of it (so that it forms a short chord through the pastry circle). The long axis of the pin should be oriented not left and right to you, but forward and backward. If you are right-handed, it is best to hold the pin with your left hand, and place it over the edge of the pastry circle at the far right.   5. Now lift the circle (hold the pastry gently on the pin to keep the pin from rolling) and move it to the pie plate so that the pin spans across the center (diameter) of the pie plate. The half of the pastry circle that goes under the bottom of the rolling pin should be resting in the pie plate, and the half of the circle over the top of the pin will be resting on the other half of the pastry circle.
  6. With the hand that is not holding the rolling pin (you might have switched hands), hold the edge of the pastry circle whose portion goes over the top of the rolling pin. While gently moving the rolling pin toward the half of the pie plate not covered, move your hand holding the pastry edge in the direction of the pin to unroll the pastry to cover the pie plate completely. “Unroll” means the rolling pin should move as the pastry rolls off the pin.

Making A Decorative Edge

Making a decorative edge can be done with both 1-crust and 2-crust pies. If you have a 1-crust pie, the edge is obviously made with the bottom crust. If you have a 2-crust pie, you can sandwich a top crust overhang of the same length with the bottom crust, and then work with the overhang.

Old-Fashioned Fork Edge

  1. Cut the pastry even with the edge of the pie plate. That is, there is no overhang. Cut with sharp knife or scissors.
  2. Dip a 4-tined fork in flour.
  3. Press the bottom of the fork into the pastry at the edge of the plate. The tine marks should be visible, but not all the way through to the plate surface.
  4. Do this for the entire rim of the plate.

Fluted Edge

  1. Cut the pastry circle so that there is a 1-inch (3-centimeter) overhang beyond the rim of the plate
  2. Fold the edge of the overhang under so that the edge rests on rim and causes the pastry to stand up.
  3. For right-handed people, hold the outer side of the standing-up pastry with the index finger and possibly middle finger holding one part of the outer side, and the thumb holding the other part of the outer side, and with the index finger of the other hand, press into the inner side of the standing-up pastry so that the pastry is pinched into a curved flute.
  4. Continue around the rim of the plate, spacing the flutes about ¼-inch (6-7 millimeters) from each other.

Rope Edge

  1. Cut the pastry circle so that there is a 1-inch (3-centimeter) overhang beyond the rim of the plate
  2. Fold the edge of the overhang under so that the edge rests on rim and causes the pastry to stand up (as is done to make the fluted edge).
  3. From the outside of the plate, press the thumb into the pastry at an angle, and at the same time, pinch the pastry between this thumb and the knuckle of the index finger (the finger might be curled or extended).
  4. Place the thumb in the groove left by the index finger (this helps you determine the spacing/position of the next pinching action), and repeat the pinching all the way around the rim.

Modified Fluted Edge

  1. Prepare the fluted edge as above, but leave ½-inch (12-13 millimeters) spacing between flutes.
  2. Take a floured 4-tine fork, and press each of the flutes, flattening them to the rim.

Turret Edge

  1. Make the stand-up edge as for the fluted edge.
  2. With a knife, cut through the pastry down to the rim at ½-inch (12-13 millimeter) intervals.
  3. Press the cut strips that are formed flat around the edge of the plate.

Scalloped Edge

  1. Make the stand-up edge as for the fluted edge.
  2. Hold the thumb and index finger of one hand apart about 1.25 inches (3 centimeters) on the outer side of the standing-up pastry.
  3. Get a round-bowl measuring tablespoon and flour it.
  4. While holding the outer side, use the bottom of the round spoon to press the inner side to make in impression of the spoon into the pastry.
  5. Proceed around the rim of the plate.
  6. Pinch the points in between.

Lattice Tops

  1. Place in the bottom crust pastry and cut a 1-inch overhanging edge.
  2. Put the filling in.
  3. Roll the pastry for the top crust into a circle and cut it into ½-inch (10 millimeter) strips.
  4. Moisten the edge of the bottom crust with water.
  5. Place the strips about 1¼-inch (3 centimeters) apart across the filling.
  6. Trim/cut the strips so that their length is even with the overhang.
  7. Press the ends of the strips into the overhang, so as to seal them.
  8. Lay an equal number of strips at right angles to the first layer. If you want to make a woven lattice top, place the strips over and under alternately around the first set of strips.
  9. Pinch the edges to seal them and make a high stand-up edge, and then flute the edge.

Pie Crusts: Making Pastry

Always use cold water when making the pie crust.

If the water feels lukewarm from the cold tap, add an ice cube to chill it. If lard is being used instead of shortening, put it into the refrigerator to chill it before using it.

Do not handle the pastry more than necessary. The body heat from your hands warms up the pastry and makes it more difficult to prepare properly. The best way to work the shortening into the flour is to use either a pastry blender or two knives in a scissor-like action.

When adding water, stir it into the dough with a fork. Add only enough water to make the ingredients hold (stick) together. Absolutely avoid making the pastry wet.

With your hands, press the pastry into a ball.

If the day is too hot, the pastry may become too soft to handle. In that case, wrap it in waxed paper and put it in the refrigerator for 30 min, or until the pastry is firm again. Then re-work the pastry.

Even if you are making a crust for only one pie, prepare enough pastry for two crusts (or more). You can refrigerate or freeze any unused pastry.

Rolling The Pastry

  1. Spread a thin layer of flour over the surface that will be used to roll the pastry.
  2. If possible, cover the rolling pin in a stockinette. Roll the stockinette-covered pin over the flour surface.
  3. Put the pastry ball on the floured surface.
  4. Put the pin on top of the ball and roll it to flatten the ball slightly.
  5. The pie crust should always be rolled so that it is circular. To do this right, roll the crust as follows:
    • Always roll from the center of the crust to the edge.
    • If necessary, push the sides of the crust in occasionally by hand.
    • To avoid making the edges too thin, lift up the pin slightly as you roll to the edge
  6. Make sure that you rotate the pin around north, south, east, and west so that the crust becomes circular and of even thickness.
  7. Lift up or move the crust occasionally to make sure that the crust is not sticking. Rolling out the crust on a cold surface helps this. If the crust does stick, use a spatula to lift it without breaking it, and place some flour underneath.
  8. If cracks or breaks appear, you can patch the tear by cutting a strip from the pastry edge, moisten the torn edges, and then lay the patch over the crack/break/tear, and press or roll the patch into it.

If you want a golden glaze on the top crust, brush the top crust—not the edge— with either milk, half-n-half (half heavy cream/half milk), undiluted evaporated milk, or slightly beaten egg white. If desired, sprinkle the crust with sugar,

If the top crust browns too much, cover the pie loosely with foil for the last 15 minutes of baking.

Recipe for A 2-Crust Pie

This recipe will make enough pastry (dough) for a single 2-crust pie or for two bottom crusts for 8- or 9-inch (20-23 centimeter) pie plates.

Beginners might want to make the 2-crust pie dough even though only making a single 1-crust pie. With experience, the recipe can be altered to reduce the amount of materials used (the more experienced will probably use 60-70% of the amounts in this recipe for a single 1-crust pie).

Obtain the following ingredients:

  • 2 cups all-purpose flour
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • ¾ cup shortening
  • 5-6 tablespoons of cold water
Do the following:
  1. Place the flour and salt in a medium-sized bowl. (“Medium-sized” bowl means that the flour should probably fill about half the volume of the bowl.)
  2. Using a pastry blender or two knives while making a scissor action with them, cut (mix) in the shortening until the mixture forms coarse-sized (rather large) crumbs.
  3. Get one tablespoon of cold water, and sprinkle it over the flour. Try to distribute it well. Do not add all of the cold water indicated in this recipe.
  4. Use a fork gently to mix the water into the flour.
  5. If the mixture is not holding together, add another spoonful of cold water and repeat mixing with the fork. It is critical not to add too much water, which makes the pastry too wet and difficult to handle and to roll out.
  6. Use your hands to shape the pastry into a ball. It is important not to overhandle the pastry: hold it only for the time necessary to shape it into a ball (the body heat from the hands can alter the texture of the pastry). If it is a hot day, it may be necessary to wrap the ball in wax paper and leave it in the refrigerator for 30 minutes.
  7. If making a 2-crust pie, cut the ball into two parts, but not equal parts: make one slightly smaller than the other. If making two 1-crust pies, then cut the ball into two equal parts. Re-shape each of the cut parts into a ball.
  8. Obtain a rolling pin. If possible, place it into a stockinette, and then lightly flour the stockinette.
  9. Find the surface you will use to roll out the pastry. A hard (marble is good) surface is essential, and it is helpful if it is cold too. It should not be hot or overly warm though. Place a light flour coating on it.
  10. Place the larger pastry ball on to the surface, and roll it into a circle as described above. The final thickness of the circle should be 1/8-inch (3 millimeters). The circle should be 2 inches (5 centimeters) larger than the pie plate; this means that the pastry circle should have a diameter 4 inches (10 centimeters) larger than the diameter of the pie plate.
  11. Transfer the pastry into the pie plate, lifted by the rolling pin, as described above.
  12. If making a 2-crust pie, pour in the filling and place the top crust pastry on top of the filling as described above, using the rolling pin.
  13. Cut the overhang from the pie plate so that 1 inch (2.5 centimeters) of pastry remains as an overhang. If making a 2-crust pie, press the top and bottom crust pastries together at the edge (it might help to wet them a little).
  14. Decorate the edge as desired.

In cases where there is too much pie filling, leftover filling can be baked in a custard cup, covered with foil if there is a crust, and the baking time should be about one-third of the pie baking time.

Pies With Ice Cream of Frozen Fillings

These pies are easier to cut, and also have more flavor. Let the pies stand at room temperature for 15 minutes after taking out of the freezer before you serve them.

Storing/Freezing Pies and Pastry

Pies that are made with cream, custard, whipped cream, or chiffon fillings must be stored in the refrigerator. They should be used (consumed) within a few days if their original appearance is desired (as long as they are refrigerated, they can be consumed without spoilage for several days, although they will not have their original appearance).

Pies filled with fruit can be left at room temperature for as much as 2-3 days. They can be stored longer if refrigerated. Cover them to keep them from drying out, especially in dry climates.

Pastry

Balls of unbaked pastry (used to make crusts) can be wrapped in plastic or aluminum foil and stored in the refrigerator for a 1-2 days.

Balls of uncooked pastry can also be freezer-wrapped (wrapping materials or containers designed for the freezer) for up to two months. When ready to be used, thaw the pastry in its wrapping, leaving at room temperature for 2-4 hours.

The pastry can be frozen after rolling out into circles also. The circles should be 3-inches (7.5 cm) longer around the edge of the pie plate they will be placed in (that is, the circles should have a diameter 6-inches or 15 cm longer than the pie plate diameter). Stack all the circles with two sheets of waxed paper for each circle. Wrap and then freeze. When ready to use, place the circle on to the pie plate and allow to thaw 10-15 min before pressing into the plate and shaping.

Glossary

bottom crust
The pastry (dough) rolled out and placed into the usually ungreased baking container (metal or glass plate). It is sometimes shaped at the edges for a decorative look (see Making Decorative Pie Crust Edges). The pie filling is then poured into/over the bottom crust. A top crust is often optionally placed on top of the filling afterwards.
one-crust (1-crust) pie
A pie with no top crust, and only a bottom crust. Cherry, pecan, and especially pumpkin pies are often 1-crust pies, although they can be made with a top crust. Apple pies are usually always made with top crusts, and are therefore not 1-crust pies. However, there are no absolute rules. Compare 2-crust pie.
top crust
A top crust is any pastry that is used to cover the filling of the pie. It might be pastry rolled out, much like the bottom crust, to cover the entire pie filling. Or it might be a decorative woven lattice top made from pastry strips. Pies with top crusts are usually called 2-crust pies.
two-crust (2-crust) pie
A pie made with both a bottom and top crust. All pies have bottom crusts on which the filling is poured. But they do not need or require a top crust, and perhaps in some cases, should not be overlayered with a top crust. Apple pies tend to be 2-crust pies. Cherry and pecan pies often are 2-crust pies. Pumpkin pies are not exactly 2-crust pies: they might have a decorative top crust which is a woven lattice top crust.
lattice top crust
This is a variation on the top crust. Enough pastry to make a 2-crust pie is rolled out and then cut into strips. The strips are usually layered atop the filling, with the strips placed over or under each other alternately, as if weaving the strips. A decorative edge to the pie is usual as well. See Making a Woven Lattice Top Crust for more information.

Obtain the following ingredients:

  • 2 cups (16 oz.) canned pumpkin sauce
  • 1 cup evaporated milk
  • 1 cup sugar
  • 1 teaspoon ground cinnamon
  • ½ teaspoon ground ginger
  • ¼ teaspoon ground nutmeg
  • ¼ teaspoon ground cloves
  • ¼ teaspoon salt
  • Brandy Hard Sauce (optional)

Do the following:

  1. Pre-heat the oven to 375° F (190-200° C).
  2. Place the pie crust pastry in the plate and complete the decoration of the edge.
  3. In a small bowl with the mixer set at high speed, beat the egg whites until soft peaks have formed.
  4. In a large bowl, and using the same beaters, beat at low speed the egg yolks, the pumpkin sauce, evaporated milk, sugar, all spices, and the salt together until they are well blended.

  7. Bake for 45 min until the filling is set and a knife inserted 1 inch from the edge comes out clean. Refrigerate. Serve optionally with the Brandy Hard Sauce.
