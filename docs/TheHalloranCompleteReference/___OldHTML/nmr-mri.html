Theory of Nuclear Magnetic Resonance

Subatomic particles can usually be characterized by three properties:
  1. charge (e)
  2. mass (m)
  3. spin or angular momentum (l)

Planck has stated that the property of angular momentum exists in certain multiples, namely in multiples of h/2π.

The magnetic moment of a spinning charged particle can be expressed as:

μ→ =  ge L→
2mc

where L→ is the angular momentum vector and g is a relational constant (c has its usual meaning). The direction of vector

holds for a singly charged particle such as a proton or electron but not a more complex nucleus of charge particles.

The fact that angular momentum is a multiple of Planck's expression can be expressed thusly:

L→ =  I→ (h/2π)

where I→ = spin. Note that | I | is a spin quantum number with values of ½, 1, 1½, .... The magnetic moment (μ→) can now be expressed as:

μ→ =  γI→ (h/2π)

where γ is the gyromagentic ratio and is equal to ge/2mc.

The spin value of a nucleus having an odd-numbered mass is a half-integral. These include 1H, 3H, 13C, 15N, 19F, 31P.

The spin of any nucleus with an even-numbered mass number (A) and odd-numbered charge (Z) is an integral: 2H, 14N.

Nuclei with quadrupole moments include: 2H, 7Li, 14N, 17O, 23Na, and 33S.

Magnetic Resonance Imaging

A magnetic resonance image is produced by using radiofrequency (RF) pulse along with gradient pulses produced in a transmitter coil to excite or re-orient the overall magnetization vector in a sample or body or tissue (target), and then to follow relaxation of the vector over time. The magnetization vector of a mass is a function of its proton density (PD). The PD of fluids is usually >95%, but water- and fat-based tissues have a PD of 60-85%.

Relaxation of the material occurs over a characteristic time period that is a property of the material. Relaxation is dependent upon a number of factors: temperature, pH, the presence of paramagnetic substances. (Some paramagnetic substances are added intentionally to improve the image contrast, and are thus called contrast enhancers.) These factors influence the time period.

Relaxation can be described by two time constants. The spin-lattice relaxation time, called T1, refers to the time required for relaxation of the magnetization vector from its excited state to the equilibrium (unexcited state), in which the vector angle move from the xy transverse plane back toward the z axis (longitudinal axis). The spin-spin relaxation time, called T2, is the time required for relaxation in the transverse plane. The table below shows the typical relaxation times for a variety of masses.

Relaxation Times of Various Materials (milliseconds)
Substance T1 T2
fluids 1500-2000 700-1200
water-based tissues400-1200 40-200
fat-based tissues 100-150 10-100

An image is created essentially by exploiting the differences in the relaxation times of the different tissues. The signal is read at the same elapsed time period, the echo time, (TE) following the excitation pulse (or sequence of pulses and intermediate relaxations). This relative strength of the signal can be translated to an intensity (whiteness vs. blackness) on an image. The difference between whiteness and blackness is essentially contrast. To visualize useful detail in the image, contrast is important, and in fact, agents can be placed within the fluid of the tissue that increase or enhance the contrast between the tissue with the agent vs. the tissue without the agent. The signal excitation has to be repeated for several cycles to get the two-dimensional representation for the image. A certain minimum time must elapse so that the signal is always the same if measured at the same location. This time is the repetition time (TR).

Sequences

MRI is characterized by a number of different excitation sequences in which one or more RF pulses are followed by one or more intervals followed then by the echo time. The spin-echo (SE) is the most common sequence. It is a pulse that alters the vector by 90°. TR is the time between these pulse, and TE is the time from pulse to signal reading (echo). The spin-echo can produce T1-, T2-, and PD-weighted images.

Glossary

echo time (TE)
the time between transmission of a radiofrequency pulse signal to excite the sample/object and the reading of the signal
fringe field
the magnetic field that extends beyond the covering panels of a (clinical) scanner
gauss
unit of magnetic field strength; symbol is G; 10,000 gauss is 1 tesla. The Earth has a magnetic field strength of 0.5 G.
gradient-echo
a sequence in which a small radiofrequency pulse is produce a flip (re-orientation of the magnetization vector) by an angle α less than 90° (compare spin-echo) before reading the signal
gradients (gradient fields)
short-term spatial variations in magnetic field strength used to localize resonance signals for the purpose of producing images
homogeneity
descriptive of the quality of the uniformity of a magnetic field
repetition time (TR)
the time between re-initiation of a radiofrequency pulse to excite the sample when performing multiple scans for producing/acquiring images or spectrometric data
saturation
the effect of observing apparently relatively shorter relaxation times in targets with actually longer relaxation times as a result of pulsing before full relaxation has a chance to occur
SNR
signal-to-noise ratio
spin-echo
a sequence of pulsing a sample to excite it and thereby reorient the magnetization vector by 90° (compare gradient-echo), and then wait for a time before reading it (reading the echo)
spin-lattice relaxation
the return to equilibrium of the magnetization vector along the z axis. Also called longitudinal relaxation. T1 is the time required for spin-lattice relaxation
spin-spin relaxation
the return to equilibrium of the magnetization vector along the transverse plane. Also called transverse relaxation. T1 is the time required for spin-lattice relaxation
susceptibility
the small differences between magnetic properties of adjoining tissues or tissue-air boundaries
TE
echo time
tesla
unit of magnetic field strength (or rather flux density) equal to 10,000 gauss
TR
repetition time
transmitter coil
the coil that emits a radiofrequency signal to excite the protons prior to relaxation
receiver coil
the coil that detects the change in the magnetization vector
