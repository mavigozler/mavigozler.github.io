Development of Atomic Theory

Ancient Greeks wondered What is matter made of? Indirect evidence that it was composed of atoms but with invention of scanning tunneling microscopy, we can see atoms now.

Dalton in 1808 presented a theory of atoms based on his studies with combustion with carbon and oxygen. Elements were known, but the chemistry indicated that elements had to be composed of something smaller to explain what was observed. Atoms have same properties, and they form the chemical compounds, combining in different proportions. Dalton had been aware that water was composed of hydrogen and oxygen and in certain proportions. He came up with its formula being OH with H having mass 1 and O having mass 8. He developed a table of atomic masses (now atomic weights). Dalton's theory of atoms had the following four principles:

  1. All matter is made of indivisible atoms. Atoms are the smallest units of matter and cannot be created, divided, or destroyed (this was later revised with the discovery of subatomic particles).
  2. All atoms of a given element are identical. Atoms of the same element have the same mass and properties (later revised—atoms of the same element can have different masses, known as isotopes).
  3. Compounds are formed by a combination of atoms of different elements. A given compound always contains the same kinds and ratios of atoms.
  4. Chemical reactions involve the rearrangement of atoms. Atoms are neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction—only rearranged.

Joseph Gay-Lussac in 1809 reported on his experiments with volumes of gases reacting with each other. He reported two volumes of hydrogen react with one volume of oxygen to form two volumes of gaseous water, and one volume hydrogen react with one volume chlorine to form two volumes hydrogen chloride. In 1811 Avogadro proposed from these results at same temperature and pressure that equal volumes of different gases contain the same number of particles

Atoms, Isotopes, Molecules, and Ions

In latter 19th century, chemists and physicists had all kinds of evidence of the atom. Now we are getting into the nature of the atom, to examine any subatomic features and what they are. British physicist J.J. Thomson from 1898-1903 applied a high voltage to a tube with a partial vacuum and he observes from the cathode rays. This discharge shows repulsion by the negative pole of applied electric field and that there is a stream of negatively charged particles (later called electrons). Thomson measures these deflections and find a charge-to-mass ratio to be

\( \frac{e}{m} = -1.76 \times 10^8\:C/g \)

Thomson wanted to understand structure of atom and his experiments concluded that electrons were part of every atom. He also reasoned that since atoms are electrically neutral, if they have negatively charged particles (electrons), they must be include positively charged particles. Thomsom developed the plum pudding model of an atom: electrons as plums within a diffuse cloud pudding of positive charge.

Robert Millikan constructed a device to atomize oil and the spray was emitted into a container with two plates: a positively charged upper plate with hole allowing oil to fall through the hole. There was also a bottom negatively charged plate. X-rays were introduced into the stream of oil droplets which would cause them to be charged, affecting the gravitational fall of the droplets. Using Thomson's value, Millikan found that the mass of an electron could be computed to be \( 9.11 \times 10^{-31}\;\mathrm{kg} \). The mass of hydrogen was known and is about 2000 times larger.

Radioactivity

Another property of elements was that some where radioactive. Becquerel (1911) discovered uranium was emitting radiation, and the Curies found it was attributable to the atoms of uranium. Ernest Rutherford found three types of radiation:

  1. alpha particles: paths of movement changed in electric field, deflected away From positive charges
  2. beta particles: movement also deflected by electric field, but away from negative end.
  3. gamma radiation: unaffected by any electric field.

A Nucleus

Elements were composed of atoms, and atoms themselves had a subatomic structure. The electron was known and its mass was quite small. Thomoson proposes a cloud of large positive charge while electrons are a plum in the pudding. Rutherford's experiment with alpha particles at gold foil and observing deflections while some particles passed through indicated that atoms were composed of a vast space with positive dense charge in a nucleus. Rutherford discovered protons in 1919, and Chadwick reported neutrons in 1932.

Characteristics of Elements/Atoms

Elements get an ID given to them: the atomic number is the number of protons in the atom. Atoms in a unique element will always have same number of protons, but neutron count can vary. Because atoms can differ in neutrons, these isotopes will have an element symbolized with the atomic number subscripted to lower left of element symbol. The mass number, which the integral sum of the protons and neutrons, will be superscripted to the left above the atomic number. Weights of atoms themselves are measured in atomic mass units (amu) which is defined as 1/12th of the mass of the 12C isotope of carbon. \( 1 u = 1.660\:539\:066\:60 \times 10^{-27}\;\mathrm{kg} \). A proton has 1.0073 amu, neutron 1.0087, and electron \( 5.486\times 10^{-4}\;\mathrm{amu}\)

Periodic Table

Developed in 1869, it shows elements that show a pattern of characteristics. The 1st period has two elements, the 2nd and 3rd periods have eight, the 4th and 5th have 18, and the 6th and 7th have 32. Vertical columns are groups. Nomenclature has some significance. The A and B labeling is a North American convention. IUPAC has set the numbering from 1 to 18 of the groups.

Compounds

Only the noble gas elements exist elements but also as pure single atoms unbonded to atoms including their own type. All other atoms exist as molecules and ions. Oxygen is found as a diatomic molecule and even as a triatomic molecule as ozone. Many of the important elements on the right side of the periodic table exist in diatomic form. Molecular compounds designate substances composed of more than one atomic type. Methane is one such compound: CH4 is composed of nonmetallic elements and it should be noted that most of the molecular compounds that exist are composed only of non-metals.

  • Molecular formulas are chemical formulas that contain the actual numbers of atoms in a molecule. The molecular formula for hydrogen peroxide is H2O2.
  • Empirical formulas are chemical formulas that reflect the relative numbers of atoms in a molecule. The empirical formula for hydrogen peroxide is HO. It is always possible to know the empirical formula if the moecular formula is known, but the converse is not true.
  • A structural formula of a molecule or compound is a molecular formula that shows the bonding arrangement between the atoms
  • Other molecular representations include Perspective drawing, which convey information using wedges and dashed lines to show atoms out of plane in three dimensional arragement, ball-and-stick models, where atoms are represented as spheres/balls and bonds are sticks, and space-filling models which show atoms scaled up

Chemical Equations

Reactions involve bond breaking and making to produce new arrangements of atoms, with reactant molecules on one side and product molecules on another. These are written as equations and with all equations, everything must balance. This balance at requires a conservation of mass and charge. As to mass, the number of atoms of each element must be equal on the left and right sides.

Besides the mass conservation (numbers of each element), equations also include the physical state of a compound/molecule: solid (s), liquid (l), gas (g), and aqueous (aq). For example, pouring hydrochloric acid on solid sodium bicarbonate will produce carbon dioxide gas, and a salt solution.

HCl (aq) + NaHCO3 (s) → CO2 (g) + H2O (l) + NaCl (aq)

The combustion of methane includes coefficients to achieve balance:

CH4 (g) + 2 O2 (g) → CO2 (g) + 2 H2O (g)

Balancing Equations

We approach the combustion of ethanol knowing carbox dioxide and water:

C2H5OH (l) + O2 (g) → CO2 (g) + H2O (g)

Mass is not conserved on both sides of the chemical equation. The steps in balancing will start out by focusing on the most complex of molecules, ethanol. It has two carbons, so the first step is to make sure two carbons are on the other side. Only one molecule has carbon in the products and it requires putting a coefficient of 2 as a count of the molecules.

There are six hydrogen atoms in ethanol, and no other hydrogen atoms. On the right side, only water has hydrogen atoms, and to balance them for six, it is convenient to use a coefficient of 3.

The final balance is for the element oxygen. First count up the side with the immediately apparent most O atoms, which is on the products side. With 2 CO2 and 3 H2O, that is 2 × 2 + 3 × 1 = 7 O atoms. To achieve that balance, 3 O2 molecules would fit properly. So the final analysis is:

C2H5OH (l) + 3 O2 (g) → 2 CO2 (g) + 3 H2O (g)

Trying another: solid ammonium dichromate (NH4)2Cr2O7 is a bright orange and can be ignited. The products end up being chromium(III) oxide, nitrogen gas, and water vapor.

(NH4)2Cr2O7 (s) → Cr2O3 (s) + N2 (g) + H2O (g)

Immediately it appears that N and Cr are balanced on both sides of the equation. Only H and O elements are not balanced. Looking at H, there are 8 H atoms on the left side, so we check how to make 8 H atoms on the right side. A coefficient of 4 for water will do that, so the H atoms are balanced. Now looking at the O atoms, the calculation is that there are 7 O atoms on the right, and that conveniently balances out by putting 4 water molecules.

(NH4)2Cr2O7 (s) → Cr2O3 (s) + N2 (g) + 4 H2O (g)

Concept of Limiting Reactions

Suppose a sandwich shop makes a popular item as follows:

2 slices bread + 3 slices meat + 1 slice cheese → sandwich

At the start of the morning, the following ingredients are found: 8 slices bread, 9 slices meat, 5 slices of cheese. How many sandwiches can be made and what will be left over?

\( \text{Bread: 8 slices bread} \times \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{1 sandwich}}{\displaystyle \text{2 slices bread}}\right) = \text{4 sandwiches} \) \( \text{Meat: 9 slices meat} \times \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{1 sandwich}}{\displaystyle \text{3 slices meat}}\right) = \text{3 sandwiches} \) \( \text{Cheese: 5 slices cheese} \times \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{1 sandwich}}{\displaystyle \text{1 slice cheese}}\right) = \text{5 sandwiches} \)

From the calculations above, the most sandwiches that can be made are 3, as the meat is the limiting ingredient. When the 3 sandwiches are made, we will have 2 slices of bread and 2 slices of cheese remaining.

The same principle applies in chemistry. Suppose we will mix 25.0 kg nitrogen and 5.00 kg hydrogen gases to make ammonia. The balance reaction is:

N2 (g) + 3 H2 (g) → 2 NH3 (g)

For the reaction amounts we have, calculate the moles of product made from the moles of reactant available:

\( \text{Hydrogen: 5.00 kg H}_2 \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{1000 g H}_2}{\displaystyle \text{1 kg H}_2}\right) \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{1 mol H}_2}{\displaystyle \text{2.016 g H}_2}\right) \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{2 mol NH}_3}{\displaystyle \text{3 mol H}_2}\right) \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{17.0 g NH}_3}{\displaystyle \text{1 mol NH}_3}\right) \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{1 kg NH}_3}{\displaystyle \text{1000 g NH}_3}\right) = \text{28.1 kg NH}_3 \) \( \text{Nitrogen: 25.0 kg N}_2 \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{1000 g N}_2}{\displaystyle \text{1 kg N}_2}\right) \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{1 mol N}_2}{\displaystyle \text{28.0 g N}_2}\right) \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{2 mol NH}_3}{\displaystyle \text{1 mol N}_2}\right) \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{17.0 g NH}_3}{\displaystyle \text{1 mol NH}_3}\right) \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{1 kg NH}_3}{\displaystyle \text{1000 g NH}_3}\right) = \text{30.4 kg NH}_3 \)

From this calculation, we can see that H2 is limiting and the most ammonia that can be made is 28.1 kg.

This calculation involved determine the total product that could be formed from each of the reactants. Another way of calculating would be to determine from the moles of one reactant the exact moles of the other reactant that would be required of the other reactant and compare that the moles available.

Pressure

Gases exert pressure. Gases form the atmosphere. The barometer is a device that measures atmospheric pressure. The Toricelli barometer works by filling a glass tube with mercury and inverting it in a dish of mercury. When this happens at sea level, a column of mercury 760 mm in height stays in the tube. Pressure varies with altitude: at 9600 feet in the mountains of Colorado. Manometers are instruments

that measure pressure, typically use mercury, units are mm Hg, also called torr in honor of Toricelli. Also 1 atmosphere has the standard of 760 mm Hg. In SI units, pressure is force per unit area, which is N/m2. This is called the pascal. 1 atmosphere is 101,325 Pa. (In imperial measures, 1 atmosphere is 14.6959 psi)

Charles' Law

French physicist Jacques Charles in 1787 found that the volume of a gas at constant pressure increases linearly with the temperature of the gas (he had been experiment with hydrogen gas in balloons). A plot of V vs T in °C for gases He, CH4, H2O, H2 and N2O with different moles of gas will have different slopes of straight lines, but all these points converge at the value −273 °C. When the plot is rescaled on the abscissa with the temperature scale in Kelvins, the convergence is on 0, otherwise known as absolute zero.

This applies for temperatures on the Kelvin scale only, so the solution sets the correct values for temperature:

\( \text{2.58 L} \left(\frac{\displaystyle \text{(38 + 273) K}}{\displaystyle \text{(15 + 273) K}}\right) = 2.79 L \)

Avogadro's Law

In 1811, Avogadro made the following observation: equal volumes of gases at the same pressure and temperature appear to have the same number of particles. This became Avogadro's Law and can be expressed mathematically as

\( V = an \)

where V is gas volume, n is number of moles, and a is a propotionality constant.

Ideal Gas Law

The analysis of Boyle's law, Charles' law, and Avogadro's law brings us to understand that the volume of a gas is directly proportional to temperature and the number of particles and inversely proportional to pressure, and that this proportionality includes a constant:

\( V = R \left(\frac{\displaystyle Tn}{\displaystyle V}\right) \)

algrebraically rearranged:

\( PV = nRT \)

This is the ideal gas law and is an equation of state for a gas. The state of a gas is described by its pressure, volume, temperature, and mass (in moles). Generally the ideal gas law works when the pressure is below 1 atm and the temperatures are high. The value for R when the pressure is measured in atmospheres and volume is in liters is 0.08206 L atm/(K mol)

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

In 1803 Dalton noted: In a mixture of gases, the total pressure is the sum of pressures of each gas exerted if the gas were alone. This is Dalton's law of partial pressures and is expressed as follows:

\( P_{TOTAL} = P_1 + P_2 + P_3 \text{+ ...} \)

\( \qquad = \frac{\displaystyle n_1RT}{\displaystyle V} + \frac{\displaystyle n_2RT}{\displaystyle V} + \frac{\displaystyle n_3RT}{\displaystyle V} \text{+ ...} \)

\( \qquad = \displaystyle(n_1 + n_2 + n_3 \text{ + ... } ) \frac{\displaystyle RT}{V} \)

\( \qquad = n_{TOTAL} (\frac{\displaystyle RT}{V}) \)

Kinetic Molecular Theory of Gases

With experimental observations about gases, it is now time to describe a model to explain the observations. Going back to scientific method, a law is a way of generalizing observations from many experiments with reproducible results. Laws permit the prediction of results in similar systems. Laws summarize observed results, but do not inform us why these observations occur. Thus theories, or built models, will provide this. Models are successful if they both explain and predict behavior. The kinetic molecular theory helps to explain how ideal gases work using a molecular scale.

Postulates of kinetic molecular theory are the following:

  1. Particles will be so small compared to distances between them that the volume of individual particles is considered neglible.
  2. Particles are in constant motion: collisions of particles with walls of container are cause of pressure exerted by gas
  3. Particles should exert no forces on each other in this gas, neither attracting or repelling each other.
  4. The average of the kinetic energy of all gas particles will be directly proportional to Kelvin temperature.

To produce the kinetic molecular model, consider a cube of side L and gas particle of mass m with velocity \( \vec{u} \) and the magnitude of velocity will have three dimensional components: \( u^2 = u_x^2 + u_y^2 + u_z^2 \). The collision frequency, or collisions per unit time, with two walls that are perpendicular to x axis is given by:

\( (\text{collision frequency})_s = \frac{\displaystyle \text{velocity in the } x \text{ direction}} {\displaystyle \text{distance between the walls}} \)

\( \qquad = \displaystyle \frac{u_x}{L} \)

The force definition is applied: \( F = ma = m \displaystyle (\frac{\Delta u}{\Delta t}) = \displaystyle \frac{\Delta (mu)}{\Delta t} \)

That last quantity notes that since the mass of a particle does not change, it can be shown as a change in momentum over time:

\( \text{change in momentum} = \Delta (mu_x) = \text{final momentum} - \text{initial momentum} = -mu_x - mu_x = -2mu_x \)

It can be shown that \( \text{Force}_x = (2mu_x)(\displaystyle \frac{u_x}{L}) = \frac{2mu_x^2}{L} \)

The total forces in all three dimensions ca be shown to be \( \text{Force}_{\text{TOTAL}} = \displaystyle \frac{2mu_x^2}{L} + \frac{2mu_y^2}{L} + \frac{2mu_z^2}{L} = \frac{2m}{L} (u_x^2 + u_y^2 + u_z^2) = \frac{2m}{L}(u^2) \)

The result shows the velocity is the sum of dimensional components. The pressure caused by the average particle can be show to be:

\( \displaystyle \frac{\overline{\text{force}_{\text{TOTAL}}}}{\text{area}_{\text{TOTAL}}} = \frac{\displaystyle \frac{2m\overline{u^2}}{L}}{6L^2} = \frac{m\overline{u^2}}{3L^3} \)

The volume of the gas V is the cube volume, L3, and the number of gas particles is nNA where n is the number of moles and NA is Avogadro's number.

\( \displaystyle P = nN_\text{A}\frac{m\overline{u^2}}{3V} \)

Considering that \( m\overline{u^2} = 2(\frac{1}{2})m\overline{u^2}\), a new expression for pressure using average kinetic energy is:

\( \displaystyle P = (\frac{2}{3})\frac{nN_\text{A}(\frac{1}{2}m\overline{u^2})}{V} \)

\( \displaystyle \frac{PV}{n} = (\frac{2}{3})N_\text{A}(\frac{1}{2}m\overline{u^2}) \)

Taking the last postulate, it can be shown that the average kinetic energy of a mole of gas particles is:

\( (\text{KE})_{\text{avg}} = N_A(\frac{1}{2}m\overline{u^2}) \)

\( \text{so } \displaystyle \frac{PV}{n} = RT = \frac{2}{3}(\text{KE})_{\text{avg}} \)

\( \displaystyle (\text{KE})_{\text{avg}} = \frac{3}{2}RT \)

The quantity \( \overline{u^2} \) represents the average of the square of velocities. The square root of that value is the root mean square velocity (\( u_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\overline{u^2}} \))

From the above for \( \displaystyle (\text{KE})_{\text{avg}} \):

\( \displaystyle N_A(\frac{1}{2}m\overline{u^2}) = \frac{3}{2}RT \)

Scientific Method

How does one address questions about nature?

  1. Make observations, collect data
  2. Suggest possible explantions: formulate a hypothesis
  3. Experiment: test possible explanations, test the hypothesis

From these steps, a theory or model is then proposed, which is a set of tested hypotheses. Observations are something that can be recorded and witnessed, while a theory is an interpretation of those observations. Theories are educated guesses, but they can be modified or revised when these theories do not account for something with new data or facts

A History of Chemistry

  • Georg Bauer: extraction of metals from ores (systematic metallurgy)
  • Paracelus: medicinal application of minerals
  • Robert Boyle (1660s): shows relationship between pressure and volume
  • Antoine Lavoisier studies combustion, notes oxygen (not phlogiston), notes conservation of mass in careful measurements
  • Joseph Proust shows a law of definite proportion in elements being present in exactly same proportion
  • John Dalton notes that carbon and oxygen can be present in two compounds in different proportions, indicating that elements are composed of something smaller, namely atoms (this is the law of multiple proportions)
