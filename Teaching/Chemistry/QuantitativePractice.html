Quantitative Practice

Making Calculations

Note that this document was originally composed for scientists in proteomics although its principles apply to anyone in a laboratory doing chemistry, biochemistry, and advanced chemistry (of all kinds). There may be references to the word proteomics, but the word can be dropped and the meaning should generally apply. A revision may come later.

The greatest struggle of inexperienced—and even some experienced— scientists is calculating reagent concentrations, volumes, and masses.

This section will provide guidelines for calculating everything related to solutions and reagents, as well as tips for making solutions and reagents.

Precision versus Accuracy

The use of a measuring instrument or device, such as a weighing balance that measures masses or a pipettor that measures liquid volumes, involves a discussion of whether the instrument is precise or accurate, or both. Generally the most desirable characteristic of the device is that it is both precise and accurate.

Suppose two pipettors are set at 1.00 ml and are used to measure out water on to a weighing balance. Pipetting is performed 10 times for each both pipettors, and the mass of the water in grams is recorded in the table below.

Computation of the average shows that both pipettors do not exactly deliver 1.000 ml, but they are nonetheless really close. This is to be expected with with pipettors typically used in the laboratory.

The more accurate pipettor will be that one close to the true value, which is 1.000 ml. The difference between the true value and the average is shown as difference in the table, and pipettor #2 closer to pipettor #1 to the true value. Thus pipettor #2 is the more accurate.

The more precise pipettor will be the one which produces the more repeatable values. This can be measured in the pipettor showing the lower coefficient of variation (CV). The value of the CV is the quotient of the standard deviation and the average. The standard deviation is computed as the square root of the sum of the square of the differences between each value in the dataset and the average of the dataset divided by the degrees of freedom (which the number of values less 1). Pipettor #1 shows a lower CV than does pipettor #2, so it is the more precise, despite being the less accurate.

With very good production quality and calibration, an instrument can be both precise and accurate.

Significant Digits

Also called significant figures, the significant digits of a value are at least the number of digits that extend from the first nonzero number to the last nonzero number, with all the zero digits that are in between as well.

Zero digits to the left of the first nonzero digit are not significant, whether they come after the decimal point or not.

Zero digits to the right of the last nonzero digits are significant if they come after the decimal point, or when there is a decimal point indicated in an integer number.

Most do not consider the zeroes to the right of the last nonzero digit of an integer as significant (see the use of scientific notation in helping decide what digits are significant).

Numbers in scientific notation have two components: the mantissa, which has a number expressed as a 100 value and perhaps a fractional portion (decimal point with numbers following it), and the exponent, which is the 10n value.

The mantissa part requires that the number be written exactly according to the significant digits of the number. Notice has the mantissa is written for the numbers that are all equal to 200,000: the zeroes that become signficant must be expressed to their accuracy in the mantissa. Thus converting a number to scientific notation forces one to think about just what the significant digits are.

Rounding Numbers

Before getting into this important subject of showing results of operations, a reminder on rounding off numbers.

Rule: when the digit used to determine rounding off is between 0 and 4, one rounds down; when it is between 5 and 9, one rounds up. Rounding down is equivalent to just truncating (lopping off) the digts from the rounding digit to the right. Rounding up is the equivalent of truncating the numbers as well, but adding 1 to the digit to the left of the rounding digit.

Suppose the number 8.49918365107 is to be rounded off to a certain digit.

Suppose the number is to have the format x.xxxxxxx, that is 7 digits after the decimal point. This means that the 8th digit after the decimal point, the number "5," is to be used in rounding off. Since "5" indicates that rounding up occurs on the 7th digit, which is "6," then the value becomes 8.4991837 when rounding up and truncation occur.

Now suppose that number will have format x.xx, or 2 digits after the decimal point. This means that the 3rd digit after decimal point, or "9," will lead to a rounding up of the number to be formatted: 8.50. This was a tricky round up in fact, because 1 was added to the 9 in the 2nd digit after the decimal point, which then carries a one to the 9 in the 1st digit after the decimal point, and so there were multiple changes of the digits as a result of these digits valued at "9."

Significant Digits of the Results of Operations On Numbers

The first principle involving determining the number of significant digits of the result of a binary operation is that the result will have the same number of significant digits as the number involved in the operation that has the least significant digits.

Here's an example. Evaluate and give the result with the correct number of significant digits the operation: 9.7×12.35=?

If one merely enters the values into the calculator and writes down all the digits reported by the calculator, the result is: 9.7×12.35=119.795

However, as a scientific result, this is not correct. For example, if the number 9.7 were a volume in milliliters (ml), and 12.35 were a concentration in micromole per milliter (which is millimolar, or mM), then the result is a number of micromoles, which is 119.795 micromoles (µmol). No scienist believes that the mass is that accurate. Since the number 9.7 has 2 significant digits, and 12.35 has 4 significant digits, the result will have signficant digits that are the least of the significant digits of one of the two numbers, namely 2 digits. Thus the result is rounded to 120 µmole, which is the answer: 9.7 ×12.35=120

The same is true if we had divided numbers, for example: 35.155250=0.14

That result was rounded off to 2 significant digits because the denominator 250 only had 2 significant digits by the rules, even though the numerator had 5 significant digits.

Setting Up A Calculation: The Numerator-Denominator Principle

A calculation is really an equation, and an equation must have an equal sign in standard mathematics.

On one side of the equal sign is the answer, the result of the calculation (usually on the right side of the equation for people who think left-to-right).

On the other side of the equation are the factors: all the numeric values that will be used to make the calculation. By definition, factors are multiplied to one another, and that is the case for pretty much 99.9% of all calculations in this setup. In rare cases, you will use algebra where you set up numbers that are added or subtracted from one another, but they become quantities (values enclosed in parentheses) either in the numerator or denominator of a factor.

All the factors will be represented in a numerator or denominator fashion. That is, if a factor is used in a division operation, then it will appear as its inverted value (that is, if value x is used to divide the factors in the calculation, it will be represented as 1 / x and multiplied to all factors).

The equation will then have the form that looks much like this: w dim1 dim2×x dim2 dim3 × dim3y dim4× z dim4 1=a dim1 [1]

The dimn, where n = 1, 2, 3, …, actually refers to the name of a dimension or unit, such mg (milligram) or µl (microliter). The values w, x, y, z are numbers expressed in any form (real or floating point or decimal; integers; scientific notation); they are the factors in this product. The product itself is the value a. Note that the value of y is in the denominator, that is the factor is 1/y.

But what is important here is not the values w, x, etc. It is the dimensions dimn. The values were merely arranged in this style in order to arrange the dimension to produce the desired final dimension with its value. Note how dim2, dim3, and dim4 all cross out, leaving dim1 in the final answer. One can remove the dimensions and then just be left with multiplying all numbers in the numerator, then multiplying all in the denominator, and finally dividing the product in the numerator by the product in the denominator to get the final answer.

In constructing the equation (calculation):

  • It is essential to know what one really wants as the answer, which is a value and possibly a dimension. For instance, if a volume is asked for, then the dimension must be in units that represent a volume (µl, ml, L). If the target answer is a mass, then it should be in some form of grams (mg, g) or possibly moles. For concentrations, this is really solution density, or mass units per volume units.
  • Write down all the factors available, even if they will not be used in the final calculation.
  • The missing factors in a calculation are typically conversion units. That is, the conversion of one expression of the volume into another. Thus there are 1000 µliters (µl) in 1 milliliter (ml), and 1000 milliliters in 1 liter (L). Know your conversion units by reflex. Note that these are not constants: a constant is value reflecting a fundamental physical or chemical property in nature or the universe. Planck’s constant is 6.62 × 10−34 J s, and not a conversion of one value into another form.
  • Constants. A typical constant in solution density determinations is the molecular or atomic weight of a substance, which is the amount of grams in one mole of the substance. This is typical missing factor whenever one factor is expressed in grams and the answer sought in some form of moles.

In setting up the final equation which is the product of these factors, the solution is arrived at by doing what is called a dimensional analysis, which was the process described above.

Lots of Examples

These are some real-life examples of where you will encounter these calculations in proteomics.

1. Make up just enough, but not really more than necessary, of a DTT and iodoacetamide reagent for adding to a complex protein mixture prior to digestion with trypsin. The following facts are available:
  1. The protein mixture in the 500 µl tube is in a 25 µl volume.
  2. You will need to make the mixture slightly alkaline, which promotes the reaction of DTT, iodoacetamide, and trypsin; we use ammonium bicarbonate (NH4HCO3) at a final 50 mM for this purpose. The NH4HCO3 has the added benefit of being volatile under a vacuum, which means it can be removed by a centfriugal evaporator.
  3. DTT will be added to a final volume of 10 mM to the alkalinized protein solution, and then left for 20-30 minutes; the reaction is promoted at a warmer temperature (say 30-37°).
  4. Iodoacetamide is added to 50-55 mM (calculate for 50 mM). It is added at 5 × the amount of DTT because some of it will react with DTT.
  5. Finally trypsin will be added. It comes lyophilized in a 20 µg amount. The manufacturer provides a resuspension buffer, which is 50 mM acetic acid, and recommends that 200 µl be added to resuspend the material. Our trypsin treatment will be at 10 ng/µl, based on the literature.
The first thing to add to the protein mixture will be the NH4HCO3, and must be at a final 50 mM. There are two ways to calculate.
  • Quick and dirty. Generally, if a stock reagent is prepared that is 10 to 20 times greater than the final concentration, it is as simple as dividing the volume of the solution to which it will be added by the fold-difference in the concentration between the reagent concentration and the final concentration. Suppose the DTT reagent stock is 500 mM (0.5 M). With a 25 µl protein solution, the amount of reagent to add is thus: 25 μl× 50 mM 500 mM=2.5μ l [2]

    Some things to note about the equation above.

    1. Equation [2] is a classic demonstration of the conservation of mass. How? The concentration of a solute in a solution multiplied to the volume of the solution gives the mass of the solute in the solution. Thus concentration × volume = mass. And using algebra, mass / volume = concentration (because mass divided by volume is also an expression of density, this equation is also called solution density).
    2. Note that equation [2] uses the format of doing calculations in formula [1] above, arranging the dimensions so that they always divide out and yielding a dimension that we want in the end (a volume).

    So you add 2.5 µl of the 500 mM NH4HCO3 to the 25 µl of the protein solution to get 50 mM NH4 as a final concentration.

    Actually, that's not entirely true. Because 2.5 µl of the reagent is added, it contributes to the final volume as well, and so the final volume must be taken into account. To get the true final concentration, one must divide the actual mass of DTT added by the final volume: 2.5μl× 500 mM (25+2.5 )μl =45.4 mM [3]

    Is this acceptable? Many scientists can live with the difference between 45+ mM and 50 mM, about a 10% difference from the desired. Suppose a 1000 mM (1 M) NH4HCO3 reagent stock was prepared and used in instead; this is a 20× concentration. 25 μl× 50 mM 1000 m M=1.25μ l [4] 125μl× 1000 mM (25+1.25 )μ1 =47.6 mM [5]

    Of course the difference will be less with use of higher stock concentrations as expected. The problem is in using stock reagent volumes too small to pipet accurately. I like to use no less than 2 µl although it is possible to pipet 0.5 µl (500 nl) accurately with some sophisticated measuring devices. Be mindful of how small a volume you can pipet confidently.

  • The Truly Accurate. In order to get that exact 50 mM ammonium bicarbonate concentration, one must use algebra to compensate for the added volume. All these equations are conservation of mass equations, and this one is no different. An unknown value x represents the volume of the reagent to be added, and the equation must reflect the fact that a final 50 mM concentration is desired. (25μl× 0 mM )+(x μ1×500 mM) =(25+x )μl×50 mM [6]

    Note that one has mixed operations here. Addition and subtraction of numbers can only occur when the dimensions are the same, and dimensions are serialized with multiplication and can be deleted with division.

    The equation is distributed: (25×0)μ l mM+(500x )μl mM= (25×50)μ l mM+(50x )μl mM   [7]

    All the units are the same now, so all operations can be done with the numbers. So looking at just the numbers, we re-arrange the equation to get x on one side: 50 0x-50x=25 ×50  [8]   x (500-50)= 1250  [9]   x =1250/(500-50 )=2.78   [10]

    Note that the 25 × 0 drops out of equation [7]. In equation [9] we distribute out the concentration as a quantity out of the unknown volume x. Both sides of the equation are divided by the quantity (equation [10]), and the result is the volume of 2.78 µl.

    We verify this calculation using the equation [3] (also [5]) form: 2.78μl× 500 mM (25+2.78 )μl =50.0 mM   [11]

Thus if you have a very accurate pipet and the time to do the algebra, you can get the very accurate concentration.

The next step is to add the DTT. From here on, it will be assumed that the Quick & Dirty method was used to calculate volumes to add, and that the stock reagents are all made up as 10×.

Assuming that 2.5 µl of NH4HCO3 was added, then the volume is 27.5 µl. If we add about 3 µl of 10× DTT (100 mM), then one will have very close to 10 mM. And, yes, the ammonium bicarbonate will be diluted out again, and be less than the 45 mM already calculated, but that's okay. An alternative might be to prepare DTT in ammonium bicarbonate, but that is probably not a good idea since it tends to chemically activate (make reactive) the DTT.

One thing left out is the preparation of those NH4HCO3 and DTT stock reagents. In proteomics, it is generally best to prepare many reagents fresh from the solid/powder. Ammonium bicarbonate is volatile, and so any stock is probably best frozen or again, just made fresh. DTT stock can be frozen, but after a year (and some think 6 months), it is probably better to make up new reagent.

In our real life example, we had several tubes and added up all the volumes for the NH4HCO3 we would need, and came up with 200 µl. At 500 mM, we want to calculate how many milligrams to weigh out: 200μl 1×500 mmol L×...= x mg   [12]

Equation [12] is how anyone would first set up such a calculation, and follows a numerator-denominator approach so that it is easy to cancel out dimensions, and be left with the final dimension. Note that the final dimension is mg, and so we know that a mg value must occur in the numerator at some point. We also see that µl stands alone in a numerator value and will have to be canceled by another µl in the denominator. Likewise for the mmol and the L value. Following the principles above, we know that the missing factors in the equation are dimension conversion values and also a constant, which is the molecular/formula weight of NH4HCO3. The rest of the factors (symbolized by the . . . in the equation) are then added in order to do these cancellations of dimensions and end up with the right value: 200μl 1×500 mmol L× L106 μl× 79.06 mg mmol= x mg   [13]

We have added two factors. A conversion factor for the volume, and the molecular weight for ammonium bicarbonate. Notice that the dimensions cancel each other out in a numerator-denominator like fashion, and we are left with mg.

Someone might be confused by looking at the molecular weight, and say, isn’t the molecular weight actually 79.06 grams per mole (79.06 g/mol)? Well, yes it is. And it is also 79.06 mg/mmol, and 79.06 µg/µmol, 79.06 ng/nmol, and so on. Notice that these conversions are made instantly in the numerator and denominator, in each case dividing by 1000 in the numerator and denominator to get the dimensions shown. These sorts of conversions can be made in one's head.

But supposed that this conversion stumps the neophyte. In that case, one adds the factor as follows: 200μl 1×500 mmol L× L106 μl× 79.06 g mol× ...=x mg  [14]

It is necessary to add the . . . because clearly there are missing factors. These are now added: 200μl 1×500 mmol L× L106 μl× 79.06 g mol× 1 mol 1000 mmolx1000 mg g =x mg  [15]

Now all the dimensions cancel out to give the right value, and all we have by adding the conversion factors is divide by 1000 in the numerator and denominator (the 1000 values will cancel themselves out). The final value in mg is easily calculated: 2001×500 1×1106 ×79.061 =7.9 mg   [16]

So to make 200 µl of 500 mM NH4HCO3, 7.9 mg needs to be weighed out.

When the chemical was actually weighed out, more than 7.9 mg was weighed. Actually 10.4 mg was weighed out into the 500 µl conical tube in which it was to be prepared. Rather than spend too much needless time trying to weigh out exactly 7.9 mg, it was decided to use the 10.4 mg. Why? First we had 1 kg of the material (that is 1 million mg!) and the cost of this is just tens of dollars (maybe just 10 dollars). The time spent trying to weigh out exactly 7.9 mg could buy another kilogram of NH4HCO3. So instead we re-calculate the amount of water to add to dissolve the salt to the correct concentration, because it is much easier to pipet microliters of water than milligrams of salt: 10.4 mg1× mmol79.06 mg ×L500 mmol ×106μ lL =263μl   [17]

Notice that in going back in the other direction in calculating microliters from the milligrams, we invert the factors that were involved in calculating milligrams from microliters. We wanted 200 µl and calculated 7.9 mg, but had 10.4 mg and calculated it should be in a total of 263 µl. Note that we since we calculated 7.9 mg needs 200 µl for 500 mM, then 10.4 mg would need 200 × 10.4 / 7.9 = 263, an obvious way of calculating proportions.

After the addition of NH4HCO3, DTT is added. DTT is rather expensive, so a 10× stock is made up in a small volume and whatever is ununsed is frozen away at −20°. Since 10× is 100 mM, we want to make 3-5 ml in a 15 ml conical tube. The molecular weight of DTT is 154.3. So we calculate the range from a minimum of 3 to a maximum of 5 ml: 3 ml1× 100 mmol L× 154.3 mg mmol× L103 ml =46.3 mg   [18] 5 ml1× 100 mmol L× 154.3 mg mmol× L103 ml =77.2 mg   [19]

The first three factors are the essential data we need (the 3rd factor is actually a constant, the molecular weight), while the last (4th) factor provides a dimension conversion. Again, when all the dimensions cancel out, then you can do the math on the numbers alone.

At the balance, 51.7 mg of DTT powder was added to a tared 15 ml conical tube. So again, it is now necessary to calculate the water to add to dissolve the DTT: 51.7 mg1× mmol154.3 mg ×L100 mmol× 103 mlL =3.35 ml   [20]

So with a P-1000 pipet set at 1,000 µl, I pipet water three times, then set it to 350 µl and pipet it once. When ready, I add 27.5 / 10 = ~2.8 µl of the DTT stock to the alkalinized protein solution.

After 20-30 minutes, I figure that I have to add (27.5 + 2.8) / 10 = ~30 µl / 10 = 3 µl of a 10× iodoacetamide solution. Since the final concentration of iodoacetamide in 50-55 mM, the concentration should be 500-550 mM.

If it is calculated that one needs 150 µl of 500 mM iodoacetamide, then add enough (usually 15%) for pipetting error, which means one needs about 170 µl. The molecular weight of iodoacetamide is 184.96. 170μl 1×184.96 mg mmol×500 mmol L× L106 μl=17.3 mg   [21]

So we try to weigh out as close to that number as possible, since iodoacetamide will not keep in solution and we don't want to throw out too much in the end.
